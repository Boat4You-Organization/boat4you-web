Catamaran Charter from Yachtclub Seget Marina Baotić in Trogir

Yachtclub Seget Marina Baotić sits at the eastern edge of the Bay of Kaštela, just west of Trogir's old stone town, and it's the largest and most logistically solid charter base in the region. With nearly 700 berths, full-service shipyard facilities, fuel dock, restaurant, and round-the-clock reception, this family-run marina (operating since 1995) doesn't just provide a mooring point — it's a complete nautical hub. For catamaran charterers, that infrastructure matters. Your provisioning, fuel, crew support, and mechanical backup are already on site.

The catamarans departing here are built for exactly these waters: shallow-draft hulls ideal for the rocky archipelago between Šolta and Hvar, twin engines that let you hold position in tight anchorages, and generous cabin space that converts breezy days into comfortable floating accommodation. The Maestral wind patterns here typically run 8-15 knots during peak season, giving catamarans steady sailing without requiring hours of tacking.

Marina Facilities and What You Get

Marina Baotić offers everything a multi-day charter requires without forcing you to search another port. The reception desk staffs 24/7 — practical if your guests arrive late or a question arises at odd hours. Fuel dock with 24-hour access means you can top tanks whenever convenient, not when the fuel barge schedules permit. The on-site restaurant (Konoba-style, open to charterers) serves Dalmatian peka and fresh seafood, convenient when your crew doesn't want to cook or engine trouble delays your departure.

The shipyard handles emergency repairs on catamaran hulls, engines, and systems that would otherwise require a full-day detour to Split. Navigation equipment, electrics, plumbing — Baotić's team has patched hundreds of charter cats over three decades. The marina's protected basin in the Bay of Kaštela means even when the Bura wind pushes hard against the mainland, your moored catamaran sits in genuinely sheltered water.

Direct walking access connects the marina to Seget Donji village (roughly 800 meters), where family-run konobas serve Dalmatian pašticada and brodet. Konzum supermarket stocks provisioning staples; the farmers market in Trogir town (15-minute drive) carries seasonal produce, Plavac Mali wine, and local cheese. You can walk on the marina grounds while waiting for guests or browsing the small gift shop.

Why Catamaran from This Marina Specifically

Catamarans require reasonable draft and protected anchorages — both of which dominate the waters from Baotić. The Bay of Kaštela's northern shore means your catamaran typically experiences 1.2–1.5 meter low water, but once beyond the bay mouth, the deep-water routes to Šolta (five nautical miles away) open into standard 3–4 meter channels. The twin-hulled design gives you shallow-draft positioning without sacrificing cabin headroom or galley space.

The Šolta-Brač-Hvar progression is the classic catamaran circuit from Trogir. Šolta's Maslinica harbour (five nautical miles, roughly 30 minutes under power) shows you the archipelago's first character: olive oil culture, quiet family inns, minimal crowds in June and September. From Maslinica, Brač's Bol beach lies 15 nautical miles northeast—a longer passage where the catamaran's stability in slightly rougher conditions becomes clear. Bol's harbor can swell with tour boats in July, but arriving early or staying a day longer captures the beach before sunset crowds.

Hvar Town (25 nautical miles from Baotić) is inevitable—and worth the sail. Catamarans moor in the main harbor alongside fishing boats and local ferries, immersing you in authentic Mediterranean harbor life rather than isolating you in a tourist marina. The narrow streets rise steeply from the waterfront; the 13th-century cathedral square sits quiet in early morning before the guided-tour crowds arrive. Morning coffee on your catamaran's deck, watching fishermen prep nets, defines the pace of this sailing circuit.

Routes from Yachtclub Seget Marina Baotić

The Protected Archipelago Run (4–5 days)

Depart Baotić for Šolta Maslinica (30 min), anchor two nights exploring olive groves and family seafood restaurants. Sail to Brač Bol (4 hours from Maslinica), afternoon swimming in the crystal-sand cove, dinner at harborside konobas. Return via Supetar (Brač's quiet northern harbor) before heading back to Baotić. Totals roughly 60 nautical miles and involves only shallow-water anchoring — ideal for first-time sailors or families with young children.

The Hvar-Focused Circuit (6–7 days)

Maslinica, then 14 hours across to Hvar Town (break at Drvenik Veli's Blue Lagoon, an hour northeast of Baotić, if wind pushes). Two nights exploring Hvar's harbor and old town. Detour to Pakleni Islands (small anchorages, few other boats by September). Return via Brač and Šolta. Roughly 110 nautical miles total; moderate weather needed for the Hvar crossing.

Extended Vis Expedition (7+ days)

For catamaran teams with navigation experience, Vis (40 nautical miles from Baotić) offers isolation that fewer charterers reach. Vis Harbour and Komiža (fishing village on the far side) have minimal tourism. Return via Brač or Hvar depending on wind and time. Requires attention to weather windows; the crossing can get lumpy if the Maestral turns gusty.

Technical Advantages of Catamarans Here

The catamaran's dual hulls spread load across two keels, allowing 1.0–1.2 meter draft instead of the 1.8–2.2 meters a monohull requires. In the Adriatic's limestone archipelago, where uncharted rocks and shallow plateaus surround most anchorages, that shallower draft is safety margin. You can approach the best anchorages — Pakleni Islands, Milna on Brač, Maslinica's eastern mooring field — that deeper-hulled boats cannot.

Twin cabins (one per hull) let crew separate. Children sleep in one hull's forward cabin while parents occupy the other, creating privacy no monohull galley-bed offers. The saloon spans both hulls with interior width (sometimes 5+ meters) that feels like a small apartment rather than a corridor. Bad-weather sailing? The catamaran's reduced healing angle means less motion sickness and less drama.

Fuel efficiency matters over a week. Catamarans typically burn 10-15 liters per hour at cruising speed; monohulls of similar passenger capacity use 20-25. That translates to roughly 400-600 euros saved on fuel per week, plus fewer fuel-stop delays. The engines' accessibility (housed in both hulls, easily serviced) means Marina Baotić's technicians can diagnose and repair issues quickly, without removing cabin trim.

Pricing and Seasonal Variations

Catamaran charter from Baotić runs roughly 500–900 euros per day depending on boat size (5–8 berths) and season. June and September (shoulder season) sit toward the lower end; July-August peak pricing reaches the upper range. Many operators offer early-booking discounts (book 12+ weeks ahead for 10–15% reduction). Fuel is separate at current rates (typically included in the weekly rate or charged per liter consumed).

Extras to budget: provisioning (budget 100–150 euros per day for four people at Croatian market prices), paid moorings in Hvar and Vis harbors (10–15 euros per night), and small-boat ferry transfers if you want time on Pakleni Islands or Drvenik Veli. Travel insurance covering cancellation and damage is advisable; charter operators require damage waiver coverage (typically 1000–1500 euros hold, refunded if the boat returns unmarked).

Local provisioning at Baotić's harbor shop or the Trogir farmers market costs roughly 60% less than European supermarkets, and the quality (Dalmatian cheeses, Pošip wine, fresh fish from the dock) elevates daily meals. Plan two market trips during a week-long charter.

Best Season for Catamaran Charter Here

June and September deliver the ideal balance: Maestral winds run steady 10-12 knots without the gustiness of July's heat-driven thermals, water temperature hovers around 22–24°C (comfortable for swimming, warm enough without wetsuit), and harbor crowding hasn't peaked. Tourist season (July-August) brings warmer water (26–27°C) but also packed anchorages — Hvar Town harbor fills with 200+ boats by late July.

May and October are survivable but unpredictable. Spring winds can turn gusty without warning; autumn brings rain squalls. Water temperatures drop to 18–20°C (cold for casual swimming). Winter (November–March) is not practical; marinas reduce staffing and fuel dock hours.

Book catamarans 8-12 weeks ahead for peak season (July-August) or 4-6 weeks for shoulder months. Last-minute availability exists in May and October but at reduced discounts.

Local Flavor: Food and Culture

The Dalmatian coast's culinary identity flows through Trogir and its surrounding harbors. Peka (meat or seafood slow-cooked under a ceramic dome) appears on nearly every konoba menu; the Best version features octopus, potatoes, and local white wine. Pašticada—beef slow-braised in red wine with prunes—is heavier, better for cooler evenings. Brodet, a Mediterranean fish stew, varies by harbor (Trogir's version traditionally includes red mullet and scorpion fish).

Plavac Mali, a deep red wine from the nearby Dalmatian vineyards, pairs naturally with these dishes. Lighter meals revolve around fresh catches: grilled mackerel (srdela), sole (list), or sea bream (orada), often simply prepared with olive oil, lemon, and herbs. Pošip, a crisp white wine from the Neretva valley (south of Trogir), accompanies white fish and seafood. Both wines are cheap (8–12 euros per bottle at Konzum) and excellent.

Street markets in Trogir (Wednesday and Saturday mornings, waterfront square) sell seasonal produce, cheeses (particularly the aged sheep's milk variety from Neretva), and cured meats. Buy fresh at the morning market, cook aboard, and you'll eat better than most restaurants offer.

FAQ: Catamaran Charter from Baotić

How far in advance should I book a catamaran from Yachtclub Seget Marina Baotić?

For July-August, book 10-12 weeks prior. June, September, and early October (shoulder season) need 6-8 weeks. May, late October, and winter months often have last-minute availability at 15-20% discounts. Early booking also unlocks operator discounts (10-15% for 12+ weeks lead time).

Is a sailing license required to charter a catamaran bareboat from Baotić?

Croatia requires a valid certificate of competency (International Certificate of Competency, RYA Day Skipper level, or equivalent) for bareboat charter of any vessel over 24 meters or with engine power exceeding 9.9 kilowatts. If your team lacks this, book a skippered catamaran charter where the operator's licensed skipper commands the boat and your group focuses on enjoyment.

What is the best month to sail from Trogir to Hvar and back?

June and September offer the steadiest Maestral winds (10-12 knots), water temps around 22-24°C, and reasonable crowd levels. July-August are warmer (water 26-27°C) but crowded. May and October work if you're flexible with weather; expect occasional rain squalls and 18-20°C water.

Can I sail from Baotić to the Kornati National Park?

Yes, but it's a longer passage. Kornati lies roughly 30 nautical miles north, requiring a 5-6 hour sail from Trogir with favorable wind. Most charterers prefer the Šolta-Brač-Hvar circuit (closer, more sheltered anchorages). Combine catamaran charter with a guided Kornati day trip or base from Šibenik marina if Kornati is your priority.

Are fuel, food, and mooring fees included in the catamaran charter price?

Typically no. The charter rate covers the boat and standard insurance; fuel, paid harbor fees (Hvar and Vis), and provisioning are separate. Budget 100-150 euros daily per four-person crew for groceries (Croatian markets are affordable), 10-15 euros per night for paid moorings in main harbors, and current diesel rates for fuel. Some operators bundle fuel into the weekly package—confirm when booking.

What happens if weather prevents sailing?

Catamarans can handle Maestral winds up to 15-18 knots comfortably; above that, most operators recommend staying in harbor or shortening passages. Bura winds (cold, northeasterly, can exceed 30 knots) rarely occur in June-September but demand respect. Your skipper or operator will advise harbor days or route changes; this is normal and not refundable, though your team can explore ashore, visit restaurants, and rest.
