Catamaran Charter from Rogač (Šolta)

Rogač anchors Šolta's identity as a regional charter hub within the Split sailing ecosystem. This working port, where ferries converge and fishing boats prepare for daily departures, positions catamarans perfectly for multi-island exploration spanning the broader Dalmatian coast. From Rogač, your catamaran can venture northwestward to Trogir's medieval old town, southeastward toward Brač and Vis, or explore the protected coves surrounding Šolta itself. The location's strategic advantage lies not in comfort amenities but in passage planning: Rogač sits at the crossroads where northern Kvarner routes meet southern Hvar and Vis approaches, making it ideal for ambitious sailors planning week-long circumnavigations rather than leisurely island hopping.

Passage Planning: North to Trogir, South to Hvar

Northwestward from Rogač, Trogir lies four hours under sail. The Venetian old town, ringed by medieval fortifications and connected to the mainland by a drawbridge, represents Adriatic cultural sailing at its finest. Anchorages near the old town fill nightly with catamaran fleets; taverna-hopping becomes the evening ritual. Longer passages approach Kvarner's northern islands (Vis, Brač's western approaches) within reach of week-long charters.

Southeastward, Hvar island lies 30+ nautical miles away. Multi-day passages through Šolta's protection, across the Adriatic's deeper channels, and toward Hvar Town's spacious harbor create legitimate passage-sailing experiences—not day-sails, but substantive open-water work. This southeasterly arc suits confident crews and well-provisioned catamarans.

Šolta as Staging Ground

Šolta's protected bays (Nečujam, Maslinica, Stomorska) serve as staging points rather than final destinations for many Rogač-based charters. Crew overnight in Nečujam bay, provision in Stomorska, and depart early the following morning for longer passages. This approach contrasts sharply with vacation-charter philosophy (maximum time anchored); instead, Rogač-based catamarans prioritize passage-planning, navigation practice, and multi-island route optimization.

Local knowledge of Šolta's harbor-entrance conditions, seasonal wind patterns, and provisioning logistics becomes crucial. Boat4You staff, familiar with Rogač's regional role, provide detailed briefings on passage planning and local contacts.

Drvenik Islands & Coastal Exploration

The Drvenik islands (Školj, Krkan, Mali Drvenik) sit southwest of Šolta, creating popular overnight stops for fleets planning southeastward passages. Multiple protected anchorages, minimal crowding, and pristine natural character make Drvenik appealing to catamaran crews seeking authentic island experiences. Passages from Rogač toward Drvenik typically proceed via Šolta's eastern bays, creating logical routing for multi-day explorations.

Marina Infrastructure & Services

Rogač harbor operates year-round. Summer fuel dock (7 AM–6 PM) serves catamarans departing on extended passages. Water filling is available; crews top tanks before departing. Basic mooring provided at €2–€3 per meter daily. Reception coordinates route planning and connects catamaran crews with local fishermen, historians, and cultural guides who enhance regional exploration.

Provisioning happens at the harborside supermarket and through local suppliers. Fresh fish markets operate seasonally (summer mornings); direct purchases from fishermen ensure quality and fair pricing. Taverna meals cost €12–€18 per person; most establishments welcome charter crews and offer informal group rates.

Seasonal Conditions & Passage Considerations

Summer (June–August) offers reliable winds ideal for passage sailing; Force 3–4 northwest winds develop predictably by midday. Spring (May–June) brings lighter conditions suited to learning passages; autumn (September–October) offers balanced wind and warm water (22–23°C). Winter sailing is challenging; November–February see occasional gales and reduced visibility.

Most passage-oriented charters operate June through September. Boat4You recommends June–July and September for the ideal balance of weather reliability and manageable crowds.
