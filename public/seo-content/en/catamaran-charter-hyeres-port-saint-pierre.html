Port Saint-Pierre Facilities & Infrastructure

Port Saint-Pierre (also called Port Pothuau) is Hyères' primary commercial and charter marina, managed by professional harbormaster services. The facility includes:

Berthing: 450+ berths, 14 dedicated to catamaran charter fleet. Fixed berths with rubber fenders and ring-bolt system accommodate vessels 30–55 feet. All berths include individual water and 220V shore power connections.

Fuel & Utilities: Dedicated fuel dock with marine diesel (currently €1.50–1.70/liter), petrol pump, potable water, pump-out service for holding tanks, ice, and waste disposal. Oil recycling available. Shower facilities, laundry, and secure parking for rental cars or crew vehicles.

Reception & Services: 24-hour harbor control, harbormaster office open 8:00–18:00 daily. Provisioning nearby (supermarkets within walking distance), provisioning via charter operators (pre-provisioned vessels available). Repair yard and marine mechanics (engine, electrical, canvas) on-site or within minutes.

Safety & Security: Gated entry, CCTV monitoring, secure crew boarding areas. All modern safety infrastructure (fire suppression, first aid station, weather radar).

Location Advantage: 4 km from Hyères-Toulon Airport

Hyères-Toulon International Airport (TLN) serves Paris CDG (2.5 hours), Lyon, Marseille, and most European hubs with direct or one-stop connections. The airport is just 4 km (10-minute taxi/rental-car drive) from Port Saint-Pierre, making crew changes seamless. Families flying in from UK, Germany, or Scandinavia appreciate the direct routes and quick marina connection—embark within 90 minutes of landing.

Rental car desks are on-site at TLN. Alternatively, local taxi/shuttle services (€15–20) connect airport to marina. Many charter operators include transfer coordination in pre-embarkation services.

Îles d'Hyères: The Three-Island Loop

Porquerolles Island (North): The largest and most accessible. Ferry service operates from Hyères mainland (20 minutes by fast ferry). Anchoring focuses on Port Man (popular, crowded) and Plage Notre-Dame (calm, spacious). Clear turquoise water, 8 km of beaches, pine forest hikes, excellent tavernas ashore. Most charter crews overnight here first night.

Anchoring restrictions: Porquerolles is a national park with limited buoy anchorages. Arrive early (before 1400) to secure spot. No bottom-anchoring in protected areas; use provided mooring buoys. Maximum stay: 2 nights per visit. Swimming and snorkeling exceptional; no jet-skis or commercial vessels allowed.

Port-Cros Island (Middle): The most protected and intimate. Named for its port (literally "port of the cross"). Dramatic red-cliffed coastlines, underwater archeological park (ancient Roman amphoras visible through snorkel mask), pristine forest ecosystem. Single anchorage in the port proper, 20 buoys for visitors. Arrival by early afternoon essential. Only taverna is the Auberge des Glycines—book dinner ahead. One-night stopover typical.

Levant Island (East): The wildest and least developed. Shallower anchorages, fewer buoys, longer boat ride from Port Saint-Pierre (8 nm). Clothing-optional beaches on the west side (famous in France). Minimal facilities ashore; bring provisions. Solitude and dramatic geology reward adventurous crews. One-night stopovers; popular with sailing clubs and quiet seekers.

Sample 3-5 Day Itinerary: Îles d'Hyères Loop

Day 1 (Hyères, Morning): Collect catamaran at Port Saint-Pierre, safety briefing (1–2 hours), provisioning at marina stores or ashore. Depart afternoon (12:00–14:00). Motor/sail 5 nm north to Porquerolles, arrive Port Man by 16:00. Anchor, swim, dinner ashore at marina taverna. Evening boat briefing, crew rest.

Day 2 (Porquerolles to Port-Cros): Morning beach swim, hike through national park forest. Depart 11:00, motor/sail east 4 nm to Port-Cros. Arrive by 12:30, secure buoy mooring early. Underwater park snorkel, lunch ashore or aboard, dinner at Auberge des Glycines (reserve ahead). Evening stargazing from deck.

Day 3 (Port-Cros to Levant, or rest-day variant): Option A: Motor/sail 5 nm to Levant (1 hour). Anchor west side, explore clothing-optional beaches or hike eastern cliffs. Dinner aboard (quieter crew evening). Option B: Rest-day at Port-Cros—sleep in, extended snorkel, repeat hike, nap afternoon, sunset anchorage evening.

Day 4 (Return to Hyères, or Toulon Extension): Motor back north 8 nm to Port Saint-Pierre (1.5–2 hours). Arrival by 13:00, early lunch, rest afternoon. Or, extend to Toulon (5 nm east), overnight, return Day 5.

Day 5 (Check-out): Morning final provisioning/water top-up, wash catamaran exterior, fuel if needed. Haul out (engine wash-down), gear stowage, handover documentation, crew depart. Typical check-out: 10:00–11:00.

Catamaran Handling in Shallow Lagoon Approaches

Port Saint-Pierre sits in Hyères Bay, where shallow lagoon transitions (depth 2–6 meters) require careful maneuvering. Modern charter catamarans (40–45 feet) typically draw 1.0–1.3 meters, granting safe passage. However, three practical notes:

Buoy Approach: Porquerolles and Port-Cros mooring buoys sit in 4–8 meter depths. Twin catamaran engines provide precision steering; approach slowly (< 3 knots), watch depth-sounder, and let crew guide you onto buoy ring (not anchor). Failure to moor properly risks swinging into neighbors.

Dinghy Launches: Catamaran wide beam allows easy dinghy stowage and deployment. Shallow anchorages suit small inflatables (90 hp outboard typical); launch from davits or deploy from stern. No deep-water restrictions apply—dinghy work is easier than monohulls.

Exit Strategy (Wind/Weather): Hyères Bay is exposed to southeast winds (rare) and mistral funneling (Sept–Oct). If forecasted wind > 25 knots, either overnight at protected Toulon Naval Arsenal (8 nm east) or secure double-mooring at Port Saint-Pierre. Catamaran stability and twin engines provide safety; unseasonable weather is rare May–September.

Provisioning & Services from Port Saint-Pierre

Supermarkets: Carrefour and Casino stores 400 meters from marina (walking distance). Fresh produce, wines, cheeses, frozen provisions, drinks. Budget €15–20 per crew daily for full meals.

Charter Pre-Provisioning: Most operators offer optional pre-loaded provisions (breakfast groceries, welcome champagne, ice). Pre-ordering saves time and ensures quality.

Fishing & Water Sports: Local dive shops (scuba, snorkel gear rental), fishing rod hire available ashore or via operators. Underwater park in Port-Cros includes guided snorkel tours (bookable in advance).

Restaurants & Bars: Hyères town center (1 km) has dozens of restaurants, from casual bistros (€12–15 mains) to fine dining (€40+). Marina taverna and waterfront cafés cater to sailors. Thursday evening is market night in town center—fresh produce, flowers, local goods.

Seasonal Considerations & Weather

May–June: Warm (20–22°C water), light-to-moderate winds (10–16 knots), lengthening daylight. Ideal for first-time island explorers and families. Buoy availability excellent.

July–August: Hot (24–26°C water), consistent thermals (12–18 knots), crowded anchorages. Book early (8–12 weeks ahead). Water conditions ideal for swimming; evening thunderstorms possible (rare). Sunset sailing + evening stargazing prime experience.

September–October: Warm (20–24°C), transition winds (variable 12–20 knots), fewer tourists. Buoys more available; less crowding. Occasional mistral squalls (rare but forecast-able). Excellent provisioning availability; better crew pacing for less-experienced sailors.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a sailing license to charter from Port Saint-Pierre?

Bareboat catamarans require proof of competency (IYC, Day Skipper, or equivalent). Most operators accept basic certifications. Boat4You partners verify your qualifications during booking. Crewed options available for those without formal certification.

Can I visit all three islands in a single weekend?

Technically yes, but not recommended. A 3-day itinerary covers Porquerolles + Port-Cros comfortably. Levant requires 4+ days. Rushing defeats the purpose. Most crews overnights 2 nights (Porquerolles, Port-Cros) and return Day 3 afternoon.

What if I can't secure a mooring buoy at an island?

Buoy supply varies; peak season (July–August) fills by 14:00. Arrive before 13:00 or book buoys in advance via harbor master. If full, motor to nearby Lavandou (15 nm east) or Bandol (20 nm west) and day-trip back via dinghy. Overnight at Toulon (calm, typically available) is backup plan.

How much does a catamaran charter from Port Saint-Pierre cost?

A 40-foot catamaran ranges €2,800–€4,200 weekly (May–June, Sept–Oct bareboat). July–August peaks €4,200–€6,500. Crewed charters add €400–€550 daily. Check live pricing and vessel specs.

Is Port Saint-Pierre suitable for solo cruisers or couples?

Absolutely. Smaller catamarans (35–40 feet) handle well with two people. Marina support, calm nearby anchorages, and short dinghy runs suit couples. Many solo/dual-crew charters depart Port Saint-Pierre for quiet island weeks.
