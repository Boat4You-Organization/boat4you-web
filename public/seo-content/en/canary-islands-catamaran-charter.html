Catamaran Charter in the Canary Islands

Why Choose a Catamaran for Canary Islands Sailing

Catamarans represent the optimal platform for Canary Islands charter sailing, delivering unmatched stability, spacious accommodations, and versatility across the archipelago's diverse sailing conditions. The dual-hull design excels in Atlantic swell conditions, minimizing pitch and roll motion that traditional monohulls experience. For families, larger groups, and sailors seeking comfort alongside performance, catamarans redefine the charter experience.

Boat4You operates a fleet of modern catamarans (34-48 feet) specifically selected for tropical and subtropical charter operations. These vessels combine excellent sailing characteristics with luxurious interior layouts, typically featuring three to four private cabins, spacious galley kitchens, and comfortable saloon areas. The wide beams—often spanning 18-20 feet—create exceptional living space and onboard stability during passages.

The shallow draft (1.0-1.3 meters) enables access to isolated anchorages unreachable by deeper-hulled monohulls. Volcanic formations throughout the Canaries create stunning yet shallow underwater topography; catamarans navigate these environments with confidence. Dual engines on larger catamarans provide redundancy and enhanced maneuverability in tight harbor approaches.

Performance in Atlantic Conditions

The Canary Islands' Atlantic location subjects sailing vessels to significant swell from ocean storms originating thousands of miles away. Unlike monohulls that pitch dramatically in these conditions, catamarans' wide stance distributes wave energy across two hulls, creating a stable platform for cooking, sleeping, and movement below decks. Charter sailors report superior comfort aboard catamarans during Atlantic swell passages, particularly on overnight trips between islands.

The trade wind regime (15-25 knots from the northeast) favors catamaran sailing, providing steady wind angles for efficient progress. Broader hulls catch more wind pressure while maintaining directional control, allowing rapid inter-island transits. Modern catamarans achieve 6-8 knots in 15-knot trade winds—sufficient for comfortable day sailing without excessive motion or fatigue.

Catamarans excel in light wind conditions too, with massive sail plans relative to weight allowing progress even in gentle breezes. This characteristic proves invaluable during occasional calms or lee-side passages where islands block trade winds. Speed potential—often 10-12 knots in 20-knot winds—reduces transit times, maximizing exploration time at each anchorage.

Interior Comfort and Space

A typical 40-foot charter catamaran offers four separate cabins: the master suite in the main hull with ensuite head and shower, plus three additional staterooms accommodating 2-4 additional guests. Total berth capacity ranges 6-8 people comfortably, making catamarans ideal for extended family groups, couples seeking private space, or small corporate retreats.

Galley kitchens aboard charter catamarans rival small apartments, featuring refrigerator/freezer units, three-burner stove, microwave, and substantial counter space. The open-plan saloon area seats eight to ten people for dining and relaxation. Hardtop canvas covers the entire maindeck, providing all-weather comfort while entertaining or taking meals al fresco.

Abundant fresh water (typically 200-300 liters) supports daily showers, laundry, and full meal preparation. Solar panels and robust battery systems ensure 110v AC power for entertainment systems, lighting, and appliances. Air conditioning on luxury models maintains comfort during surprisingly warm anchorages. These amenities transform weeks-long charters into genuine floating homes rather than camping experiences.

Inter-Island Itineraries and Passages

A typical week-long catamaran charter covers 60-90 nautical miles of inter-island passages plus numerous day sails and anchorage explorations. The recommended northern route connects Las Palmas (Gran Canaria) → Puerto de Mogán → Puerto de las Nieves → Vallehermoso (La Gomera) → San Sebastián (La Gomera) → Playa Santiago (La Gomera) → Tenerife southern coast, covering manageable daily distances of 15-25 nautical miles.

The southern and eastern route from Tenerife explores Los Gigantes, Playa de las Américas, Costa Adeja, south to Fuerteventura and Lanzarote, then northeast to Arrecife. This 7-10 day itinerary suits sailors seeking consistently moderate winds and protected anchorages. Passages range 6-12 hours, allowing overnight motor-sailing or day-and-a-half transitions when favorable winds prevail.

Catamaran performance transforms longer passages into positive experiences rather than endurance tests. The Tenerife-to-Gran Canaria passage (approximately 40 nm) typically requires 6-8 hours with the trade wind assistance, completed comfortably in a single morning or early afternoon. No overnight offshore passage becomes necessary unless deliberately undertaken for ARC training or transatlantic preparation.

Anchorages and Anchoring Characteristics

Catamaran twin anchors—typically sized at 35-45 pounds—secure easily in the volcanic sand and mud substrates prevalent throughout the Canaries. The shallow draft permits anchoring in 6-10 meters of water while maintaining 200+ meters scope, providing reliable holding in northeasterly trade winds. Emergency anchoring in 4-meter shallows remains possible, though challenging.

The spacious foredeck and wide beam simplify anchor deployment and retrieval without bow-thruster assistance. Most charter catamarans feature electric windlasses, motorized winches reducing the physical effort required for 60 meters of scope in deeper water. Dual hawsepipes accommodate Mediterranean mooring (dropping stern anchor, motoring in to the dock), expediting berthing procedures in crowded charter harbors.

Popular anchorages include Playa de las Americás (Tenerife—excellent holding, beach provisioning), Arguineguin (Gran Canaria—established charter base), Corralejo (Fuerteventura—stunning desert vistas), and Puerto de Mogán (Gran Canaria—picturesque village and provisioning). Each location suits catamaran operations perfectly, offering good holding ground and access to services.

Pricing, Seasons, and Availability

Catamaran charter rates vary seasonally: high season (November-March) commands €2,500-€4,000 weekly; shoulder seasons (April-May, September-October) offer €1,800-€2,800 weekly; summer (June-August) reductions drop rates to €1,500-€2,300 weekly. Pricing includes bareboat charter, insurance, and navigation equipment. Skippered charters add €300-€500 daily for professional crew.

Booking six months ahead secures peak-season availability. Easter and Christmas periods book entirely months in advance. Last-minute charters (within four weeks) encounter limited inventory but may negotiate discounts. Flexibility regarding travel dates dramatically improves pricing and selection.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many people can comfortably sleep aboard a 40-foot catamaran?

Most 40-foot catamarans accommodate 6-8 people in four separate cabins. Two people occupy the master suite; two additional double or twin cabins sleep four more; a small third cabin occasionally sleeps two. Charter operators typically restrict occupancy to six for optimal comfort, leaving cabin space for luggage and personal items.

Can inexperienced sailors charter a catamaran in the Canary Islands?

Bareboat charters require at least one certified skipper with verifiable sailing experience. Many charter companies provide introductory sailing courses before your vacation begins.

Is catamaran sailing more stable than monohull sailing?

Yes—catamarans' dual-hull design minimizes pitch and roll motion significantly. Most sailors report superior comfort aboard catamarans, especially during Atlantic swell passages. The wider beam distributes forces across two hulls, creating a remarkably steady platform for sleeping, cooking, and dining below decks. Children, elderly passengers, and seasickness-prone individuals typically prefer catamarans.

What is the fuel consumption for a 40-foot catamaran?

Twin 55-horsepower diesel engines consume approximately 8-12 liters per hour while motoring at full cruise speed (8-10 knots). Excellent wind conditions in the Canaries mean sailors typically motor only 5-15 percent of charter time, minimizing fuel costs. Budget €200-€400 weekly fuel for a standard bareboat charter under typical conditions.

Why might I choose a monohull sailing yacht over a catamaran?

Monohull yachts offer superior sailing performance, tighter handling, and lower charter costs (typically €300-€600 weekly cheaper). Sailors pursuing oceanographic accuracy or competitive racing favor monohulls. Catamarans suit families, larger groups, and comfort-focused vacationers.
