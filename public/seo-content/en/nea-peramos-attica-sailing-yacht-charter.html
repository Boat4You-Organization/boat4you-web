Why Sail from Nea Peramos?

Nea Peramos embodies village sailing culture. Located on Megara Bay, 70 kilometres west of Athens, this is where working fishing boats still dominate the harbour and taverna owners know regulars by name. Unlike commercial ports, Nea Peramos offers sailors a slower, deeper connection to place. The 32-strong sailing fleet comprises classic monohulls—sloops, cutters, and traditional rigged vessels—built for real sailing rather than showboat comfort.

The village's position is strategically brilliant: northwesterly winds send you directly toward the Corinth Canal, opening routes to the Ionian and beyond. Eastward, the Saronic Gulf's quieter reaches offer sheltered, less-crowded mooring fields than Lavrion or Alimos. Nea Peramos is where you sail first, socialise second. The place itself—narrow streets, fishing heritage, taverna culture—infuses your charter with something deeper than logistics.

The Fleet: Sailing Character & Reliability

Our 32 monohulls range from nimble 32-foot day sailors to 43-foot cruising vessels, predominantly Beneteau, Jeanneau, and Bavaria designs. All carry modern rigging (most feature in-mast furling and self-tacking jibs), full navigation suites, and reliable diesel engines for motoring to windward. The fleet emphasises proven, simple systems over fancy electronics—most crews find that refreshing.

Cabin layouts vary: smaller yachts suit couples; 38–43-foot vessels comfortably sleep 6–8. All carry auxiliary engines, autopilot, and modern galley systems. Tender dinghies and snorkelling kits are included.

Sailing Routes: Two Directions, Infinite Possibilities

Westward (toward the Ionian): a 3–4 hour reach brings you to the Corinth Canal entrance. Transit the Canal (a once-in-a-lifetime experience) and emerge into the Ionian Sea. From there, Kefalonia, Ithaca, Lefkas, and the Ionian island chain are yours to explore—fewer tourists, wilder coastlines, and dramatic mountain backdrops. Many crews plan a week-long Corinth-and-Ionian loop departing Nea Peramos.

Eastward (toward the Saronic): sail to quieter Megara Bay anchorages, then venture toward Aegina, Poros, Spetses, and Hydra. This direction is gentler and perfect for learning crews or those seeking relaxed pacing.

Wind & Weather from the Village Perspective

Megara Bay and the western Saronic see predictable northerly winds in summer (10–16 knots on average), with occasional stronger blows. The Ionian approach via the Canal benefits from broader wind patterns and slightly more breeze as you sail westward. Spring (May) and autumn (September–October) offer the best conditions: moderate winds, comfortable temperatures (18–22°C air), and lower crew stress.

Summer is warm (26–29°C), with reliable afternoon breezes perfect for families. Winter sees occasional southerly gales and colder water; most crews avoid this season.

Nea Peramos Town: Culture, Food & Community

This is not a tourist theme-park village. Nea Peramos is where locals live, fish, and cook. Waterfront tavernas are family-run, often owned by the same families for decades. Order grilled octopus, sea urchin, or fresh mussels, and the owner will tell you which boat landed them that morning. Regional white wine from Megara Bay vineyards pairs perfectly with the local catch.

The village Thursday/Friday market is where to provision: tomatoes, cheese, bread, local honey, and wines at fair prices. Many sailors spend their final night ashore eating, drinking local wine, and soaking in the village atmosphere before casting off.

Facilities & Provisioning at Nea Peramos

The small marina (60–80 berths) is intimate and well-run. Water, power, fuel, and waste disposal services are available. The village lacks mega-marts, but provisioning is straightforward via neighbourhood shops, bakeries, and the waterfront fish market. Everything a crew needs sits within a 10-minute walk of the quay.
