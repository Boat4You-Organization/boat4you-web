The Lycian Coast: History at Every Anchorage

The Lycian Coast, stretching roughly 150 kilometres from Fethiye to Antalya, comprises some of the Aegean and Mediterranean's most evocative sailing grounds. Ancient Lycian civilisation left behind an unmistakable architectural legacy: carved rock tombs perched on cliffsides, ceremonial temples, and amphitheatres now accessible only by water. Key sailing destinations include Myra—with its impressive four-storey tomb façades cut directly into granite walls—Olympos, where the legendary Chimaera eternal flames still burn across the mountainside, and the sunken city of Kekova, a partially submerged Hellenistic settlement visible beneath crystal-clear waters.

Primary Destinations and Sailing Bases

Kaş, the southernmost Turkish marina and a gem of refined Levantine sailing, sits directly opposite the Greek island of Megisti. This intimate port, populated by whitewashed boutique hotels and small cafés overlooking the harbour, offers the perfect launch point for exploring Kekova's underwater ruins and the dramatic Simena clifftop castle. Day sails from Kaş reach Kekova in four to five hours, allowing charter guests to dive and snorkel over ancient structures—tombs, houses, and ceremonial sites submerged two thousand years ago.

Kalkan, 30 kilometres north-west of Kaş, provides a deeper natural harbour and a picturesque village atmosphere with excellent provision markets and waterfront dining. Sailing vessels based in Kalkan offer excellent routes toward Phaselis, a Hellenistic port city with three natural harbours and standing temple remains, or westward toward Kemer and its combination of mountain scenery and historical sites.

Kemer, centrally located and host to a well-developed modern marina, acts as a natural hub for longer itineraries. Kemer sits roughly 45 kilometres south-west of Antalya and connects easily to the high peaks of the Taurus Mountains. Crewed charters departing from Kemer often follow week-long itineraries encompassing Olympos, Phaselis, and the quieter anchorages of Çıralı and Adrasan.

Finike, slightly inland and less frequented than Kaş or Kalkan, remains a working fishing village and authentic Turkish harbour. Finike serves as an ideal waypoint for multi-week voyages or as a quieter alternative to busier charter bases. The anchorage offers quick access to Olympos and Chimera Bay, where geothermal flames and thermal springs create a unique combined sailing and hiking experience.

Wind Conditions and Sailing Seasons

The Turkish Mediterranean enjoys consistent summer thermals and seasonal winds. May through September delivers warm, stable weather with average temperatures between 28°C and 32°C. The Etesian winds—predictable northerlies flowing from the Aegean—typically establish by mid-morning and provide excellent sailing conditions through late afternoon. Winter months (November to March) bring intermittent southerly and north-westerly winds, rougher seas, and occasionally closed anchorages, though the region remains significantly more sheltered than northern Aegean waters.

Typical One-Week Itinerary

A classic Lycian Coast circuit departs Kaş on a Monday morning, navigating first to Kekova's sunken ruins for two days of exploration and diving. The vessel then sails to Simena, a dramatically perched village accessible by tender, before continuing north-west toward Olympos and Chimera Bay—a half-day sail of roughly 35 nautical miles. The itinerary typically includes three to four additional anchorages chosen for calm conditions, underwater archaeology, or beach access, concluding Friday afternoon back in Kaş or alternative ports depending on charter flexibility.

Cultural and Gastronomic Highlights

Mediterranean Türkiye represents a crossroads of Greek, Roman, Persian, and Ottoman influences, visible in both archaeological sites and contemporary cuisine. Waterfront restaurants in Kaş and Kalkan serve fresh grilled fish, meze platters, and traditional Turkish mezze sourced from daily markets. Local specialities include mezze (herb and cheese pastries), salatası (traditional salads), and exceptional fresh seafood. Boat4You crewed charters often include provisioning support, helping guests and crews plan onboard meals featuring local ingredients and regional recipes.

Diving and Water Activities

The sunken city of Kekova represents the Mediterranean's most accessible underwater archaeological site accessible to charter guests. Snorkelling and diving operations run throughout summer months, with local dive centres offering PADI certification courses and guided explorations of tomb structures, quays, and residential foundations submerged 2 to 8 metres below the surface. Cliff diving near Kaş, particularly at locales like Aquarium Bay, attracts adventure enthusiasts, while kayaking through shallow coastal grottoes and bioluminescent caves offers quieter water activities.

Marina Services and Provisioning

Modern marinas in Kemer and Kalkan offer fuel, fresh water, and power; Kaş and Finike provide more basic but reliable facilities with exceptional provisioning markets nearby. Greek provisioning (fresh vegetables, cheeses, and wines) arrives regularly at Turkish ports via local supply networks. Repair facilities for engines, rigging, and electrical systems are available in larger centres, though pre-charter vessel inspections and equipment checks are advisable for critical systems.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best season for sailing the Turkish Mediterranean?

May through September offers ideal conditions: warm weather (28–32°C), consistent Etesian winds, and reliable sunshine. June and July peak in both weather stability and charter demand. September provides slightly fewer crowds while maintaining excellent conditions. April and October offer shoulder-season advantages with fewer guests, though occasional wind variability may necessitate itinerary flexibility.

Can beginners sail gulets and crewed yachts in this region?

Absolutely. Crewed charters separate sailing expertise from guest involvement. Professional crews handle sailing, navigation, and docking while guests relax, assist optionally, or pursue water activities. pairs beginner-friendly crews with suitable vessels and simplified itineraries to ensure comfort and safety throughout the charter.

Are sailing yachts or gulets better for Mediterranean Türkiye?

Both excel in this region. Gulets offer romantic aesthetics, spacious decks, and established crewed infrastructure ideal for guests prioritising comfort and cultural immersion. Sailing yachts provide more hands-on marine experience, greater manoeuvrability in tight anchorages, and lower daily costs. helps match vessel types to guest preferences, experience levels, and budget.

Is diving at Kekova accessible during charter?

Yes. Most charters include two to three days anchored near Kekova, with easy access to local dive operators and snorkelling guides. Certified divers can explore underwater structures; non-certified guests enjoy guided snorkelling at shallower depths. coordinates with reputable dive centres to ensure safety and compliance with Turkish maritime archaeology regulations.

What documentation do I need for a Turkish Mediterranean charter?

A valid passport (valid for at least six months beyond your charter date) is essential. Sailing qualifications (RYA, ASA, or equivalent) are required only if you plan to skipper yourself; crewed charters require no sailing certifications. Travel insurance is strongly recommended.
