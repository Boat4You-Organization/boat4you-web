The Inner Islands: A Week of Pure Discovery

A typical week-long charter from Eden Island Island follows the well-traveled inner-island route: northeast to Praslin's Baie Ste Anne (overnight), south to La Digue and Félicité Island (two nights exploring Anse Source d'Argent), westward to Silhouette Island (one night in untouched anchorages), with return legs visiting Curieuse (home to the Seychelles National Park's giant tortoise population), Cousin Island (a strict bird sanctuary), and smaller uninhabited islands where coral reefs and remote beaches await snorkelers and beachcombers.

Each island destination offers distinct experiences. Praslin's Vallée de Mai showcases the Coco de Mer palm—an endemic species whose enormous, twin-lobed coconut inspired centuries of maritime legend. La Digue epitomizes tropical island romance: ox-carts instead of cars, granitic boulders framing pristine beaches, and welcoming local restaurants serving fresh-grilled fish. Silhouette Island's dense forests, endemic species, and absence of commercial tourism create a raw, wilderness experience rare in the modern Indian Ocean.

Snorkeling and Marine Adventures

Seychelles reefs rank among the world's finest snorkeling destinations. Vibrant coral gardens host tropical fish in dizzying variety—parrotfish, surgeonfish, groupers, snappers—alongside sea turtles, rays, reef sharks, and octopus. Most Eden Island catamarans carry comprehensive snorkeling gear and tender dinghies for reef access. Boat4You advises on reef-safe sunscreen (protecting coral ecosystems) and sustainable snorkeling practices. Many charters include encounters with whale sharks (seasonal, June–September, thrilling but harmless) and manta rays gliding through clear waters.

Provisioning and Dining Experiences

Eden Island's proximity to Victoria means access to the capital's central market—arguably the Indian Ocean's finest source of fresh seafood and tropical produce. Boat4You's provisioning coordinators work with local vendors to stock your catamaran with grouper, emperor, kingfish, and seasonal catches; mangoes, papayas, coconuts, and breadfruit; and authentic Creole ingredients like chili, lime, and coconut milk. Aboard the boat, you can prepare fish rougail (spiced fish stew), curried octopus, or simple grilled catches with tropical sides. Shoreside dining at Praslin and La Digue restaurants—often run by families serving ancestral recipes—complements onboard meals beautifully.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics.
