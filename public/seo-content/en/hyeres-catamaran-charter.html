Why Hyères is a Premier Catamaran Destination

Hyères occupies a uniquely favourable position on the Côte d'Azur. Just 40 kilometres south of Toulon and 50 kilometres northwest of Saint-Tropez, the city sits at the mainland's closest approach to the Îles d'Hyères—a protected national park comprising three main islands (Porquerolles, Port-Cros, Île du Levant) and numerous smaller islets. The waters between the mainland and the islands are sheltered from Atlantic swells and Atlantic-born storms by the islands themselves, creating a natural lagoon-like sailing ground perfect for catamaran cruising. Hyères is also blessed with Mediterranean light: year-round sunshine, crystalline waters, and the distinctive luminosity that inspired painters from Monet to contemporary artists.

For catamaran sailors, Hyères's advantages are manifold. The twin-hulled design thrives in the shallow bays and narrow channels between islands—areas where monohulls struggle. The stability and deck space catamarans provide means your crew enjoys the Mediterranean climate without the fatigue of significant heeling. Each offers the comfort and stability that modern Mediterranean sailing expects.

The Îles d'Hyères: A National Park Paradise

The Îles d'Hyères national park protects one of the Mediterranean's last truly pristine archipelagos. Porquerolles, the largest and most accessible island, features a broad sandy beach, a small village, restaurants, and hiking trails ascending to the island's interior pine forests. The island is car-free; visitors explore on foot or bicycle, lending an old-world Mediterranean island charm. Port-Cros, dramatically different, consists of rocky coves, underwater seagrass meadows, and a marine protection zone where snorkelling reveals extraordinary biodiversity—amberjacks, groupers, octopus, and Mediterranean seahorses inhabit the waters. Île du Levant, partially occupied by a small village and a French military facility, offers more remote anchorages and an authentic island atmosphere untouched by mass tourism.

Sailing a catamaran through this archipelago delivers unparalleled freedom. Catamarans' shallow draft (typically 1.0–1.5 metres) allows access to tiny coves and shallow bays where monohulls cannot venture. The stability means your passengers remain comfortable even when anchored in slightly exposed positions. The spacious deck provides platform for swimming, snorkelling, and the leisurely Mediterranean experience that defines luxury sailing. A week-long catamaran charter from Hyères might include overnight anchorages in pristine Port-Cros coves, daytime swimming and beachcombing on Porquerolles, and exploration of minor islands (Îlot de la Gabinière, Les Embiez) where few tourists venture.

Hyères Town: Medieval Old Town, Palm Avenues, and Local Culture

Hyères itself warrants exploration beyond its function as a charter base. The medieval old town (Vieille Ville), perched on a hillside 150 metres above the harbour, features winding cobbled streets, stone archways, and the Castle of Hyères (now ruins, but offering panoramic views). Palm-lined avenues descend toward the waterfront, remnants of the 19th-century era when Hyères was France's premier Côte d'Azur resort, favoured by Napoleon's brother and British nobility. The town's 2,000 years of history—from Greek settlement to medieval fortress to belle époque resort to modern maritime hub—is woven into every corner.

The waterfront promenade offers restaurants ranging from casual fishermen's tables to contemporary Mediterranean establishments. The market (daily, mornings) supplies fresh produce, fish, and Provençal ingredients. Wine from nearby regions (Côtes de Provence rosé, inland red from the Var hinterland) is exceptional and reasonably priced. The town's position between Toulon's urban energy and Saint-Tropez's exclusivity offers a middle ground—authentic, unpretentious, and deeply Mediterranean without pretence.

Catamaran Sailing Routes from Hyères

The classic Hyères-based catamaran circuit begins with a morning departure toward Porquerolles (14 nautical miles), arriving by late morning for a long afternoon of beach exploration and hiking. Overnight mooring in Porquerolles village bay or at a protected beach anchorage. Day two continues to Port-Cros (7 nautical miles east), where the protected marine sanctuary offers exceptional snorkelling and coastal hiking. Days three and four explore remote coves and smaller islands (Bagaud, Lévan's smaller islets), with overnight anchoring in pristine, quiet locations. Days five and six might return westward, exploring the Presqu'île de Giens (a sandy peninsula with lagoons and salt marshes) and visiting Île du Levant for a day visit, or extending toward the Îles d'Hyères' western approaches.

An alternative route pushes northwestward toward Saint-Tropez (25 nautical miles), a full-day passage suitable for performance-oriented sailors. Saint-Tropez itself remains crowded and expensive, but nearby anchorages (Îlot du Banc, Rabiou Rocks) offer dramatic scenery and quieter yacht communities. A three-day extension to Saint-Tropez with return to Hyères creates a memorable loop exploring the Côte d'Azur's most famous stretches.

Seasons, Weather, and Sailing Conditions

Hyères's sailing season runs April–October, with May–September offering the most reliable weather. Summer brings the Mistral wind (northwestern), a dry, steady flow descending from the Rhone valley. The Mistral typically develops in mid-morning, strengthens through afternoon (15–25 knots), and eases at sunset—a rhythm that favours catamaran sailing and allows for early-evening anchoring. Catamarans thrive in the steady Mistral, their shallow draft and stable platform making them ideal for the variable conditions typical of Côte d'Azur summers.

June and September are exceptional: warm weather, fewer crowds than July–August, and more variable winds that challenge and reward sailors. Spring (April–May) brings warming water and unpredictable but interesting conditions. Autumn (October) delivers warm water and occasional dramatic skies; by November, dwindling daylight and occasional Atlantic-born weather systems make extended sailing less attractive.

Flora, Fauna, and Provençal Cuisine

The Hyères region is defined by its natural beauty. Pine forests (Aleppo pine) cloak the Îles d'Hyères; thyme, rosemary, and wild fennel perfume the hillsides. Marine life is extraordinary: groupers, amberjacks, and octopus inhabit the rocky coves; Mediterranean seahorses and seagrass meadows create an underwater landscape of surprising complexity. Land fauna includes wild boar, hares, and numerous bird species (Scops owls, kestrels) typical of Mediterranean scrubland. Spring wildflowers (anemones, poppies, sage) explode across the hillsides in April–May.

Provisioning from Hyères harbour supplies fresh local produce: tomatoes, aubergines, courgettes, local cheese (tomme de Savoie from inland). Fish is abundant: sea bass, sea bream, local mussels, and spider crabs. Olive oil from the Var hinterland is golden and peppery—perfect for dressing simple Mediterranean salads. Rosé wine from Côtes de Provence vineyards (Bandol if you prefer red) pairs perfectly with grilled fish and local herbs.

A 40–45 foot catamaran typically costs €3,500–€6,000 per week in high season (July–August), €2,500–€4,000 in low season (May, June, September). Bareboat rates are 20–30% lower than crewed options. Fuel, mooring, and provisioning are additional.

June and September are ideal: warm, fewer crowds, and more interesting wind patterns. May offers spring flowers and warming water. October provides warm water and dramatic autumn light. July–August are warmest but busiest; April and November are possible but less comfortable.

Absolutely.
