Port of Mykonos: Your Charter Base & Facilities

Sailing the Cyclades: Top Routes from Mykonos

From Mykonos, the Cyclades unfurl before you, offering countless possibilities for sailing itineraries. The islands are typically separated by manageable distances, allowing for leisurely day sails and plenty of time to explore ashore.

The Delos-Rhenia Day Sail

Just a short sail southwest of Mykonos lies Delos, a UNESCO World Heritage site and the mythical birthplace of Apollo and Artemis. It's a powerful ancient sanctuary, typically visited for a few hours. Adjacent to Delos is the uninhabited island of Rhenia, famous for its pristine, sheltered coves and crystal-clear turquoise waters. Ornos Bay on Mykonos also offers a tranquil anchorage. This route is perfect for a gentle first day of sailing or a relaxed day trip, offering stunning swimming and snorkeling opportunities away from the Mykonian buzz.

Island Hopping to Syros and Paros

For a multi-day adventure, consider heading west to Syros, the administrative capital of the Cyclades, boasting neoclassical architecture in Ermoupoli and a unique cultural blend. From there, a moderate sail south will bring you to Paros, known for its traditional villages like Naoussa and Paroikia, golden beaches, and excellent local cuisine. These islands offer a more relaxed pace than Mykonos, with charming harbors and authentic tavernas. The Remote Northern Cyclades

More adventurous sailors might point their bow north towards the lesser-visited Northern Cyclades, like Tinos or even Andros. Tinos, often called the "Holy Island," provides a spiritual contrast to Mykonos, with its impressive Panagia Evangelistria church and over 1,000 dovecotes dotting the landscape. These islands offer a chance to experience a more rugged and traditional side of Greek island life, with fewer tourists and more secluded anchorages. Remember, sailing these northern reaches often involves encountering the stronger Meltemi winds.

Navigating the Aegean: Winds, Seasons, and Conditions

Sailing from Mykonos means embracing the distinctive conditions of the Aegean Sea, particularly its famed summer winds.

The Mighty Meltemi

The Meltemi is the defining weather pattern for summer sailing in the Cyclades. This strong, dry northerly wind typically blows from May to September, often reaching 5-7 Beaufort (17-33 knots) for days at a time, especially in July and August. While it can create exhilarating sailing, it demands respect and good seamanship. Sailing yachts, designed for these conditions, offer a thrilling ride, but careful route planning is essential to find sheltered anchorages in its lee. It's an integral part of the Cycladic sailing experience and a challenge many experienced sailors relish.

Best Months for Sailing

The shoulder seasons, May-June and September-October, often present the most balanced sailing conditions. In May and June, the Meltemi is usually gentler, blowing 3-5 Beaufort (7-21 knots), and the islands are less crowded. The water is refreshing, and the landscapes are at their greenest. September and early October offer consistently good winds, with the sea still warm from summer and tourist numbers beginning to thin. July and August are peak season, with the strongest Meltemi and the highest demand, so booking well in advance is highly recommended.

Planning Your Mykonos Sailing Charter: Practicalities & Costs

A sailing yacht charter from Mykonos typically ranges from 2,500 to 7,000 Euros per week, depending on the yacht's size, age, and the season. Bareboat charters require a valid sailing license from at least one crew member, recognized by Greek authorities. If you prefer to relax and let a professional handle the navigation, a skippered charter is an excellent option, typically adding 150-200 Euros per day for the skipper's fee. Fuel costs for sailing yachts are considerably lower than motorboats, primarily used for maneuvering in and out of port or when winds are light.

Provisioning is straightforward in Mykonos. Large supermarkets near the port offer a wide selection, and local markets provide fresh produce, seafood, and the distinctive Cycladic cheeses. Many charter companies also offer pre-provisioning services. Remember to factor in port fees if you plan to moor in busy harbors; while anchorages are free, some popular island ports may charge a small fee. Sample 7-Day Sailing Itinerary from Mykonos

This itinerary focuses on a mix of iconic sites and tranquil anchorages, ideal for a sailing yacht.

Day 1 (Mykonos to Rhenia): Arrive in Mykonos, complete yacht check-in, provision. Enjoy a short shakedown sail to the uninhabited island of Rhenia (approx. 1.5 hours), anchoring in one of its sheltered, pristine bays for swimming and a peaceful first night under the stars.

Day 2 (Rhenia to Syros): Set sail for Syros (approx. 3-4 hours), heading to Ermoupoli. Explore the neoclassical town, walk up to Ano Syros, and savor dinner at a traditional taverna. Moor stern-to in Ermoupoli harbor, immersing yourself in the town's vibrant atmosphere.

Day 3 (Syros to Paros): A refreshing morning sail to Paros (approx. 3 hours). Anchor off Naoussa or in Paroikia. Spend the afternoon exploring the charming fishing village of Naoussa, with its Venetian fortress, or the bustling main town of Paroikia. Indulge in fresh seafood by the waterfront.

Day 4 (Paros to Antiparos): A short hop to Antiparos (approx. 1 hour). Explore its famous sea cave, swim in the clear waters of Faneromeni beach, and enjoy the laid-back vibe of Antiparos town. The passage between Paros and Antiparos is a pleasant, often calm stretch.

Day 5 (Antiparos to Naxos): Head to Naxos, the largest of the Cycladic islands (approx. 2-3 hours). Anchor near Chora, explore the Portara monument at sunset, and wander through the old town's winding alleys. Naxos offers excellent produce and a chance to experience authentic island life.

Day 6 (Naxos to Delos & Mykonos): A morning sail back towards Mykonos, with a crucial stop at Delos (approx. 3-4 hours). Take a tender ashore to explore the ancient ruins, then find a beautiful anchorage off Delos or Rhenia for a final swim before returning to Mykonos Port in the late afternoon for your last evening on board.

Day 7 (Mykonos): Disembarkation at Port of Mykonos. Time for final souvenir shopping or a last stroll through Mykonos Town.

Experiencing Mykonos: Local Culture & Provisions

Mykonos offers more than just vibrant nightlife; it's steeped in Cycladic culture, visible in its distinctive architecture and local traditions. While chartering from the port, take time to explore Mykonos Town (Chora), from the iconic windmills above Little Venice to the whitewashed labyrinth of alleys perfect for a sunset stroll. Discover artisan shops, enjoy strong Greek coffee, or try local specialties like _louza_ (cured pork) and _kopanisti_ (spicy cheese).

For provisioning, beyond the main supermarkets, seek out local bakeries for fresh bread and pastries, and greengrocers for seasonal fruits and vegetables. Don't miss a chance to pick up some local honey or a bottle of ouzo for sundowners on deck. Dining ashore offers everything from sophisticated fine dining to humble tavernas serving up grilled gyros or fresh seafood, often accompanied by the cheerful clinking of Mythos beer bottles. FAQ

What sailing license is required for a bareboat charter from Mykonos?

For a bareboat charter in Greece, at least one person on board must hold an internationally recognized sailing license, such as an ICC (International Certificate of Competence) or an RYA Day Skipper certificate. Additionally, a second competent crew member capable of assisting with boat handling is typically required, or a signed declaration of their sailing experience. Boat4You can help you verify your license validity before booking.

Are there sheltered anchorages near Mykonos for sailing yachts?

Yes, despite Mykonos's exposure to the Meltemi, several sheltered anchorages are available. The nearby island of Rhenia offers numerous protected coves, particularly on its eastern side, providing excellent overnight stops. Within Mykonos itself, Ornos Bay and some spots around the south coast can offer good protection depending on the wind direction. Proper navigation and weather monitoring are always crucial for safe anchoring.

What are the typical costs for a Port of Mykonos sailing yacht charter?

The cost for a sailing yacht charter from Mykonos varies significantly by season, yacht size, and age. Expect to pay roughly 2,500 to 4,000 Euros per week in shoulder season (May/June, Sept/Oct) and 4,500 to 7,000 Euros or more during peak summer months (July/August) for a mid-sized sailing yacht. These prices typically exclude fuel, port fees, and provisions, which you should budget for separately. A skipper, if desired, is an additional cost.

How far in advance should I book my sailing yacht in Mykonos?

Given Mykonos's popularity and the demand for quality sailing yachts, it's highly advisable to book your charter at least 6-9 months in advance, especially if you plan to travel during July or August. For the shoulder seasons, 3-5 months ahead is often sufficient, but earlier booking always provides a wider selection of yachts and potentially better rates.
