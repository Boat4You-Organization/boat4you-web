Why Choose a Catamaran for Saronic Sailing?

A catamaran is the premier choice for families, larger groups, and anyone seeking stability, space, and ease of handling in the Saronic region. The twin-hull design eliminates the heel and motion of monohulls, making the sailing experience comfortable even in moderate swell. Boat4You offers a fleet of well-maintained sailing catamarans across the 38–50-foot range, all based at Athens Alimos Marina—Greece's largest charter hub with ~1,100 berths.

Catamarans excel in the Saronic for practical reasons: shallow draft (often 1.2–1.5 m) allows access to anchorages at Mandraki (Hydra), Chinitsa (Porto Cheli), and the narrow anchorages of Spetses where deeper-keeled monohulls cannot venture. Twin engines provide redundancy and maneuvering power in tight marina berths. Wide side decks and spacious coach roofs offer superior seating for sunbathing and dining. Capacity ranges from 4–8 berths, with modern galleys, multiple heads, and air-conditioning on newer models.

The Saronic Experience: Catamaran Routes and Anchorages

A typical 7-day Saronic catamaran charter departs Alimos and explores the following route: Alimos → Aegina (30 nm, 4–5 hours), Poros (15 nm, 2 hours), Hydra (12 nm, 2 hours, anchor at Mandraki), Spetses (12 nm, 2 hours), Porto Cheli (20 nm, 3–4 hours, anchor Chinitsa), return Alimos via Dokos and Aegina (60 nm, 8 hours). Total distance ~150 nm, easily manageable in 7 days with leisurely daily sails of 2–4 hours.

Shallow-draft catamarans access hidden anchorages: Russian Bay (Poros) at 2 m depth, Kounoupitsa (Ermioni) in 3 m, and Vidi (Spetses) in 2–3 m. Monohulls and power boats often cannot reach these gems. Daytime sails average 6–8 knots in spring/autumn and 5–12 knots in summer Meltemi winds, depending on route direction.

Climate and Seasonal Conditions

Catamarans thrive in the Saronic May–October season. June and September offer 8–15 knot winds and calm seas (ideal for families); July and August bring stronger Meltemi 15–25 knots (exhilarating for experienced sailors but bumpy for young children). Water temperature climbs from 19°C (May) to 26°C (August), suitable for swimming. Air temps hover at 28–33°C in summer.

Catamarans are particularly well-suited to the Meltemi, as the twin hulls resist weather helm and leeway, and the spacious interior offers refuge from sun and wind. Beam reach and downwind angles—common on Saronic loops—are the catamaran's sweet spot.

Licensing and Crew Requirements

Greece requires bareboat charterers to hold an International Certificate of Competence (ICC) or equivalent. Boat4You verifies credentials at booking. Groups lacking a licensed skipper can hire a professional captain (50–80 EUR/day) to handle sailing while guests enjoy the experience. A VHF-qualified crew member must be aboard alongside the skipper. All vessels carry valid Greek flag registration and insurance.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are catamarans faster than monohulls in the Saronic?

Catamarans are slightly slower upwind (due to less weather helm control and surface drag) but excel downwind and on reaches. In typical Saronic conditions (beam reach), they match monohull speeds; in strong Meltemi, lighter catamarans pull ahead. Motor comfort is the real advantage.

Can a catamaran fit into tight Mediterranean anchorages?

Yes. Shallow draft (1.2–1.5 m) and dual hulls allow tight maneuvering. Mandraki (Hydra), Chinitsa (Porto Cheli), and Vidi (Spetses) are catamaran-friendly. Some anchorages like Assos require careful approach, but skilled captains navigate them routinely.

What is the fuel consumption of a Saronic catamaran?

A 42-foot catamaran burns 8–12 liters per hour under motor at 7–8 knots. A full 400-liter tank provides 800+ nm range, easily covering a Saronic loop. Fuel is cheaper in Alimos and Piraeus than on islands.

Is a catamaran good for non-sailors?

Absolutely. Modern catamarans with motorized sails, autopilot, and redundant engines are very manageable. Hire a skipper (50–80 EUR/day) to handle navigation and sailing; crews focus on enjoying the islands.

What safety equipment is required on a Saronic catamaran charter?

All catamarans carry mandatory Greek/EU maritime safety equipment: life jackets (one per person), safety harnesses, liferaft with capacity for 125% of persons aboard, flares and signaling equipment, fire extinguishers, first-aid kit, sea anchors, fishing kits, and seasickness medication. VHF radios are standard; satellite phones available on request. Life jacket checks and man-overboard drills occur pre-departure.

Can a catamaran be chartered for corporate team-building or special occasions?

Group rates apply; larger parties (12+ people across 2–3 vessels) receive package discounts. Pre-charter planning sessions align events with itinerary and guest preferences.

What is the typical crew size on a bareboat catamaran versus a crewed charter?

Bareboat catamarans carry guest crews only (no professional crew); guests handle navigation, sailing, provisioning, and cleaning. A skipper (optional, 50–80 EUR/day) assists but does not assume full command. Light-crew catamarans provide a captain + hostess (100–150 EUR/day combined), handling navigation, mooring, and galley/cleaning support. Fully crewed catamarans (rare; 50m+) employ captain, chef, and deckhand—comparable to superyacht service. Most Saronic catamarans are bareboat or light-crew options.

How accessible are Saronic anchorages to swimmers and water activities?

Saronic anchorages are exceptionally swimming-friendly: shallow waters, sandy bottoms, calm conditions, and clear visibility. Mandraki (Hydra), Russian Bay (Poros), Chinitsa (Porto Cheli), and Vidi (Spetses) are premier swimming spots. All anchor in 3–8 m depth, allowing swimming within meters of the boat. Jet skis, paddleboards, and snorkeling gear are often available; tenders (dinghy dinghies) facilitate beach exploration. Summer water temperatures (25–26°C) suit prolonged swimming.
