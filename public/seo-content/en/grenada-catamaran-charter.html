Grenada Catamaran Charter: Spacious Island Sailing in the Spice Isle

Catamarans are the workhorses of Caribbean group sailing, and Grenada's island chain rewards their unique strengths perfectly. If you're thinking about a multiday charter with 6–12 people, or you simply want the extra deck space and shallow-draft access that a cat offers, Grenada is where the math works: you avoid the tight anchorages of the BVI, you skip the long upwind slog to the Bahamas, and you land in a place where trade winds and shelter come together. The Grenadines—Carriacou, Petite Martinique, Union Island, and the Tobago Cays—sit just north of Grenada proper, waiting to be sailed. Port Louis Marina and Le Phare Bleu are the natural launching points. This guide unpacks why a catamaran makes sense here, what routes and bases matter, and what you'll actually spend.

Why Catamarans Suit Grenada's Waters and Island Geography

Grenada sits at the southern edge of the Windward Islands, below the hurricane belt, with a charter season that runs December through May under steady northeast trade winds of 15–20 knots. The primary appeal of a catamaran is stability and shallow draft. Twin hulls mean less heel in wind, flatter cabin floors, and easier movement on deck—critical when you're managing kids, older guests, or anyone prone to seasickness. A cat typically draws 3.5–4.5 feet, compared to 6–8 feet for a monohull, which opens anchorages that single-hulled yachts avoid.

Grenada's geography reinforces this advantage. The main island is mountainous and relatively compact—St. George's on the south coast is the anchor point. Carriacou, 23 nautical miles north, is smaller and greener, with Tyrell Bay offering a traditional working harbor feel. Petite Martinique beyond that is barely a dot, but its tranquility is legendary. The Grenadines themselves—technically part of Saint Vincent and the Grenadines—include Union Island, the Tobago Cays, and smaller islands sprinkled between. The distances are modest: Port Louis to Carriacou is a half-day sail, Carriacou to the Tobago Cays another morning run. A catamaran's speed and directional stability let you cover these hops comfortably and still have time to anchor by lunch and explore.

Windward Islands sailing also means you're not fighting constant upwind conditions. The northeast trades blow steadily, and the island chain funnels them predictably. You'll find protected anchorages on the lee sides—Prickly Bay, True Blue Bay, Clarkes Court Bay on Grenada; Sandy Island, Anse La Roche on Carriacou. A catamaran's shallow draft lets you poke into bays and reef-fringed coves where deeper monohulls can't venture. The volcanic topography creates dramatic backdrops and reliable holding in sand and mud. Most days, you're sailing in 15–25 knots with good swell management under a cat's wide beam.

Top Catamaran Routes: Leveraging Beam and Draft

The Classic Grenada-to-Grenadines Run takes you north from Port Louis Marina or Le Phare Bleu (Grenada's main charter bases) to Carriacou, then onward to Petite Martinique or Union Island. Day one: motor-sail or sail to Tyrell Bay, Carriacou—a true working harbor with fishing boats, local shops, and restaurants that feel untouched by tourism. Day two: continue to the Tobago Cays (if you're Island Hopping to Union Island first) or loop back south. The route typically spans 50–70 nautical miles over 5–7 days, with daily sails of 4–8 hours. A cat's speed—typically 8–10 knots upwind, 12+ knots on reaches—means you're not spending full days at sea; you're maximizing exploration.

The Southern Grenada Loop stays on Grenada proper, threading bays and reefs from St. George's clockwise: Port Louis, Prickly Bay, True Blue Bay, Clarkes Court Bay, Dragon Bay, Magazine Beach, and back. This 3–4 day route is ideal for first-timers or families new to sailing. Distances are short (2–4 hours between anchorages), water is turquoise and protected, and you can hire a local guide at any stop. The underwater Molinière Sculpture Park near St. George's is a catamaran-friendly anchorage with snorkeling from the dinghy. A shallow-draft cat lets you poke into Magazine Beach and Lance aux Épines without worry.

The Spice Island Dive Loop pairs sailing with Grenada's growing reputation for underwater adventure. Anchor off Tyrell Bay or Sandy Island (Carriacou) for access to reef dives and wreck dives—the Bianca C, a cruise ship scuttled in 1961, is a deep wreck (80+ feet) but iconic. Shallow-draft advantage: you can anchor in 25–30 feet of water close to dive sites, avoiding the long dinghy runs that slow down monohull-based trips. Many catamarans in Grenada's charter fleet accommodate dive briefings on the spacious cabin sole, and the dual-hull design stabilizes the boat when divers are boarding.

The Working-Fishing-Village Immersion anchors in Hillsborough (Carriacou) or Gouyave (Grenada's nutmeg port). These aren't glammed-up resort towns; they're authentic. Gouyave's Saturday morning fish market is a sensory event—locals selling parrotfish, lambi, and conch straight off wooden boats. A catamaran's wide platform and shallow draft let you anchor close and dinghy to shore easily. Crew feel safe, kids can move freely, and you're not fighting a listing deck when hauling provisions aboard.

Technical Advantages: Hull Design, Cabin Layout, and Power

A catamaran's dual hulls create a wide platform that translates into real-world benefits on a Grenada charter. First: motion. Monohulls rock and roll; catamarans pitch less, heel less, and drift sideways less in a seaway. When you're sailing in 18-knot trades with a 4-foot swell, a 45-foot catamaran feels like a stable platform rather than a pendulum. Guests sleep better, cook safer, and move around the boat with confidence. The wide stance also provides redundancy—if one hull takes on water (rare), the other keeps the boat afloat and stable.

Interior layout is a catamaran signature. You get a long cabin trunk down the middle with headroom and light, plus separated cabins under each hull wing. A typical 45-footer sleeps 8–10, with private heads (some with showers) per cabin cluster. Galleys are generously proportioned—wide counters, forgiving motion for cooking. The saloon is airy, with windows and hatches. Contrast this with a 50-foot monohull, which might feel cramped in the aft cabin despite the larger waterline. For families, the extra room prevents friction; for charter groups, the multi-cabin layout lets people claim territory and maintain privacy.

Engine setup on a catamaran typically runs two 40–65 hp diesels, one per hull. That gives you redundancy (lose one engine, the other pushes you to harbor at reduced speed) and impressive maneuverability—you can rotate the boat nearly in place by powering one engine forward and one in reverse. In tight anchorages like Port Louis Marina or Prickly Bay, that agility matters. You're not wrestling a 45-ton monohull into a slot; you're pivoting a 25-ton cat with surgical precision. Fuel efficiency is often comparable to a monohull of similar length, though the total capacity is split between two tanks.

Sailing performance varies. Modern catamarans aren't the drift-down-wind slugs of 15 years ago. Newer models point well into the wind (within 50–55 degrees true), are stable on the helm, and accelerate quickly. In Grenada's steady 15–20 knot trades, a well-tuned catamaran cruises at 8–10 knots upwind and easily exceeds 12 knots on reaches. The wide beam means less leeway, and the shallow draft (3.5–4.5 feet) means you're not dragging an undersea anchor. You'll make your daily mileage and still have time to explore.

Pricing and What to Expect on a Grenada Catamaran Charter

Catamaran rates in Grenada depend on size, age, and whether it's bareboat or crewed. A bareboat 40-footer typically runs $3,500–5,500 per week in the high season (January–March); a 45-footer, $5,000–7,500. Low season (June–November) discounts drop those figures by 20–40%. Crewed catamarans—where a professional skipper and cook accompany you—start around $10,000 per week for a 45-footer and climb from there. That includes the crew's expertise, meals, and often fuel.

Additional costs to budget: mooring fees at Port Louis Marina or Le Phare Bleu ($40–80 per night), provisioning (groceries and water, typically $100–200 per person per week), dinghy fuel, and any shore activities. Dive operators charge $120–160 per dive, snorkel tours are $40–60, and restaurant meals in Grenada run $15–35 per person. Travel insurance for a charter yacht is wise, especially outside the hurricane season window—expect $5–15 per day depending on coverage.

Seasonal pricing shifts significantly. Peak (December–February) is when North Americans and Europeans escape winter; it's also when Grenada's dry season is most stable and winds are consistent. March–May is a sweet spot—fewer crowds, slightly lower rates, still excellent weather. June onwards, wet season brings afternoon showers and higher humidity, but wind remains steady and anchoring is peaceful. Hurricane risk, while Grenada is south of the traditional hurricane belt, ticks up June through November; comprehensive insurance is prudent if you're chartering in those months.

Best Times and Bases: Seasonality and Marina Strategy

December through May is the obvious choice for most people—predictable northeast trades, dry weather, blue skies, and a steady flow of international visitors. Peak season (January–March) means more boat traffic in anchorages and busier restaurants, but also reliable weather forecasts and active charter companies with full fleets.

If you prefer quieter waters, try April–May or November. April-May is the tail end of the dry season; weather remains excellent, crowds thin, and rates drop 10–20%. November is the opening of the wet season, but storms are isolated and predictable—you can see them developing and position the boat accordingly. June–October is genuinely quieter, better for divers and reef explorers, but afternoon showers are frequent and some restaurants close.

Port Louis Marina (St. George's, Grenada) is the primary base. It's in the center of the action—literally harbor-side in the capital—with restaurants, shops, provisioning, and fuel. Downsides: it's busy, moorings are tight, and the harbor can feel chaotic on busy days. If you're crewed, the marina staff coordinate everything. If bareboat, expect to manage close-quarters maneuvering.

Le Phare Bleu Marina (nearby, just south of St. George's) is quieter and more resort-like, with a pool, restaurant, and cleaner water. It's a touch more peaceful, slightly longer commute to the capital, but many bareboat charters prefer it for that reason. Some boats are based here; others are based at Port Louis.

Prickly Bay Marina (southern coast, Grenada) is the off-the-beaten-path base. It's small, intimate, and surrounded by good anchorages. Less hustle, more local flavor, ideal if you're planning a 5–7 day loop around Grenada without heading north to the Grenadines.

Sample Catamaran Itinerary: Seven Days in the Grenadines

Day 1 (Grenada): Pick up your 45-foot catamaran at Port Louis Marina in the morning. Spend the afternoon provisioning at local markets, familiarizing yourself with the boat, and motoring to Prickly Bay (30 minutes, 5 nm). Anchor, swim, settle in.

Day 2 (Grenada to Carriacou): Sail north-northwest from Prickly Bay to Tyrell Bay, Carriacou—23 nm, typically 4–5 hours of sailing in 15–18 knot trades. Arrive by early afternoon, explore the village, eat fresh lambi or conch at a local spot. The bay is well-protected and holding is excellent sand.

Day 3 (Carriacou): Day sail to Sandy Island, just offshore from Carriacou's west coast. 3 nm, 45 minutes. Sandy Island is a postcard: white sand spit, shallow turquoise water, no development. Snorkel the reefs, dive (if certified), relax. Anchor in 20 feet, dinghy to shore.

Day 4 (Carriacou to Union Island): Sail south-southwest to Union Island, approximately 20 nm. Stop at Petite Martinique en route (6 nm) if you want to visit the island's tiny village. Union is more developed—restaurants, shops, dive operators—but still laid-back. Anchor at Chatham Bay or off Ashton.

Day 5 (Union Island to Tobago Cays): Short 6 nm sail to the Tobago Cays, a protected marine park of five tiny islands with pristine reefs and white sand. Anchor in the sandy lagoon, snorkel, dive, or simply float. This is peak Caribbean postcard scenery. Park fees apply (~$10 per person, per day).

Day 6 (Tobago Cays to Carriacou): Sail back north to Carriacou for a final night. 20 nm, 4–5 hours. Overnight in Tyrell Bay or Sandy Island. Explore any part of Carriacou you missed, or simply relax before the return journey.

Day 7 (Carriacou to Grenada): 23 nm sail south to Port Louis Marina, arriving by early afternoon. Debrief, return the catamaran, fly home with stories and photos.

Why Catamaran Charters Matter: Answering Real Questions

Are catamarans slower than monohulls?

In sustained sailing, monohulls often edge out catamarans upwind. But in Grenada's trade wind regime—steady, directional northeast blow—a modern catamaran matches or beats a monohull's practical speed. You're not racing; you're moving comfortably from island to island. And in the Grenadines, where distances are short, the catamaran's extra space, shallow draft, and stable motion often win out in real-world satisfaction. A 45-foot cat cruises at 8–9 knots upwind; a 45-foot monohull might achieve 8.5–9.5. The difference is an hour over a 40 nm sail. The catamaran's interior comfort erases that cognitive gap fast.

Can a bareboat catamaran crew of 4–6 handle Grenada?

Yes, provided you have one person with solid sailing experience (or you hire a local skipper for the first day or two). A catamaran, especially a modern one, is forgiving. Twin engines make maneuvering in marinas straightforward. The motion is gentle, so crew fatigue is lower. And in Grenada, distances between anchorages are short—you're rarely facing a full ocean passage. If your team includes kids or non-sailors, the catamaran's stability and spacious deck are genuine advantages. Most bareboat operations offer a half-day or full-day briefing with a local crew member.

What if someone gets seasick on a catamaran?

Catamarans heel less and pitch less than monohulls, which helps. But in 20-knot trades with a 4-foot swell, even a cat moves. Dramamine or ginger helps many people; some find acupressure wristbands useful. The flat cabin floor and larger windows (which reduce the closed-in feeling) make the interior more comfortable. And in Grenada, you're typically anchored by mid-afternoon, so motion happens during sailing hours, not overnight. Getting the seasick person to the helm (where they can see the horizon and feel in control) often works wonders.

Is Grenada safe for catamaran charters?

Grenada is among the safer Caribbean destinations. Petty theft happens (don't leave valuable gear unattended on deck), but violent crime against tourists is rare. Stick to established anchorages (Port Louis, Prickly Bay, Tyrell Bay, Sandy Island) and avoid anchoring alone in isolated coves after dark. Use your dinghy lock, secure the boat at night, and follow commonsense travel practices. Most charter companies provide security briefings. The local sailing community is tight-knit and helpful.

Can I get a catamaran charter with no sailing experience?

Absolutely. Many charters are crewed—you hire a professional skipper and chef, and you're a passenger-with-privileges. You can learn, help trim sails if you want, or relax. Frequently Asked Questions

How far in advance should I book?

For peak season (July and August), reservations 4–6 months ahead secure the best fleet selection and pricing. Shoulder months (May, June, September) typically allow booking 6–8 weeks out.

What does the charter price include?

Base pricing usually covers the vessel, insurance, and marina fees at the home base. Fuel, provisioning, skipper service, end-cleaning, and tourist tax are generally added separately. Check the specific listing on Boat4You for a full breakdown.

Do I need a sailing licence?

For bareboat charter, a valid skipper licence plus a VHF certificate are typically required, and rules vary by country. If you don't hold one, hiring a professional skipper is straightforward — pricing runs roughly 150–220 euro per day plus meals.
