The Sailing Ground: Strait of Bonifacio and Beyond

The Strait of Bonifacio, just 11 kilometres wide, is the dramatic heart of this sailing area. Powerful currents and strong winds make it an exhilarating crossing, typically completed in 1.5–2 hours. The Maddalena Archipelago, straddling the strait's northern edge, features granite islands with white-sand beaches, sheltered anchorages, and pristine marine reserves. To the south, the Lavezzi Islands offer smaller, more intimate anchorages for those seeking solitude.

Corsica's western and eastern coasts provide diverse cruising. The rugged southwestern bays around Propriano and Bonifacio offer mountain backdrops and wild charm, while the eastern shore near Bastia and the northern cape present different sailing characters entirely. Sardinia's northeastern coast mirrors Corsica's appeal with glittering granite peaks rising from gin-clear water.

Typical 7–14 Day Itinerary

A classic week-long cruise departs Ajaccio or Porto Vecchio, crosses the Strait of Bonifacio, and explores the Maddalena Archipelago with daily anchorages and exploration hikes. Days 4–5 typically visit Spargi, Budelli (famous pink-sand beach), and Razzoli. Day 6 loops back toward Corsica, finishing in Bonifacio's harbour. A 10–14 day version extends south to Sardinia's Cala Goloritze and Costa Smeralda, or north to Cape Testa and the Bocche di Bonifacio marine reserve.

Winds, Weather & Best Seasons

The Mistral—a cold northwesterly—dominates spring and can funnel through the strait with fierce gusts. The Libeccio (southwesterly) arrives in summer and autumn, offering steady, warm sailing. May through June and September through October provide the sweet spot: strong but manageable winds, calm seas, and warm water (19–22°C in shoulder seasons, 24–26°C in peak summer). July and August are crowded and hot; winter cruising is possible but requires confidence in rougher conditions.

Marinas & Departure Points

Ajaccio and Porto Vecchio (Corsica) are primary bases with modern marina facilities and excellent provisioning. Propriano (Gulf of Valinco) offers quieter, more authentic Corsican charm. La Maddalena town (Sardinia) provides a jumping-off point for archipelago exploration, with fuel, water, and supplies. All marinas accept Boat4You charters with advance coordination.

Provisioning & Local Flavour

Corsica and Sardinia boast some of the Mediterranean's finest local cuisines. Ajaccio's bustling markets offer Corsican charcuterie, Calenzana cheese, and local wines. Fresh fish, wild boar pasta, and fig-studded desserts dominate menus. Island villages feature small provisioning shops; larger supermarkets cluster in towns. Both islands produce excellent wines—Corsican Patrimonio reds and Sardinian Cannonau are local treasures. Anchoring in quiet coves, crews often trade fresh produce with fishing boats.

Cultural Heritage and Historical Sites

Beyond sailing, Corsica and Sardinia offer rich cultural heritage. Ajaccio celebrates Napoleon's birthplace; historic palaces and museums explore Corsica's complex past. Porto Vecchio, Bonifacio, and Bastia each feature medieval citadels, Genoese towers, and charming old towns accessible by short dinghy or shore excursions. Sardinia's archaeological sites—Nuraghi stone fortresses, pre-historic tombs—dot the landscape. Boat4You crews frequently combine sailing with cultural exploration, anchoring near historic towns and extending shore time for museums, markets, and local discovery.

FAQ

Is the Strait of Bonifacio difficult to cross?

Not for experienced sailors in fair conditions, but strong currents and winds demand respect. Timing your crossing with tide tables and weather forecasts is essential. Boat4You provides detailed briefings and can arrange skippered charters if you prefer local expertise at the helm.

What's the best time to visit Corsica and Sardinia?

May–June and September–October offer ideal winds, fewer crowds, and pleasant temperatures. July–August are warmer but crowded; April and November are quieter but less reliable for sailing. Winter is possible for adventurous crews but not recommended for leisure charters.

Are there long-range anchorages or is daily sailing short?

This region supports both styles. Daily sails typically range 15–25 nautical miles, with 4–7 hour passages allowing relaxation and exploration. The Maddalena Archipelago features close-knit anchorages; longer transits to Sardinia's south coast offer more variety for extended charters.

Can we charter with children?

Absolutely. Calm anchorages, shallow wading, and kid-friendly villages make Corsica and Sardinia ideal for families. catamarans offer extra cabin space and stability; many parents choose May–June for warmer water and gentler winds.

Is provisioning and grocery shopping easy?

Yes. Larger towns (Ajaccio, Porto Vecchio, La Maddalena) have supermarkets and fresh markets. Smaller island villages have basic supplies. Stock well in main towns before heading to remote coves. Wine and local specialities are readily available and reasonably priced.

Safety and Navigation Considerations

The Strait of Bonifacio demands respect but poses no barrier to competent sailors. Timing your passage with tide forecasts and weather windows is essential. Current flows southeasterly during flood tide and northwesterly during ebb; understanding these patterns ensures smooth transits. Most crews coordinate passage timing with professional skippers' advice or arrange skippered services for added confidence. Modern GPS, detailed charts, and radio weather briefings eliminate guesswork; countless sailors cross daily without incident.

Environmental Sensitivity and Marine Reserves

Both Corsica and Sardinia are steeped in marine conservation. The Straits of Bonifacio marine reserve, Maddalena National Park, and Scandola UNESCO site exemplify Mediterranean environmental commitment. crews are briefed on anchoring protocols, protected species, and fishing restrictions. Responsible cruising—using designated mooring buoys where available, minimizing seabed disturbance, respecting seasonal closures—ensures these pristine waters remain pristine for future generations. Visitors discover diverse marine life: Posidonia seagrass meadows (critical for Mediterranean ecosystems), monk seals in remote grottos, endemic fish species, and protected seabird colonies.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.
