Catamaran Charter from Bonifacio, Corsica

Boat4You operates 9 catamarans from Bonifacio, Corsica's dramatic southern fortress town perched 70 metres above a fjord-like harbour carved into limestone cliffs. Bonifacio is the gateway to the Lavezzi islands' protected marine reserve, the Strait of Bonifacio crossing to Sardinia (8 nautical miles), and some of the Mediterranean's most photogenic and adventurous cruising. For crews seeking dramatic seascapes, multicultural yacht clubs, and access to both Corsica and Sardinia, Bonifacio is incomparable.

Bonifacio Marina and Citadel

The harbour lies beneath the medieval citadel, a limestone fortress rising 70 metres above the waterline, accessible by staircase or narrow streets leading upward through the old town. The main marina basin holds 340 berths with fuel, water, electricity, and chandlery; approach depths exceed 4 metres. The atmosphere is theatrical: limestone cliffs frame the harbour mouth, sailboats nestle below the fortress, and diners spill onto harborside terraces as sunset ignites the walls amber and rose.

Uptown, the citadel's medieval narrow alleys, ramparts, and generational family restaurants—some run by the same families for decades—offer Corsican dining and cultural immersion unmatched elsewhere on the island. Many crews spend first and last nights ashore, exploring the fortress, dining on local Corsican wine and seafood, and marvelling at views across the Strait to Sardinia's granite mountains.

The Lavezzi Islands Marine Reserve

Five to seven nautical miles south of Bonifacio lie the Lavezzi islands, a protected marine reserve of granite and limestone islets, pristine white-sand beaches, and crystalline lagoons of impossible turquoise. Lavezzu and Cavallo are the principal islands; smaller skerries and reefs scatter throughout. The reserve prohibits commercial fishing and anchoring in core zones, but yacht anchoring in designated areas is permitted with mooring fees (12–15 euros per night).

Catamarans are ideal for Lavezzi exploration. Their 1.0–1.3-metre draft allows anchoring in 2–3 metres of water in the shallowest lagoons; many monohulls must anchor outside and tender ashore. The lagoons' flat bottoms and light sand provide excellent holding; a second anchor prevents swing in variable evening winds. Crews typically spend 2–3 nights at Lavezzi, swimming, snorkelling over dusky grouper breeding zones, and exploring islet edges by dinghy. The sunset light on white sand and turquoise water is incomparably Mediterranean.

The Strait of Bonifacio Crossing

Eight nautical miles separate Corsica from Sardinia—a crossing steeped in history, challenge, and reward. Ancient mariners called it the "Mouth of Hell"; modern sailors find it navigable but demanding. Strong tidal streams (up to 1.5 knots), funnelled swell, and ancient wrecks command respect. Yet catamarans handle the passage with confidence. Their shallow draft minimizes the risk of unexpected deep-water currents; twin engines and wide beam provide directional stability and rapid transit (1.5–2 hours under power, 3–4 hours under sail depending on wind).

Plan the crossing for daylight and near-slack tide (tide tables for Bonifacio are essential). Depart Lavezzi at dawn, motor or sail north-east through the Strait, and anchor off Palau, Maddalena, or Porto Cervo (Sardinia's mega-yacht hub) by early afternoon. The return crossing (south-west) is equally straightforward. Many week-long charters split time: 3 days Corsica (Bonifacio–Lavezzi), 3 days Sardinia (Maddalena–Porto Cervo), and 1 day transit.

Sardinia Extensions and Multicultural Cruising

Sardinia's Costa Smeralda, 8 nautical miles south, is dramatically different from Corsica. Porto Cervo is a glittering resort town with mega-yachts, international cuisine, and premium provisioning. The Maddalena archipelago (a national park) offers easier, less-crowded alternatives with good anchoring and stunning granite seascapes. Palau is a working fishing village with authentic character, excellent seafood, and friendly locals.

Crews who cross the Strait often plan 10–12-day charters: 4 days exploring Corsica's south-east coast and Bonifacio, 4 days Lavezzi and Sardinian islands, and 2–4 days returning north via the Calanques de Piana or west coast. This extended itinerary showcases two countries' distinct sailing cultures, landscapes, and provisioning traditions.

Corsica's South-East Coast from Bonifacio

North of Bonifacio, the Côte Rocheuse (rock-lined coast) stretches towards Porto-Vecchio (20 nautical miles) and Solenzara (40 nm). Dramatic granite boulders, narrow coves, and dramatic cliff faces characterize the approach. Named anchorages—Cala di Trana, Cala Gonone, Île de la Pietra—are accessible to shallow-draft catamarans; deeper monohulls must moor outside or proceed northward. These east-coast coves are less crowded than west-coast anchors and offer exceptional snorkelling over Roman amphoras and submerged anchor stocks.

Pianottoli-Caldarello, 15 nautical miles north-west of Bonifacio on the south coast, offers a quieter alternative to Bonifacio's bustling harbour—a small, sheltered haven popular with boutique charters and crews avoiding crowds.

Limestone Caves and Grotto Exploration

Bonifacio's foreshore is riddled with caves and grottoes carved by centuries of wave action. The Grotte du Sdragonato (Dragon's Grotto) is the most famous, accessible by guided boat tour from the old harbour or explored by tender from anchored yachts. Crews with sense of adventure kayak or paddle-board into narrow sea caves, navigating by headtorch and reverberating echo. The limestone formations are extraordinary—arches, columns, and cathedral-like chambers reflecting water light in surreal patterns.

Provisioning and Crew Services

Bonifacio's old-town markets and supermarkets offer abundant provisions: local Corsican wine, charcuterie (lonzu, coppa, prisuttu), fresh fish, and seasonal vegetables. Thursday and Saturday mornings see the main market in the upper town; dedicated provisioning crews arrive early and carry groceries downhill to the marina. Restaurants range from casual harbourside snack bars to fine dining; many crews provision half-meals (antipasti, cheese, wine) and dine aboard, watching sunset ignite the limestone fortress.

The marina offers fuel, water, electricity, Wi-Fi, and laundry. A boatyard handles minor repairs; mechanics familiar with catamaran engines are on call. Chandlery stocks ropes, charts, and spares. Rental cars and taxis connect the harbour to the interior (Bonifacio's only public road access is uphill through the old town).

Winds and Seasonal Patterns

Bonifacio's position at Corsica's southern tip exposes it to stronger winds than northern ports. The libeccio (south-westerly) funnels up from the Strait; the mistral occasionally spills from the north-east. Summer maestrale (north-westerly) is typically light to moderate. May–June and September–October offer ideal conditions: warm water, stable winds, and fewer crowded anchorages. July–August attract Mediterranean cruisers from across Europe; Bonifacio and Lavezzi fill with yachts, prices rise, and finding quiet anchorages demands early arrival or alternative timing.

Winter sailing is possible but exposed; Atlantic fronts funnel through the Gulf of Lion and down the Rhône valley, occasionally reaching Corsica with 25+ knot winds. Spring and autumn charters reward patient planners with Mediterranean serenity and European sophistication.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the Strait of Bonifacio crossing difficult?

Navigable but demanding. Strong tidal streams (up to 1.5 knots), funnelled swell, and ancient wrecks require awareness and respect. Plan for daylight, near-slack tide, and weather above 8 knots of wind. Catamarans handle the passage more comfortably than monohulls. Boat4You provides detailed tide tables, current charts, and optional skipper support for first-time crossers.

Can I moor in Lavezzi's most beautiful lagoons?

Yes, in designated anchorage zones. Mooring fees (12–15 euros per night) support marine reserve management. Anchoring is prohibited in core marine conservation areas, but dinghy exploration is permitted. Local guides and marked mooring fields ensure you anchor safely in pristine turquoise water.

How far is Bonifacio from Sardinia by boat?

Eight nautical miles to Maddalena; 12 nautical miles to Palau; 18 nautical miles to Porto Cervo. Under motor, expect 1.5–3 hours depending on current and sea state. Under sail, 3–5 hours on a typical passage.

Is Bonifacio a good base for families?

Excellent. The harbour is sheltered, provisioning is abundant, and Lavezzi islands offer shallow swimming and snorkelling ideal for children. The fortress and old-town exploration engage older kids. Quiet anchorages abound just 5 nautical miles north and south. Families often base themselves in Bonifacio for 2–3 nights, exploring Lavezzi and the Côte Rocheuse by daysail.

What should I provision before sailing south to Sardinia?

Stock fresh water, fuel, and food for 8–12 hours without resupply. Sardinian villages have markets, but prices are higher than Corsica. Provisioning in Bonifacio—local wine, charcuterie, fresh vegetables—is inexpensive and excellent. Fresh fish is available daily from morning boats at the quayside.
