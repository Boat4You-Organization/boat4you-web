Catamaran Charter from Vodice

Vodice offers something rare in the Adriatic: a perfect balance between the Kornati Islands' isolated anchorages and family-friendly infrastructure. This small coastal town, 12 nautical miles north of Šibenik, sits at the mouth of the Krka River and serves as the gateway for bareboat catamaran charters into one of Europe's most untouched sailing areas. Unlike the monohull sailing yachts that dominate here, a catamaran from Vodice delivers spacious cabins, a level galley with room to cook for groups of 8–10, and the shallow draft needed to explore tight anchorages in the Kornati that deeper-hulled boats must avoid. The wind here is textbook: steady Maestral (10–18 knots from the northwest by afternoon) building from calm mornings into energetic sailing conditions by midday, then fading to glassy evenings perfect for anchor swimming.

Vodice is home to ACI Marina Vodice (modern, full-service) and Marina Tribunj (5 minutes west, smaller, equally well-maintained). Together, they serve 300+ berths and have become the primary base for families and groups seeking a week of sailing without solo-sailor intensity. A catamaran from here lets you spend days exploring the 89 Kornati islands in protected bays, take a half-day detour up the Krka River to see Skradinski Buk (limestone waterfalls), and return each evening to an anchorage so quiet that the only sound is your anchor chain and the splash of a dinghy dipping ashore.

Why a Catamaran Works Perfectly from Vodice

The Kornati Islands demand three things from a charter boat: shallow draft (to anchor in 10–12 meters rather than 20+), spacious deck and cabin (for groups wanting to relax rather than constantly sail), and reliable anchoring gear (because you'll be setting and resetting daily). A catamaran ticks all three boxes. The 1.2–1.4 meter draft allows approach to small coves that monohulls avoid entirely. The wide beam provides enormous deck space — an aft platform perfect for evening drinks and socializing, a forward trampoline ideal for sunbathing, and a large cabin area where kids don't feel claustrophobic during afternoon siesta.

Most Vodice catamarans are 38–45 feet, sleeping 8–10 people across three to four cabins. The galley sits higher than on a monohull, reducing seasickness risk and making cooking less miserable. The engine power (two 30–50 hp motors) means you can hold position in the afternoon Maestral without constant anchor resetting. Evening sailing is gentle — full-battened mains and genoas power a catamaran efficiently in the 12–16 knot winds that prevail at sunset. Many crews find a catamaran from Vodice feels like a floating villa: spacious enough to avoid overcrowding, reliable enough to trust your safety, and simple enough that even bareboat newcomers (with proper credentials) can manage it comfortably.

Best Routes from Vodice for Catamaran Charter

The Classic Kornati Loop (5–7 days)

Most Vodice catamaran charters spend the bulk of the week anchored in the Kornati National Park. The typical arc begins with departure from Vodice heading south through Murter Channel into the archipelago's northern reaches. The first day's passage is short (8–12 nm), arriving at an opening anchorage like Marimore or Tušnjare by late afternoon. Days two through five explore deeper park anchorages: Telašćica Bay (on Dugi Otok, famous for dramatic cliffs), Mula (shallow, surrounded by uninhabited islands), and smaller bays bearing names like Žut, Rava, and Krakan. These bays rarely hold more than 5–10 anchored yachts in peak summer (compared to 50+ boats at popular Mediterranean spots); the experience feels genuinely remote.

A catamaran's stability means you can spend entire days at anchor without resetting, even if the afternoon Maestral builds to 18 knots — the boat rests level, and you're comfortable napping, reading, or swimming. Monohulls heel at anchor in wind; catamarans sit upright, beam-on, swinging freely. The difference in comfort is profound, especially for families with children or anyone prone to motion sensitivity.

By day six, you'll likely return northward, either cruising back to Vodice directly or taking a day-sail detour up the Krka River (if draft and energy permit) before a final evening anchorage. Return to Vodice on day seven, arriving by early evening.

Krka River and Waterfall Day Excursion (included in most weeks)

The Krka River flows inland from Vodice for 12 nautical miles to Skradin, where limestone canyons narrow and Skradinski Buk — three successive waterfalls — create a natural spectacle. A catamaran can navigate the river (draft-dependent; confirm with your charter company), though many crews anchor securely in Vodice and hire a local water taxi or guide for a day-trip to see the falls and explore Skradin village (a UNESCO-listed heritage site on the river itself). The falls are framed by national park boundaries; admission is €15–20 per person. This excursion breaks up a week of island sailing and offers a dramatic change of scenery — limestone canyons instead of open sea, fresh water instead of salt, the sound of cascading water instead of wind and waves. It's particularly memorable in May and June when snowmelt from the Dinaric Alps increases water flow, making the falls dramatically powerful.

Šibenik Town and Southern Islands (3–5 day variation)

Instead of or in addition to the Kornati, catamaran charters can explore south toward Šibenik (12 nm from Vodice), a medieval fortified town whose cathedral is a UNESCO World Heritage site. The harbor entrance requires careful navigation due to narrow channels, but once inside, you're anchored in history. South of Šibenik, smaller islands like Prvić, Zlarin, and the Žirje group offer quieter anchorages and fewer competing boats. This route sacrifices Kornati isolation for cultural richness and better provisioning access — important if your group is sensitive to limited supplies.

Fleet Types from Vodice

38–42 foot catamarans (6–8 berths)

Entry-level catamarans, typically with two to three cabins. Cabin space is tighter; the galley is functional but compact. These work well for couples, small families, or groups that don't mind proximity. Bareboat pricing: €4,000–6,500 per week, depending on season. These boats are reliable and economical but less spacious than larger alternatives.

42–45 foot catamarans (8–10 berths)

The sweet spot for Vodice family charters. Three to four cabins (six to eight of them with private heads), spacious galley, and significant deck area. The on-deck living is where families spend most of their time: forward trampoline for sunbathing, aft platform for evening gatherings, and a flybridge (on some models) offering 360-degree views. Bareboat pricing: €5,000–8,500 per week, depending on season and boat condition. Skippered or crewed options add €150–300 per day.

Larger catamarans (45–50 feet, 10–12 berths)

Premium vessels with four to five cabins, dual heads/showers, and significant galley and living space. Less common from Vodice (more typical of Split-based charters), but available through larger charter companies. Pricing: €8,000–12,000+ per week. These suit large family groups or corporate getaways.

Practical Details: Season, Wind, and Licensing

Licensing and bareboat requirements

Catamaran bareboat from Vodice requires an International Sailing Certificate (ASA 104 or equivalent) or proof of sailing experience. Most European sailors hold IYT or RYA certificates; US sailors typically have ASA cards. If you lack credentials, hire a skipper (€150–300 per day) or take an ASA 104 course beforehand. Boat4You explains bareboat eligibility and certification pathways. Many groups hire a skipper even with credentials, simply to focus on relaxation rather than navigation; the cost is worth the peace of mind.

Weather and wind patterns

The Maestral (northwest wind) dominates May through October, typically building from 8 knots in late morning to 15–18 knots by 2–3 pm. In the Kornati's narrow channels between islands, gusts can reach 20–22 knots, but the shallow, protected bays provide reliable anchoring. Morning sailing is often light-air ghosting; afternoon sailing energetic and real. Bora (northeast, 20–40 knots) is rare in summer but can arrive in April, May, or October. It hits suddenly, blows hard for 12–36 hours, and departs. Most skippers will anchor securely and wait it out; a week-long charter has time flexibility.

Best season: June and September. Warm water (22–24°C), consistent Maestral, manageable crowds, and comfortable air temperatures. July–August are hottest and busiest; book by March. May and October are cooler but still excellent; Bora risk increases slightly. November–March requires experienced crews and flexibility.

Provisioning and supplies

Vodice has a Konzum supermarket and local bakeries. Before departing for the Kornati (where the islands have no shops), provision heavily: bread, fresh vegetables, cheese, wine, and protein (tinned fish, cured meat). Budget €250–350 for a week of groceries. Most catamaran crews find that simple cooking aboard (pasta, grilled meat or fish, daily bread) suits the lazy rhythm of island anchoring better than elaborate meals. Eating ashore occasionally — when the catamaran returns to Vodice or when exploring a village konoba — breaks up the routine. A catamaran's spacious galley, unlike a monohull's cramped quarters, makes self-catering actually enjoyable.

Pricing and What to Expect

A 40-foot catamaran (three to four cabins) from Vodice costs roughly €4,500–8,000 per week depending on season and boat condition. Shoulder months (May, June, early September) run €4,500–6,000. Peak summer (July–August) climbs to €6,500–8,000. April and October are cheaper (€4,000–5,500). Winter is unpredictable and less suitable for bareboat newcomers. Adding a skipper costs €150–300 per day (€1,050–2,100 per week). Fuel typically costs €200–300 for a week of mixed sailing and motoring. Kornati National Park permits add €300 per week or €30 per day. Marina fees in Vodice are €25–35 per night; if you navigate inland to Skradin (Krka River), expect €20–30 per night. Provisioning runs €250–400 per week.

Sample Itinerary: Seven Days from Vodice

Day 1 (Monday): Depart Vodice by 10 am after briefing and provisioning. Motor-sail south through Murter Channel into the Kornati. Afternoon Maestral builds; you'll trim main and genoa and sail the final miles. Arrive at Marimore or Tušnjare by 4 pm, drop anchor in sand (holding is excellent), and settle in. Dinner aboard — fresh bread from Vodice, local cheese, cured meat, and a bottle of Babić wine from the Šibenik hinterland.

Day 2 (Tuesday): Short sail to Mula or Opat (4–6 nm). Morning sailing is light; afternoon Maestral energizes the day. Anchor in shallow turquoise water (10–12 meters). Spend the afternoon and evening swimming, snorkeling (small fish dart around the anchor chain), and reading on deck. Dinner aboard or simple grilled fish heated on the stove. Water is so clear you can watch fish beneath the boat.

Day 3 (Wednesday): Longer passage to Telašćica Bay, Dugi Otok (8–10 nm). This is a real sailing day — the Maestral builds, the catamaran heels gently, and the navigation demands attention (tight channels between islands, but marked and navigable). Telašćica's dramatic limestone cliffs frame your arrival. Anchor in the deepest part of the bay, explore the shore by dinghy, and watch the sunset paint the cliffs orange. Pašticada (beef braised slowly with prunes and red wine) reheats beautifully aboard; serve with wine and fresh bread.

Day 4 (Thursday): Day sailing and exploration within Telašćica's multiple anchorages, or a sail to quieter Žut, Rava, or Krakan islands. Mornings are calm; you can swim and read without constant anchor worry. Afternoon sailing, as the Maestral builds, is the day's highlight. Dinner on deck as the wind drops — pasta with local olive oil and tinned fish, or grilled calamari if you picked any up in Vodice.

Day 5 (Friday): Leisurely exploration or a longer sail back toward Vodice (start early for midday arrival, or anchor halfway for an overnight). Consider a detour: if your skipper is confident and your draft is shallow (typically 1.2–1.4 m), motor up the Krka River to Skradin (5 nm inland, a 1-hour passage). Anchoring in the river, surrounded by limestone canyons and medieval town, is surreal. Visit Skradinski Buk waterfall by water taxi or hired guide. Return downriver by evening.

Day 6 (Saturday): If you stayed inland on Day 5, return to open water on Day 6. Or, continue exploring southern Kornati islands (Žirje, Klint, Svetac) if time and weather permit. Swimming and snorkeling take up most of the day. Evening at anchor, dinner aboard, early sleep before a full day tomorrow.

Day 7 (Sunday): Morning sail back to Vodice (8–15 nm depending on current anchorage). Light Maestral, relaxed pace, arrival by early afternoon. Return the catamaran, collect your deposit, and head to a harborside konoba for a farewell meal ashore — gregada or brodet (fish stew), Plavac Mali wine, and conversation about the week just completed.

Getting to Vodice

Zadar airport (50 km north, 1 hour drive) or Split airport (80 km south, 1.5 hours drive) serve Vodice. A rental car or prearranged taxi reaches the marina in 60–90 minutes. Most charter companies offer transfer arrangements; confirm at booking. The marina staff will brief you on the catamaran, check safety equipment, and assist with provisioning.

Local Flavor: Dalmatian Food and Wine

Dalmatian cuisine reflects its maritime and highland heritage. Gregada — a fisherman's stew of mixed fish, tomato, white wine, and garlic — appears at nearly every waterfront konoba. It's humble, authentic, and perfect for reheating aboard: buy it from a konoba in Vodice, store in a container, and heat it for dinner while anchored in the Kornati. Brodet is similar but made with red wine and specific fish like scorpion fish and monkfish. Pašticada (beef braised with tomato, prunes, and red wine, served with gnocchi) is the meat tradition — slow-cooked comfort that improves as it sits.

Babić (deep red, earthy, from the Šibenik area) is the regional wine that pairs with both fish stew and meat. Plavac Mali (fuller, deep red) is equally good and slightly more common at restaurants and markets. Pošip (bright white, crisp, from nearby Korčula) is the seafood pairing — ideal chilled aboard as an evening aperitif. Buy a bottle at Vodice's supermarket; it costs €4–7 and tastes better sipped at anchor in absolute stillness than it would in any restaurant.

FAQ

Do I need a sailing license to bareboat charter a catamaran from Vodice?

Yes, most operators require an International Sailing Certificate (ASA 104 or equivalent). If you lack one, hire a skipper (€150–300 per day) or take an ASA 104 course beforehand. Many groups hire a skipper even with credentials, prioritizing relaxation over boat handling. Boat4You's how-we-work section explains certification and licensing pathways.

Is a catamaran better than a sailing yacht for families with children?

Yes, typically. A catamaran's spacious deck, level cabin, and stable platform (even in wind) suit families with children aged 6+. The deck offers room for kids to play safely; the galley is large enough for cooking without stress; and the stability means less motion sickness risk. A sailing yacht's narrower berths and heeling motion can be uncomfortable for young children or non-sailors.

What is the best time to charter a catamaran from Vodice?

June and September offer the most consistent Maestral, warm water (22–24°C), and manageable crowds. May and October are cooler but still excellent; Bora risk is slightly higher. July–August are hottest and busiest; book by March. Avoid November–March unless you're an experienced crew comfortable with unpredictability.

How much wind is typical in the Kornati?

The Maestral typically builds from 8–10 knots in late morning to 15–18 knots by early afternoon. Gusts to 20–22 knots are possible but rare in summer. Wind drops away by sunset, leaving most anchorages calm by evening. This rhythm — calm morning, energetic afternoon, peaceful evening — is what makes Kornati sailing exceptional.

Can I take a catamaran up the Krka River?

Possibly, if your draft is 1.3 meters or less and your beam is under 13 meters (most 40-foot catamarans qualify). The river is navigable for 12 nm to Skradin, but confirmation is essential before departure. Bridges, shallow sections, and local traffic require careful attention. Many crews choose to anchor in Vodice and take a day-trip to Skradinski Buk by hired boat instead — simpler and equally rewarding.

What should I provision for a week in the Kornati?

Buy bread, fresh vegetables, cheese, eggs, and protein (tinned fish, cured meat) from Vodice before departing. Stock wine, olive oil, and pasta heavily — they're the backbone of onboard meals. Budget €250–350 for a week of simple self-catering. Most crews eat simply and well aboard: daily fresh bread and cheese for breakfast, cold lunch from yesterday's leftovers, and grilled meat or fish for dinner. Eating ashore occasionally breaks the pattern and provides access to specialized items you can't provision aboard.

Can I charter a catamaran with a skipper from Vodice?

Yes. Skipper rates are €150–300 per day. A professional skipper handles navigation and boat operations while you enjoy the Adriatic, explore the islands, and relax with family or friends. This is especially popular with multi-generation groups, families with young children, or anyone wanting the security of an experienced hand on watch overnight.
