Catamaran Charter in Athens

Why Choose a Catamaran from Athens?

Athens is Greece's premier gateway for catamaran charters. The city sits just 45 minutes from Alimos Marina—one of the Mediterranean's largest multihull bases—making it the ideal departure point for families and groups seeking stability, space, and comfort. Boat4You offers over 195 catamarans in the Athens and Saronic Gulf region, ranging from bareboat cruisers to crewed luxury cats ready for week-long Cyclades expeditions.

Catamarans are perfect for Greek sailing: their shallow draft allows access to isolated anchorages in the Saronic Gulf, their wide beam creates ample deck and cabin space, and their fuel-efficient twin engines provide both security and range. Whether you're planning a family week or a multi-generational adventure, a catamaran from Athens delivers the stability, amenities, and freedom to explore Greece's most celebrated islands.

Top Routes and Day Sails from Athens

The Saronic Gulf—stretching southeast from Athens toward the Peloponnese—offers protected, island-rich cruising perfect for catamarans. A classic three-day loop reaches Aegina, Poros, and Hydra, each with Venetian charm, whitewashed villages, and excellent tavernas. The passage from Athens to Aegina takes just 4–6 hours, making it ideal for a short break or a full week's itinerary.

For longer charters, catamarans from Athens regularly push south toward the Cyclades, entering the Cycladic cluster via the scenic island passage. The islands of Kea and Kythnos—less crowded than Mykonos or Santorini—offer excellent shelter and authentic Greek hospitality. Many week-long charters follow the Saronic loop, then venture toward the southern Cyclades, returning to Athens with time for provisioning and final wind-down.

Marinas and Logistics in Athens

Alimos Marina, 8 km southeast of central Athens, is the charter hub. Over 900 berths accommodate everything from dinghies to 50-metre superyachts, and the facility offers full rigging services, provisioning, and chandleries. Athens International Airport lies 45 minutes north; a taxi or bus connection gets you to Alimos in under an hour. Piraeus, the adjacent commercial ferry port, is best avoided for charters—it's crowded with commercial traffic—but Lavrion Marina, 50 km southeast of Athens, offers an alternative deep-water base for larger vessels.

Boat4You charters are based primarily at Alimos. Upon arrival, your Boat4You check-in team will brief you on local regulations, meltemi wind patterns (critical July–August), and radio protocols. Supermarkets, fuel stations, and laundry facilities cluster around the marina; many charterers stock provisions the day before departure.

Seasonal Sailing and Weather

Athens' sailing season runs April through October. May–June and September–October offer ideal conditions: steady, moderate breezes (10–14 knots) and water temperatures between 20–24°C. July and August bring the notorious meltemi wind—a dry, northerly flow that can gust 20–30 knots daily, particularly in the open Cycladic passages. Experienced sailors relish the meltemi; families often prefer early or late season for gentler conditions.

Winter sailing (November–March) is possible but rare; many catamarans are hauled for maintenance. Spring charters enjoy green hillsides and wildflowers; autumn brings flat seas and an intimate atmosphere once tourist crowds thin. Plan your Boat4You catamaran dates accordingly, and typically ask your booking team about typical wind and swell for your chosen week.

Provisioning and Local Food

Saronic Gulf islands—Aegina, Poros, Hydra—are famous for local fare. Aegina's pistachio industry dominates the island; seek out family-run nut shops and bakeries. Poros offers excellent fish tavernas along the waterfront; the narrow strait between Poros and the mainland creates a picturesque dining backdrop. Hydra, car-free and steeped in Bohemian history, has become a foodie haven with creative Mediterranean menus.

Stock main provisions in Athens (Alimos has two supermarkets) before departure, then top up fresh produce, fish, and local wine at island ports. Greek island farmers' markets (typically mornings) offer seasonal tomatoes, feta, olives, and herbs at excellent value. Many charter companies offer welcome baskets with Greek wine, cheese, and olives—ask Boat4You about this when booking.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a sailing licence to charter a catamaran from Athens?

Most bareboat catamarans from our fleet require proof of competence—typically an ICC (International Certificate of Competence) or equivalent national qualification. If you lack certification, consider a crewed charter or a skippered catamaran; our team can advise during booking.

What is the best month for a family catamaran charter in Athens?

May, June, and September offer mild winds, warm seas, and fewer tourists. July–August are hotter and windier (meltemi), but perfect for experienced crews. Avoid November–March for family holidays unless you're an experienced cold-weather sailor.

How much does it cost to charter a catamaran in Athens?

Prices vary by season, boat size, and crewing. A 40-foot bareboat typically runs €2,500–4,500 per week in shoulder season, rising 30–50% in summer. Crewed charters start around €5,000–8,000 weekly.

Can I skip ports and explore remote anchorages in a catamaran?

Absolutely. One of the catamaran's strengths is shallow draft—you can anchor in crystal bays that larger yachts cannot access. The Saronic Gulf has hundreds of named anchorages; popular spots include Doukato (a scenic uninhabited isle), Porto Tolo, and various mooring fields in the Cyclades.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.
