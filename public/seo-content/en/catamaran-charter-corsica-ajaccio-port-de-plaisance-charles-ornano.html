Port de Plaisance Charles Ornano: Facilities and Access

Charles Ornano is Corsica's flagship marina, built to modern European standards with 830 deep-water berths, fuel docks, shore power (230V and 400V), fresh water, and a well-stocked chandlery. The reception hall offers showers, laundry, and Wi-Fi; a boatyard hauls vessels for maintenance. Approach depths exceed 3.5 metres even at low tide, welcoming catamarans with shallow 1.0–1.3-metre drafts. Mooring is straightforward; the port authority assigns berths upon arrival, typically close to the fuelling station for immediate provisioning.

The airport is a 15-minute taxi ride (10–15 euros); many crews fly in the morning, pick up a hire car or rental catamaran by early afternoon, and depart Ajaccio by sunset to maximize their first sailing day. The marina's location on the city's western edge means you're 10 minutes by foot from provisioning (supermarket Carrefour, fishmonger, bakeries) and 20 minutes' walk from the old town, where Napoleon's boyhood home and atmospheric cafés reward evening exploration.

Corsica's West Coast: From Ajaccio Northward

From Ajaccio, the classic Corsican cruise unfolds northward. The Îles Sanguinaires, 6 nautical miles west of the marina, anchor crews for their first night. These red-granite isles, home to a lighthouse and legend-soaked history, offer dramatic anchorages and snorkelling. Return to Ajaccio at dawn or push north towards the Calanques de Piana (20 nautical miles)—UNESCO limestone formations rising 300 metres from mirror water, accessible only by sea and traversable by tender or dinghy.

Calvi lies 50 nautical miles north-west of Ajaccio, a classic 10-hour passage under sail or 5–6 hours by motor. The citadel-guarded port is atmospheric and historically rich; nearby, the Scandola Nature Reserve (UNESCO) offers extraordinary granite and porphyry cliffs, marine caves, and protected waters. A week-long circuit (Ajaccio–Îles Sanguinaires–Calanques–Calvi–Scandola–return) showcases Corsica's west-coast jewels while building crew confidence in catamaran handling.

Catamaran Advantages in Ajaccio Waters

Multihulls excel in Ajaccio's operational environment. The shallow coves and rock-lined bays north of the city demand draft discipline—catamarans at 1.0–1.3 metres access lagoons where keeled monohulls ground. The Îles Sanguinaires' approach narrows through a rock-marked channel; twin-hulled vessels track true and provide instant shallow-water alarms. The Calanques de Piana's inner anchorages require precise positioning in confined space; catamarans' wide beam and dual-engine manoeuvrability enable confident anchoring in 2–3 metres surrounded by 300-metre cliffs.

Stability is paramount. Ajaccio's summer libeccio (south-westerly breeze) often builds through the day, occasionally reaching 15–20 knots. A catamaran's flat-bottomed hull and wide beam provide excellent motion control; families and mixed crews stay comfortable while experienced sailors enjoy exhilarating beam reaches. Deck space is generous, so children roam safely while parents supervise from the helm or saloon.

Essential Anchorages and Provisioning

Ajaccio town harbour sits immediately north of Charles Ornano marina, offering old-town atmosphere and provisioning. The main market (Wednesday and Saturday mornings) bursts with local produce, Corsican wine (Patrimonio reds), and charcuterie. Carrefour supermarket, fish stalls, and bakeries line the waterfront.

Île Rousse, 35 nautical miles north-west, is a secondary provisioning port with excellent facilities and a sandy beach suitable for families. Calvi, further north, provides gourmet provisioning and evening atmosphere. South of Ajaccio, Propriano and Porto-Vecchio offer sheltered east-coast alternatives for longer or multi-week charters.

Day-Sail Routes from Ajaccio

Skippers new to Corsica often plan 5–8 nautical-mile daysails, building confidence before longer passages. Ajaccio–Îles Sanguinaires (6 nm, 1.5 hours) is a textbook first run: protected approach, scenic anchorage, and quick return to port. From Sanguinaires, push north towards the Calanques (14 nm, 3.5 hours) for dramatic lunch-stop anchoring. Seasoned crews tackle the Calvi passage (50 nm, 10 hours under sail) in a single leg, timing departure for dawn and reaching Calvi by sunset.

Shorter loops—Ajaccio to Propriano (30 nm east-coast circuit via the Îles Lavezzi) or Ajaccio to Île Rousse and back (70 nm, 3-day triangle)—suit variable-weather charters when open-water conditions shift. Napoleon and Corsican Culture

Ajaccio, birthplace of Napoleon Bonaparte, pulses with historical intrigue. His boyhood home (Maison Bonaparte) opens to visitors; the cathedral where he was baptized anchors the old town. Evening exploration yields atmospheric narrow streets, family-run restaurants serving Corsican cassoulet and seafood, and piazza gatherings where locals and visitors mingle over pastis and charcuterie. Crews who sleep aboard typically venture ashore for dinner, returning to their catamaran by 11 p.m.—a rhythm that blends sailing with cultural immersion.

Corsican wine (Patrimonio, Vin de Corse appellations) and local charcuterie (lonzu, coppa, prisuttu) are inexpensive and exceptional. Markets offer fresh mozzarella, anchovies, and local cheeses. This gastronomic richness makes Ajaccio an ideal base for week-long charters where crew provisioning and celebratory dinners enhance the sailing adventure.

Seasonal Patterns and Weather

May through October is the prime season. June and September offer ideal conditions: stable winds (maestrale light to moderate from the north-west), warm water (22–26°C), and fewer crowded anchorages than July–August. Spring (May) occasionally sees mistral spillover from the north, but by late May, conditions settle. Early July brings build-up; August is hot and crowded; September returns stability and magical light.

The libeccio (south-westerly) dominates late summer, accelerating to 15–20 knots by afternoon on many days. Experienced crews relish the challenge; families and novices might plan shorter passages or sheltered daysails when the breeze builds. Frequently Asked Questions

How far is the airport from Charles Ornano marina?

Ajaccio Airport (Campo dell'Oro) is 5 kilometres from the marina—a 15-minute taxi or rental car drive. Many crews arrive mid-morning, collect their catamaran by early afternoon, and depart by sunset on their first day.

Can I walk to provisioning from the marina?

Yes. Carrefour supermarket, fishmongers, bakeries, and the main market (Wednesday and Saturday mornings) are within 10 minutes' walk. The old town and restaurants are 20 minutes on foot. Many crews provision immediately after collecting their boat, then depart for anchorages north or west.

What is the depth at Charles Ornano?

The marina approach and berths maintain 3.5+ metres at low tide, welcoming shallow-draft catamarans. Our fleet draws 1.0–1.3 metres, so mooring depth is rarely a concern. Fuel dock and water berth are alongside; you'll find ample swing room in the anchorage if you moor outside the basin.

How crowded is Ajaccio in July and August?

Very. July and August attract thousands of French and Italian charterers; anchorages north of Ajaccio (Sanguinaires, Calanques) fill by 3 p.m. June and September offer perfect conditions with 40% fewer boats. May and October are quieter still, though wind patterns are slightly less predictable.

Is the Calanques de Piana accessible for first-time charterers?

Yes, with care. The approach is straightforward; the dramatic scenery is worth every nautical mile. Local pilots and detailed chart books (French Pilot or British Admiralty Mediterranean Pilot) guide safe anchoring. recommends a skipper brief before your first visit, or hire a local captain (200–300 euros per day) for the initial exploration.
