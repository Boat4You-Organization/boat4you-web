Monohull Sailing Routes from Le Marin

The classic northward passage to St Lucia (25 nm, 5-6 hours) is a textbook reach under trade wind conditions. From St Lucia, continued northing toward Dominica (40 nm) can be a close reach or even beat, depending on the day's wind. Experienced monohull sailors relish the challenge of these passages, reading the sea state and adjusting canvas for maximum efficiency.

An alternative circuit heads south toward the Grenadines: Bequia (40 nm), Canouan (70 nm), and Union (90 nm). These passages are predominantly downwind or reaching, ideal for cruising under sail and enjoying the rhythm of wave and canvas. Many monohull charterers spend 10-14 days sailing this circuit, moving gently from island to island and fully absorbing the Caribbean sailing experience.

For experienced hands, the windward passage to Barbados (120 nm) offers a challenging offshore passage with significant distance and open ocean time—perfect for sailors wanting to prove their seamanship.

Sailing Season and Weather Patterns

The Caribbean cruising season runs November through April. Trade winds average 12-20 knots, with February-March typically the strongest. December and April are slightly lighter but still reliable. For monohull sailors, these conditions are ideal: enough wind for responsive sailing, but not so much as to require reef management constantly.

From Le Marin, the northeast trade wind is remarkably consistent. Morning departures with favorable slant often allow day sails of 20-40 nautical miles by evening. Night sailing is safe and common, with clear skies and visible celestial navigation aids.

Off-season sailing (May–October) is possible but requires weather vigilance and flexibility. Summer squalls are common, though rarely dangerous. Insurance and availability are better, and prices drop substantially.

Island Stops and Sailing Culture

Each island offers distinct character. St Lucia's Pitons are magnificent under sail, with anchorages suitable for monohulls. Dominica's anchorages are wilder, better suited to experienced crews. The Grenadines are idyllic for cruising sailors: small communities, protective reefs, gentle holding ground, and sailing yachts are the norm—not motorboats or mega-catamarans.

Stop at Bequia for Admiralty Bay and the Princess Margaret Beach. Canouan's anchorages are pristine. Salt Whistle Bay in Mayreau is legendary for its beauty and the simple beach bars. Union Island offers a bustling anchorage and provisioning, a last resupply before longer passages.

Navigation and Safety

Weather routing is available through various services, and the Caribbean is well-charted and straightforward to navigate. Overnight passages are common and safe, with reliable sailing winds and clear visibility on most nights.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need professional sailing qualifications?

Bareboat monohull charters typically require proof of sailing experience: a recognized qualification (RYA, ASA) or captain's reference. can recommend experienced skippers to hire as crew if your group lacks formal credentials. Professional crew elevates both safety and enjoyment.

What's the difference between a monohull and catamaran charter?

Monohulls heel on wind (heeling angle 15-25 degrees), require active sailing, and respond sensitively to trim. Catamarans sit level, are more forgiving, and require less attention. Monohull sailing is more engaging and rewarding for experienced sailors; catamarans suit relaxation-focused groups and first-time charterers.

Can we sail at night?

Yes, safely. The Caribbean offers excellent conditions for night sailing: clear skies, visible stars, consistent winds, and well-charted waters. Watchkeeping requires at least two experienced sailors. Many crews sail at night to arrive at popular anchorages early, securing the best spots.

How much sailing experience do we really need?

A week bareboat monohull charter benefits from at least 100-200 hours of sailing time and experience handling the vessel in various wind conditions. If your group has less experience, hire a professional skipper for €100-200 per day—well worth it for safety, efficiency, and learning.
