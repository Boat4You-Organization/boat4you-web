Key Coastal Towns and Anchorages

Benidorm stands as the region's largest resort hub, famous for its distinctive skyline of high-rise hotels overlooking crescent beaches. Many charterers use Benidorm as a provisioning stop—excellent supermarkets, fresh fish markets, and lively restaurants make it ideal for restocking provisions. Just 15 nautical miles south, Altea captivates with whitewashed architecture, narrow cobbled streets, and a picturesque old town perched above the harbor. The levante wind (strong easterly) often fills these waters, creating excellent sailing conditions for experienced crews.

Dénia, lying 20 nautical miles north, serves as a crucial gateway to the Balearic Islands and ferry point to Ibiza. This elegant harbor town boasts modern marina facilities and a thriving seafood culture; local caldos (fish stews) and fideuà (noodle-based seafood dish) are specialties. Calpe, with its iconic Peñón d'Ifach rock formation rising 332 meters from the shore, sits 18 miles southeast and provides safe anchorages and excellent provisioning. Santa Pola and Torrevieja further south offer quieter alternatives for those seeking less crowded surroundings.

Tabarca Island and Day-Sailing Routes

Just 12 nautical miles from Alicante city, Tabarca Island is a protected marine reserve and must-visit destination for Boat4You charters. This uninhabited rocky island offers crystal-clear waters ideal for snorkeling and swimming. A typical day sail from Benidorm to Tabarca and back (approximately 24 nautical miles round-trip) takes 4–5 hours depending on sea state, leaving ample time for lunch at anchor and underwater exploration. The shallow reefs surrounding Tabarca teem with Mediterranean fish species.

A classic 7-day itinerary from Alicante might read: Benidorm (Days 1–2), Altea (Day 3), Dénia (Days 4–5), Calpe (Day 6), and return via Tabarca (Day 7). This 120-nautical-mile loop showcases the region's diversity—beaches, cliffs, mountain-backed harbors, and protected marine areas. Winds typically blow from the northeast (levante) or occasionally from the southwest (solano), making for predictable sailing conditions.

Local Cuisine and Provisioning

The Costa Blanca's culinary tradition centers on seafood. Paella, the iconic Spanish rice dish, originated inland but thrives here with seafood variants—paella de marisco features prawns, mussels, and fish. Arròs a banda (rice cooked in fish broth) and all i pebre (eel and potato stew) are local favorites. Fresh fish markets in every port village deliver exceptional quality at reasonable prices. Don't miss fideuà, a noodle-based alternative to paella found throughout the region, or caldillo—a traditional fish and potato broth beloved by local fishing families.

Provisioning is straightforward across the region. Supermarkets like Carrefour and Mercadona operate throughout Benidorm, Dénia, Calpe, and Alicante. Local fish markets offer the freshest catch; visit early morning for optimal selection. Wine enthusiasts appreciate the nearby Jumilla and Yecla wine regions inland—many charterers arrange day trips or stock Spanish wines aboard.

Wind Patterns and Best Seasons

The levante wind dominates spring and early summer, blowing from the northeast with consistent force 3–5 gusts. This reliable wind pattern attracts sailors worldwide and creates predictable sailing conditions. Summer (July–August) occasionally experiences calmer conditions interspersed with sudden levante squalls. Autumn (September–October) brings more variable winds but fewer crowds. Winter charters (November–February) remain possible but require robust crews; the occasional temporal (powerful storm) can strike suddenly.

Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer ideal conditions: warm water, manageable wind, and moderate crowd levels. These shoulder seasons command premium pricing but deliver unmatched comfort for leisure charterers. FAQ – Alicante and Costa Blanca Sailing

What is the best time to sail in the Alicante region?

Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer optimal conditions: reliable levante winds, warm water (22–24°C), moderate crowds, and stable weather. Summer (June–August) delivers warmth and reliable sunshine but can feel crowded. Winter sailing is possible but requires experienced crews prepared for occasional storms.

How far is Tabarca Island from Benidorm?

Tabarca lies approximately 12 nautical miles southeast of Benidorm—a leisurely 2–3 hour sail depending on wind conditions. The island's protected marine reserve status has preserved exceptional snorkeling and diving. Day-trip excursions from any Costa Blanca base remain popular.

What are typical overnight anchorages in the region?
