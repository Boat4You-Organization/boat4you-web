Classic Saronic Gulf Itineraries

The Saronic Gulf is the iconic bareboat sailing destination. Depart Alimos and sail north-west to Aegina (15 nm, 3 hours), where Perdika Harbour and the Temple of Aphaia await. Continue to Poros (28 nm), famous for its lemon orchards and the narrow Limen Channel separating Poros from the Peloponnese—a thrilling day-sail punctuated by delicious lunches at waterfront tavernas.

Hydra (40 nm) is unmissable: this carless island is a maze of stone mansions cascading to a natural harbour. Overnight here and explore on foot. The final leg to Spetses (55 nm from Alimos) takes you past rocky islets and into a harbour that hosted the independence-era Greek navy. A classic one-week loop covers all four islands plus smaller anchorages like Dokos, Patroclus, and Fourni—reliable no two days are the same.

Cyclades Crossings and Multi-Week Adventures

Experienced sailors often extend beyond the Saronic, venturing east to the Cyclades. The passage from Alimos to Mykonos (90 nm) typically takes 18–24 hours, depending on wind and tide. Once in the Cyclades, you have endless possibilities: visit the archaeological wonders of Delos, explore Paros's marble quarries and kitesurfing beaches, or cruise to remote Antiparos for cave exploring and anchorages that feel untouched by time.

Two-week charters from Alimos might combine a Saronic week with a Cyclades dash, or focus entirely on the Cyclades archipelago. Summer wind patterns favour eastward crossings in early June and late August; midsummer can bring erratic conditions. Boat4You recommends consulting with your charter company about seasonal timing and optimal routing for your skill level.

Provisioning and Eating Well in the Saronic

The Kalamaki suburb surrounding Alimos is a provisioning paradise. Lidl and other supermarkets stock Greek staples (feta, olives, ouzo, wines) alongside international products. Pre-ordering through your charter company saves morning hassle; your yacht can be fully provisioned and ready to sail within hours of arrival.

Tavernas dot the marina waterfront and nearby beaches. Try local fish at Kalamaki's working harbourside establishments; the catch-of-the-day is fresher than anywhere else. Once at sea, anchor in protected bays like Perdika or Hydra Harbour, where fishermen-turned-restaurateurs serve simple, exquisite meals. Island provisioning at Poros, Hydra, and Spetses is reliable; fresh produce, local cheeses, and regional wines enhance onboard meals.

Winds, Weather, and Sailing Seasons

Summer (June–September) brings the Etesian wind pattern: consistent northerly winds of 12–18 knots, perfect for sailing into the Saronic and Cyclades. Skies are clear, water temperatures are 25–28°C, and weather windows are reliable. The trade-off: crowded anchorages and peak-season prices.

Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer gentler winds, fewer boats, and lower costs. Water is warm enough for swimming (20–24°C). Late April through early June and late September through mid-October are ideal: wind is steady, weather is benign, and you'll find peaceful coves and attentive tavernas. Winter cruising (November–March) is possible but brings variable weather and occasional gales; only experienced skippers should venture out.

Bareboat Certification and Insurance

Most bareboat charters from Alimos require the skipper to hold an IYT Competent Crew certificate, RYA Day Skipper, or equivalent national qualification. Some operators conduct practical assessments for sailors with substantial experience but no formal credentials. Insurance is mandatory: loss-of-deposit cover (typically €800–2,000) protects both you and the charter company against accidental damage.

FAQ

What sailing experience do I need for a bareboat charter?

A minimum of 2+ years coastal sailing experience and a valid skipper's certification (Competent Crew or equivalent). If you're uncertain, many operators offer skippered (crewed) options or shorter coastal courses to build confidence. Boat4You helps match your experience level to the right vessel and charter type.

How much does a one-week bareboat charter cost?

A 40-foot sailing yacht in summer costs €1,000–1,800 per week; 45-footers, €1,500–2,500. Shoulder seasons (spring, autumn) are 20–30% cheaper. Fuel, food, mooring fees at islands, and end-of-charter cleaning are typically separate. Boat4You's search tool displays exact pricing by date and vessel.

Can I overnight at Alimos before and after my charter?

Yes. The charter company can arrange pre- or post-charter mooring at Alimos for €30–50 per night. This is useful if your flight arrives in the evening or departs early; it allows you to settle in, check the yacht, and depart fresh. Many skippers spend a night provisioning and plotting their route.

What happens if I encounter bad weather during my charter?

Safe harbours abound in the Saronic—Poros, Hydra, and Spetses all offer excellent shelter. Most bareboat charters include a weather routing briefing; the charter company provides daily forecasts and can advise on safe havens. Many operators have a flexible re-booking policy: if severe weather forces cancellation, you can reschedule for the next available week at no additional cost (minus operational fees).

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.

What deposit is required at booking?

Most operators request 30–50% at contract signing, with the balance due 4–6 weeks before departure. Exact terms appear on your quotation before you commit.

Can I move my charter to a different week if I need to?

Usually yes, subject to fleet availability and any operator handling fee. Request the change as early as possible; mid-season swaps are harder than off-season.

Is there a standard security deposit for damages?

Yes. Most operators block a refundable deposit on your credit card at check-in, typically €2,000–€5,000 depending on vessel. A damage-waiver upgrade reduces or eliminates this hold — ask your consultant for the specific options on your chosen boat.
