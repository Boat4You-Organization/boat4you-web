Sailing Yacht Charter from Vodice

Vodice is the northern gateway to some of the Adriatic's most prized sailing waters. This small resort town, 12 nautical miles north of Šibenik, sits at the mouth of the Krka River and serves as the primary base for charters bound for the Kornati Islands National Park — an archipelago of 89 sparsely inhabited limestone islands scattered across azure water. The Kornati are what many sailors cross the world to experience: clear shallow bays, protected anchorages, minimal development, and sailing conditions that reward small-boat seamanship. Vodice's two marinas (ACI Marina Vodice and Marina Tribunj, just 5 minutes west) offer modern facilities, reliable provisioning, and access to winds that the locals describe as textbook: steady Maestral from the northwest by afternoon, rare Bora in summer, and enough thermal chop by midday to keep sailing interesting without crossing into uncomfortable.

A sailing yacht from Vodice is a statement of intention. You're not cruising for the Instagram moments or anchoring next to 50 other boats; you're after sailing — the rhythm of trimming jib and main, reading the water's surface for wind shifts, feeling the yacht respond as it heels. For experienced sailors, Vodice is a pilgrimage. For those new to sailing but willing to learn, a week anchored in the Kornati with a local skipper or fellow charterers willing to teach is formative in ways that longer passages under power can never replicate.

Why Sailing Yachts Excel in Vodice's Waters and the Kornati Route

The Kornati Islands are tailor-made for sailing. The archipelago provides short hops — 3 to 10 nautical miles between anchorages — that build sailing confidence and don't overwhelm a crew's stamina. The bays are shallow (10–15 meters) with sandy/rocky holding ground that accepts an anchor readily. Wind patterns are predictable: the Maestral builds from 10 knots in the late morning to 15–18 knots by afternoon, then drops away by sunset, leaving most anchorages mirror-flat by evening. This rhythm — morning drift and light air, afternoon sailing with purpose, evening calm — is what sailors dream about.

A monohull sailing yacht (36–45 feet is typical for Vodice charters) excels here because it handles the afternoon Maestral naturally and can rest beam-on to the wind overnight without rolling (as a catamaran might if it swings at an awkward angle). The sailing is genuine — you're trimming, tacking, and reading wind shifts — not ghosting under power with the engine on standby. The heel angle builds character and muscle memory. Anchoring in 12 meters of clear water, watching small fish dart through your mooring bridle, feels like being inside the water rather than floating above it.

The Kornati Islands: Routes and Destinations

The Kornati National Park (5–7 days)

Kornati National Park covers about 220 square kilometers and requires entry permits (paid by the skipper or charter company; roughly €300 per vessel per week, or €30 per day). The park preserves the islands by limiting development and controlling anchorage to designated mooring buoys. A typical week from Vodice follows this arc:

Days 1–2: Departing Vodice, you'll sail south through Murter Channel into the archipelago's northern reaches. The first night might be Marimore or Tušnjare, small bays with limited but adequate shelter. Sailing here means short tacks between islands, constant small adjustments to trim, and the satisfaction of having navigated entirely under sail.

Days 3–4: Push deeper into the park to Telašćica Bay (on Dugi Otok, the largest island) or to anchorages around Mula, Opat, and Obonjan. Telašćica is perhaps the Kornati's most famous anchorage — a deep, protected bay framed by high cliffs and known for its dramatic sunsets. Mula is shallower, more exposed to wind, but surrounded by completely uninhabited islands. Swimming and snorkeling in 30 meters of clear water is standard.

Day 5: Explore smaller anchorages — coves with names like Žut, Rava, Krakan — where you might anchor alongside only one or two other boats (in peak summer) and experience genuine isolation.

Days 6–7: Return northward, potentially taking a day-sail detour up the Krka River to Skradinski Buk (limestone waterfalls and a national park within a national park), or simply make leisurely passages back to Vodice, arriving by early evening.

The Krka River Interior (2–3 day extension)

The Krka River flows inland from Vodice for 12 nautical miles to Skradin, where limestone canyons and three successive waterfalls (Skradinski Buk) create a landscape more reminiscent of the Grand Canyon than the Mediterranean. A sailing yacht can navigate the river (check draft and clearance), though many charterers take a day-sail break at Vodice, anchor securely, and hire a local guide to explore Skradin by road or hired boat. The journey upriver is negotiable at 6–8 knots in good conditions; the falls are worth the effort. This is also where you'll find authentic inland Dalmatian food — not waterfront tourist fare, but family konobas in Skradin and Krka village serving gregada and pašticada as they've been made for generations.

Šibenik Town and The Island Chain South (3–5 day variation)

Šibenik itself, 12 nautical miles south of Vodice, is a medieval fortified town famous for its cathedral (a UNESCO site, unusual in Croatia for having no bell tower, just a dome). The harbor is narrow and requires careful navigation, but once anchored, you're in a living Adriatic town — not a tourist resort. South of Šibenik, smaller islands (Prvić, Zlarin) and the Žirje island group offer quieter sailing and fewer anchored fleets. This route sacrifices the Kornati's isolation but offers more cultural diversity and closer proximity to mainland resources (provisioning, restaurants, markets).

Fleet Options and Sailing Character

36–40 foot sailing yachts (6–8 berths)

Entry-level for bareboat sailing in this region. Typically sloop-rigged (jib and main), with moderate beam and draft (1.4–1.6 meters). Easy to handle for a two-person crew, but cabin space is tight and overnight accommodation is narrow berths. Pricing runs €3,000–5,500 per week depending on season. These boats are ideal for experienced sailors prioritizing sailing over comfort.

40–45 foot sailing yachts (8–10 berths)

The sweet spot for Vodice charters. Larger cabin area, better galley, and improved stability mean more comfort without sacrificing sailing character. Many are split-rigged (with a genoa and working jib) or cutter-rigged, giving options to reef quickly if the Maestral kicks up. Bareboat pricing: €4,000–7,000 per week, depending on season and boat condition. These yachts suit families and groups with some sailing experience.

Skippered and crewed options

Boat4You and local operators offer skippered charters (skipper joins the crew at €150–300 per day) and fully crewed charters (skipper plus one or more crew members, €250–500 per day combined). This is ideal for groups wanting to sail but lacking bareboat credentials, or for those wanting to focus on relaxation rather than navigation and boat handling.

Practical Matters: Wind, Season, and Licensing

Wind patterns and sailing days

The Maestral (northwest wind) is Vodice's default condition from May through October. It typically builds from 8 knots at 10 am to 15–18 knots by 2–3 pm, making morning light-air sailing tense and afternoon sailing energetic. Gusts to 22–25 knots are possible but rare in summer; when they occur, a experienced crew will reef and continue, while newcomers might anchor early and enjoy an evening swim. Bora (northeast, 20–40 knots) is rare in summer but possible in April and October. It arrives suddenly, blows hard for 24 hours, and is gone. Winter Bora (November–March) is more common and can pin boats in harbor for several days. The absolute best sailing is June and September — warm, consistent wind, and comfortable air temperatures (20–22°C in June, 19–21°C in September).

Licensing and bareboat requirements

Bareboat sailing from Vodice requires an International Sailing Certificate (ASA 104 or equivalent) or proof of equivalent sailing competence. Most charterers from the US and UK will have ASA cards; Europeans often hold IYT or RYA certificates. If you lack credentials, hire a skipper (€150–300 per day) to travel with you, or take a one-week ASA 104 course beforehand (many sailing schools in Croatia offer them; €800–1,200 per person). Provisioning and supplies

Vodice has a Konzum supermarket and several local bakeries. Bread, fresh vegetables, cheese, wine, and basic provisions are available. However, before departing for the Kornati (where shops are nonexistent), provision heavily. The park offers no services; your boat is your grocery store. A week typically requires 120–150 euros in food and water if you're cooking simple meals aboard. Eating ashore occasionally (at Telašćica's small restaurant if available, or returning to Vodice or Šibenik for dinner) is a nice break and usually cheaper than provisioning gourmet ingredients.

Pricing and Season

A 40-foot sailing yacht from Vodice costs roughly €3,500–7,000 per week depending on season. Shoulder months (May, June, September, early October) run €4,000–5,500. Peak summer (July–August) commands €5,500–7,000. April and October are cheaper (€3,500–5,000) but bring cooler air and potential Bora wind. Winter is unpredictable and less suitable for bareboat novices.

Sample Itinerary: Seven Days Sailing from Vodice

Day 1 (Monday): Depart Vodice by 10 am after safety briefing and provisioning. Light Maestral (8–10 knots); motor-sail south through Murter Channel into the Kornati. Arrive at Marimore or Tušnjare by 4 pm, set anchor, swim, and settle in.

Day 2 (Tuesday): Short sail to Mula or Opat (3–5 nm). Light morning air requiring patience; energetic afternoon Maestral. Anchor in shallow turquoise water, snorkel off the side of the boat, and explore. Dinner aboard — simple pasta with local olive oil and tinned fish, or grilled meat prepared on the stove. Plavac Mali wine.

Day 3 (Wednesday): Longer passage to Telašćica Bay, Dugi Otok (8 nm). The Maestral will be building; this is real sailing. The bay's dramatic cliffs frame your arrival. Anchor in the deepest water and take the dinghy ashore to explore the beach and rock formations. Sunset drinks on deck as the wind drops.

Day 4 (Thursday): Explore Telašćica's different anchorages, or sail to a quieter island group nearby (Rava, Žut, Svetac). The morning may offer light sailing; the afternoon Maestral builds sailing excitement. Swimming in absolutely clear water is the day's primary activity.

Day 5 (Friday): Day-sail back north, or if you're rested, push deeper into the park toward the southern reaches. This is when you might see dolphins (less common than you'd hope, but possible). Evening anchor in another quiet bay, sharing the anchorage with perhaps one other sailing yacht.

Day 6 (Saturday): Leisurely sail back toward Vodice (12 nm). Light morning air, afternoon sailing. Arrival by late afternoon allows a shower at the marina and dinner ashore at a local konoba.

Day 7 (Sunday): Day off in Vodice. Explore Šibenik's cathedral (45-minute walk or taxi), provision for the next leg, relax at the marina, or sail a short afternoon return to a nearby island anchorage (Prvić, Zlarin) before returning to Vodice for a final evening.

Getting to Vodice

Zadar airport (50 km north, 1 hour drive) or Split airport (80 km south, 1.5 hours drive) are the nearest options. A rental car or prearranged taxi from the airport reaches Vodice in 60–90 minutes. Most charter companies offer transfer arrangement; confirm at booking. The marina staff will brief you on the yacht, check safety equipment, and assist with final provisioning.

Local Flavor: Dalmatian Food and Wine

Dalmatian sailing cuisine is simple and honest. Gregada — the fisherman's stew — appears on every konoba menu and makes excellent overnight fare cooked aboard: mixed fish, tomato, white wine, garlic, and patience. Brodet (more refined, with red wine) is another classic. Pašticada (beef braised with tomato, prunes, and red wine) is the meat dish that sustains multi-day sailing trips. Buy these at restaurant in Vodice or Šibenik before departure, heat them aboard, and they improve with time.

Babić (deep red, from the Šibenik hinterland, earthy and structured) is the regional wine that suits both fish and meat cooked aboard. Pošip (bright white, crisp, from Korčula) is the seafood pairing. Sipping cold Pošip while anchored in a bay where no motorboat engine sound intrudes — only wind, water, and conversation — is why sailing yachts exist.

FAQ

Do I need a sailing license to bareboat charter from Vodice?

Yes, most operators require an International Sailing Certificate (ASA 104 or equivalent). If you lack one, hire a skipper (€150–300 per day) or take an ASA 104 course before departure. Boat4You explains bareboat eligibility in detail.

What is the best time to sail from Vodice?

June and September offer the most consistent Maestral, warm water (22–24°C), and manageable crowds. May and October are cooler but still excellent; Bora risk is slightly higher. July–August are hottest and busiest; book by March. Avoid November–March unless you're experienced in Bora conditions.

How much wind is typical in the Kornati?

The Maestral typically builds from 10 knots in late morning to 15–18 knots by early afternoon. Gusts to 22–25 knots are rare in summer but possible. The wind drops away by sunset, leaving most anchorages calm by evening. Bora (northeast, 20–40 knots) is rare in summer but can arrive suddenly in spring or fall.

Can a family with children sail from Vodice?

Yes, if the children are at least 8–10 years old and the parents have sailing experience or book a skipper. The Kornati's short passages (3–10 nm) and calm anchorages suit families better than longer open-water sailing. Bareboat charters with very young children (<8) are less ideal unless you have substantial sailing experience; a skippered charter allows parents to relax while a professional handles navigation.

What should I provision for a week in the Kornati?

Buy bread, fresh vegetables, cheese, eggs, and protein (tinned fish, cured meat) from Vodice before departing. Stock wine, olive oil, and basic pantry items. Budget €200–300 for a week of simple self-catering. Eating ashore occasionally (if Telašćica's restaurant is open, or returning to Vodice or Šibenik for dinner) breaks up onboard cooking. Most sailors enjoy the simplicity of boat food — pasta, grilled fish, and wine consumed at anchor in absolute quiet.

Is there fresh water in the Kornati?

No. All water for drinking, cooking, and showering comes from your boat's tank. A 40-foot yacht typically carries 300–400 liters (€20–30 to fill, available at Vodice marina). Plan for 20–30 liters per person per day; a week for two people requires 280–420 liters, fitting easily in one tank fill. Shower water can be saltwater rinse with a hand-pump; this minimizes freshwater use.

What happens if the Bora wind arrives during my charter?

Bora is rare in summer but possible in spring and fall. A strong Bora (25+ knots) typically arrives for 12–36 hours, then clears. Most experienced skippers will seek shelter (often behind a larger island, or returning to Vodice's protected harbor) and wait it out. A week-long charter has time flexibility; a delayed return date or changing plans is the standard response. Bora in summer is usually not severe enough to be dangerous, just uncomfortable.
