Sailing Yacht Charter Marina Zadar

Marina Zadar, formerly known as Tankerkomerc, occupies a working harbourside position in Zadar's Gaženica district, south of the Old Town. With over 250 berths accommodating vessels up to 40+ metres, it stands as one of the central Adriatic's most capable deep-water bases for serious sailing. A sailing yacht departure from Marina Zadar puts you within arm's reach of the Kornati archipelago and Telašćica National Park — two of the region's most rewarding cruising grounds — while keeping Old Town's restaurants and culture just a 15-minute walk away.

This combination of maritime infrastructure and accessible heritage makes the marina a natural choice for sailors seeking genuine island-hopping experiences without sacrificing shore-side amenities. The Adriatic's characteristic wind patterns — reliable Maestral during summer months, brisk Bora from the Velebit mountains in winter — favour a sailing itinerary from this position more than most Croatian bases.

Why a Sailing Yacht from Marina Zadar Works

Sailing vessels have particular advantages in the waters around Zadar. The Maestral, the region's signature summer wind, typically arrives mid-morning and strengthens through afternoon, creating ideal conditions for upwind work to the southern islands. Unlike powered boats, a sailing yacht converts that wind into pure forward motion; you'll spend less fuel and fewer engine hours reaching Kornati than a motorboat would require. The archipelago's 89 islands and numerous anchorages favour the nimble manoeuvring and shallow-draft capabilities that pure sailing offers.

Marina Zadar's deep-water location means you can depart with a full week's provisioning in the holds and still maintain proper heel and performance. Provisioning at Zadar's waterfront markets — near Pet Bunara and Konoba Stomorica, both within walking distance — adds no berth time penalty. The 250+ berth capacity also reduces summertime competition for good mooring space, a real advantage during July when Split and Trogir marinas contract to standing-room-only density.

Week-Long Sailing Routes from Marina Zadar

Kornati Circuit: Five Days in the Archipelago

The Kornati National Park covers 35 square kilometres of open water scattered with uninhabited limestone islands. From Marina Zadar, the passage south to the park entrance (roughly 20–25 nautical miles) takes 4 to 6 hours under mixed sail and motor — long enough to settle into rhythm without exhausting a crew. Most sailing charters anchor first at Lovišća, a protected cove on Kornati's western edge, then work progressively through Veli Pažnjol, Purara, and the northern islands. The islands themselves host no permanent populations, but Veli Žavorić offers a small restaurant (just three mooring buoys) serving fresh fish and Pag lamb. Nights are silent except for water lapping hulls and the occasional wind shift.

Sailing in tight quarters between islands demands focus but rewards it. Channels often run 200–400 metres wide, with holding ground (sand and mud) stable enough for lunch stops. The park's current regulations limit daily visits to a maximum number of vessels, keeping crowds manageable. Budget two full days to sail the inner Kornati route and at least one day anchored to do nothing but read and swim.

Telašćica Extension: Natural Beauty and Sheltered Anchorages

Telašćica National Park, occupying the southern half of Dugi Otok island (15 nautical miles from Marina Zadar), provides a natural rest day for sailing crews. The bay forms a dramatic natural harbour — limestone cliffs rise 160 metres directly from the waterline — and holds 15+ distinct anchorages suitable for sailing yachts. The Saline, a small saltwater lake in the park's interior, sits accessible by a 1km walk from the north anchorage. The park restaurant at Saline Junction serves Dalmatian peka (baked meat and vegetables under hot embers) and local Pošip wine from small family vineyards near Zadar.

A sailing yacht can anchor at Saline, Tiha Pego, or Mirna — all protected even during strong Bora wind. The holding ground is mud, ideal for security over multi-day stays. Most charters allocate two nights here, using the protected basin to service sails, recharge batteries, and restock fresh water.

Pag Island Loop: Cheese, Salt Pans, and Wind-Shaped Landscape

Pag island, 25 nautical miles south-southwest of Marina Zadar, represents a fundamentally different sailing experience — exposed to open channels with consistent wind and few protected anchorages, but rewarding for crews comfortable with dynamic sailing. The island produces two legendary products: Pag cheese (a hard, salty variety aged in underground caves) and sea salt harvested from industrial salt pans visible from the water. The town of Pag features a Renaissance town square and several konobas serving cheese-based specialities like pag makarun (thick noodles with meat sauce and local cheese).

Most sailing charters visit Pag as a day destination from Zadar or as an anchor stop en route to Kornati. The exposed western coast offers few shelter options, but the eastern side near the town provides adequate holding in 4–6 metres. The island's reputation for strong Maestral and occasional katabatic wind from the interior requires attentive sail management, but skilled crews find the sailing exhilarating.

Sailing Yacht Specifications and What to Expect

Sailing yachts from Marina Zadar typically range from 30 to 45 feet, with draft between 1.5 and 2.0 metres. Most are Beneteau, Dufour, or similar European makes, equipped with modern electronics (chartplotter, autopilot, VHF) and adequate engine power to motor through windless stretches. Bareboat charters require proof of sailing credentials — typically a valid ICC (International Certificate of Competence) or equivalent national qualification. Crewed options include a professional skipper and often a cook, converting the yacht into a floating hotel with navigation expertise included.

Cabin layouts include the master berth aft, a saloon convertible to sleeping (usually two berths), and forward cabins. Galleys are compact but functional for meal preparation. Fresh water capacity ranges from 150 to 400 litres depending on vessel size — adequate for 5–7 days with rationing.

Practical Details: Pricing, Seasons, and Logistics

Sailing yacht charter from Marina Zadar ranges from roughly 1,200 to 2,800 euros per week depending on vessel size, season, and whether a skipper is included. Peak season (July–August) commands the upper end; shoulder months (May, June, September) offer 15–25% discounts. Early-season departures (April) and late autumn (October) can drop charter rates another 20%, though wind conditions become less predictable.

Marina Zadar lies approximately 15 kilometres east of Zadar Airport (ZAD). Airport transfer takes 20–25 minutes by taxi or rental car. The nearest major city, Zadar itself, offers supermarkets (Konzum, Lidl) and traditional markets (Narodni Trg fish and produce market) for fresh provisioning. Restaurants near the marina include the long-established Pet Bunara, famous for seafood risotto and locally caught octopus, and Konoba Stomorica, which specializes in grilled fish and pašticada (slow-braised beef with plums).

Sailing season peaks May through October, with summer (June–August) offering the most wind but also the most crowded anchorages. Bora wind season (November–March) brings strong, cold northeasterly gusts from the Velebit mountains — dramatic sailing for experienced crews, but dangerous for casual charterers. Most bareboat charters run May through September; crewed options extend into April and October when conditions still favour relaxed cruising.

Bareboat vs. Crewed: Choosing Your Style

Bareboat sailing from Marina Zadar suits crews with proven blue-water or Mediterranean sailing experience. You'll handle all navigation, sail trim, and mooring procedures. The freedom to anchor anywhere within park regulations and follow your own pace appeals to sailors who prefer self-reliance. Most operators require a sailing certificate and ask for evidence of prior bareboat experience (logbook entries or character references from previous charter companies).

Crewed sailing adds a professional skipper (and often a cook) whose local knowledge shortens the learning curve for unfamiliar waters. Skippers familiar with Kornati and Telašćica waters know which anchorages are currently holding well, where to find the best local restaurants, and how to read wind patterns developing off the Velebit. A week's crewed sailing costs roughly 30–40% more than bareboat but eliminates stress for mixed-experience crews.

FAQ

Do I need a sailing license to charter a sailing yacht from Marina Zadar?

Bareboat charters require an ICC (International Certificate of Competence) or a national cruising license demonstrating at least 100 hours of open-water sailing in the past 12 months. Some operators accept logbook entries and references instead. Crewed charters have no license requirement; the skipper handles all navigation and seamanship.

What is the best month for sailing from Marina Zadar to Kornati?

June and September offer ideal conditions: reliable Maestral wind, moderate sea states, and fewer vessels than July–August. May and October work well for experienced crews prepared for variable conditions. July and August guarantee wind and calm waters but bring crowded anchorages and peak pricing.

How far is Marina Zadar from Zadar Airport?

The marina sits approximately 15 kilometres from Zadar Airport (ZAD). Airport transfer by taxi or rental car takes 20–25 minutes and costs roughly 20–30 euros for a shared taxi or 40–50 euros for a private transfer.

Can I provision a yacht at Marina Zadar?

Yes. The Narodni Trg market is a 15-minute walk, offering fresh fish, vegetables, and local cheeses. Nearby supermarkets (Konzum, Lidl) stock packaged goods. Many charters include provisioning services; ask your operator for the current list of waterfront suppliers.

Is Kornati National Park free to enter with a sailing yacht?

Entry to Kornati National Park requires a park fee (roughly 100–150 kuna per day, approximately 13–20 euros) per vessel. The fee covers one mooring buoy and park services. Most charter operators collect this as part of the final bill or advise you to pay at the park office upon arrival.

What local food should I try while sailing from Marina Zadar?

Pag cheese (aged in salt caves) pairs with local Pošip white wine. At anchorages, try Veli Žavorić's fresh fish or any konoba's maraschino (cherry liqueur from the Zadar region). Peka — meat and vegetables slow-roasted under hot embers — appears at waterfront restaurants and is a signature Dalmatian dish.
