Catamaran Charter Marina Zadar

Marina Zadar, the former Tankerkomerc facility, has evolved into the central Adriatic's premier catamaran charter base. The 250+ berth harbour in Zadar's Gaženica district accommodates multihulls up to 45 feet with plenty of swinging room — increasingly important as catamaran popularity grows. From here, the region's shallow-draft cruising grounds become genuinely accessible: Telašćica National Park's protected inner bays, Ugljan and Pašman islands' sheltered anchorages, and the Kornati archipelago's northern passages all favour the kind of light-draught, spacious vessels that catamarans are built to be.

Catamarans changed family sailing and group charters fundamentally. The wide platform creates more deck space for sunbathing and entertaining, the shallow draft (typically 1.0–1.2 metres) opens anchorages that monohull yachts cannot reach, and the natural stability means less heeling and more comfortable galley work for non-sailors. From Marina Zadar, all of this translates into a week where you can safely anchor in 1.5 metres of water in Telašćica's Saline bay, spend afternoon swims without worrying about the yacht drifting, and let crew members of varying experience feel genuinely included.

The Catamaran Advantage in Zadar Waters

Catamarans from Marina Zadar deliver speed and stability that monohulls cannot match in light-wind conditions. The Maestral often arrives weak during early morning, building slowly through mid-day. A catamaran's wide hull separation and modern sail plans accelerate faster in marginal wind, making those early-morning passages from Zadar to Kornati (25 nautical miles) achievable in 5–6 hours under sail rather than the motor-dependent 8–10 hours a monohull might require in the same conditions.

Stability at anchor matters profoundly for family charters. Catamarans sit nearly flat even in gusty conditions, eliminating the constant roll and pitch that can spoil evening meals and discourage children from exploring the cabin. Shallow draft opens Telašćica's inner coves — Mirna, Saline, Tiha Pego — where depth rarely exceeds 2.0 metres but holding ground (mud over sand) is superb. You can anchor 50 metres from white limestone cliffs without grounding, something a traditional 1.8-metre-draft monohull cannot safely do.

Galley and Cabin Space

Catamaran galleys sit higher off the water than monohull galleys, reducing motion during meal prep and making cooking less exhausting. The broad beam allocates generous cabin widths; staterooms typically feature double berths with headroom comfortable for sitting upright — a marked contrast to the cave-like quarters of compact monohulls. Most charter catamarans from Marina Zadar (Lagoon 380–450, Catana 41–47 class vessels) offer sleeping for 8–10, split across a master suite, two to three guest cabins, and a saloon convertible to sleeping berths. This layout suits multigenerational groups and larger friendship crews without forcing anyone into a windowless cave.

Telašćica National Park: The Catamaran's Natural Home

Telašćica National Park occupies the southern half of Dugi Otok, roughly 15 nautical miles south of Marina Zadar. Its defining feature — a deep, sheltered natural harbour ringed by 160-metre limestone cliffs — creates one of the Mediterranean's most dramatic backdrops. More practically, it provides 15+ distinct anchorages, all suitable for shallow-draft catamarans, many impossible for boats drawing more than 1.5 metres.

Saline, the park's northern anchorage, sits directly below a saltwater lake accessible by a short walking path. The small restaurant there serves Dalmatian peka (slow-roasted meat and vegetables) and local Pošip wine. Most catamaran charters spend two full days anchored at Saline, using the protected water for swimming, snorkelling, and a casual pace that takes pressure off skippers and crew. The Saline holding ground (mud) is textbook — you'll set anchor in 5–6 metres and sleep peacefully.

Tiha Pego and Mirna offer alternative anchorages on the bay's eastern and southern sides, each suited to different wind directions. A catamaran's shallow draft means you can anchor in any of these spots with confidence, rather than hunting for the one deeper slot that traditional monohulls require.

Kornati Circuit from Marina Zadar

The Kornati archipelago, 89 islands scattered across 35 square kilometres, delivers the Adriatic's purest sailing experience. From Marina Zadar, the passage to the northern Kornati islands (roughly 20–25 nautical miles) takes a catamaran 4–6 hours under mixed sail and motor. Entry to the national park requires a fee (approximately 13–20 euros per vessel per day) and limits daily vessel count, keeping crowds manageable.

Catamarans navigate Kornati's tight island channels with ease. Passages between islands often run 200–400 metres wide; a catamaran's responsive steering and moderate-draught hull position you safely in the deepest water. Anchorages scatter throughout the park — Lovišća, Veli Pažnjol, Purara, northern reaches — offering choices based on wind direction and mood. Most catamaran charters allocate 3–4 days in Kornati, moving daily and anchoring at a new spot each evening.

Veli Žavorić hosts a small restaurant (mooring buoys available) serving fresh fish and local lamb. Provisioning opportunities are minimal — bring supplies from Zadar. The islands themselves are uninhabited and protected; swimming is unrestricted, and the clarity of the water (typically 20–30 metres visibility) makes snorkelling exceptional.

Pašman and Ugljan: Easy Day Sails from the Marina

For families wanting relaxed pacing, Pašman and Ugljan islands (3–5 nautical miles south of Marina Zadar) provide gentle day-sail destinations without the commitment of Kornati's longer passages. Pašman's eastern anchorages (Velji Rat, Ždrelac Strait) offer sheltered spots in 3–5 metres of water, ideal for catamaran anchoring. The islands' interior towns (Pašman town on Pašman, Preko on Ugljan) feature local konobas serving fresh fish, octopus salads, and cheese-based pasta dishes.

Most catamaran charters mixing families with varying experience build a flexible itinerary: perhaps two nights at Telašćica (anchored and relaxed), two nights in Kornati (daysailing and new anchorages), and a final evening at Pašman or Ugljan (easy sail, early sunset return to Marina Zadar). This split-tempo approach keeps energy high without burning out non-sailor crew members.

Practical Considerations and Seasonal Patterns

Catamaran charter from Marina Zadar ranges from approximately 1,400 to 3,200 euros per week, depending on vessel size (typically 40–47 feet), season, and whether provisioning or a skipper is included. Peak season (July–August) commands premium pricing; shoulder months (May, June, September) offer 15–25% reductions. April and October charters can approach 40% off peak rates, though weather becomes less predictable.

The marina sits 15 kilometres from Zadar Airport (ZAD). Airport transfer by taxi or shared transfer service takes 20–25 minutes and costs 20–30 euros shared or 40–50 euros private. Zadar itself offers supermarkets (Konzum, Lidl) and the traditional Narodni Trg market for fresh provisioning — fish, vegetables, local Pag cheese, and the region's distinctive Maraschino cherry liqueur.

Sailing season peaks May through October. June and September offer ideal conditions: reliable Maestral wind, moderate sea states, and manageable anchorage density. July and August guarantee wind and calm waters but bring peak pricing and crowded anchorages. Crewed charters extend into April and October when professional skippers can manage variable conditions; bareboat charters typically run May–September.

Why Catamarans Suit Family Groups

Stability, space, and shallow draft combine to make catamarans the practical choice for families with young children or mixed-swimming-ability groups. The flat platform means children can move freely about the yacht without constant bracing. The wide cabins let each family have genuine privacy rather than sleeping in shifts. The shallow draft opens anchorages where kids can wade 50 metres from the yacht without depth exceeding shoulder height — a freedom that traditional monohulls simply cannot provide.

Catamarans also forgive modest sailing experience. The natural stability reduces seasickness, the responsive steering makes basic docking easier, and the broad deck and high freeboard create a sense of security that narrow, deep-hulled monohulls don't offer nervous sailors. Most catamaran charters from Marina Zadar accommodate mixed-experience crews without hesitation.

FAQ

Is a sailing license required to charter a bareboat catamaran from Marina Zadar?

Bareboat catamarans require an ICC (International Certificate of Competence) or national sailing credential demonstrating recent open-water experience. Some operators accept logbook entries or references. Crewed charters include a professional skipper; no license is required.

Can I visit Telašćica National Park with a chartered catamaran?

Yes. Entry requires a park fee (approximately 13–20 euros per vessel per day). Catamarans are ideal for Telašćica's shallow anchorages — the park's inner bays rarely exceed 2.0 metres depth, and catamaran shallow draft makes all anchorages accessible. Most charters include the park fee or advise payment upon arrival at the park office.

What is the typical sleeping capacity of a chartered catamaran from Marina Zadar?

Charter catamarans (40–47 feet class) typically sleep 8–10 people across a master suite, two to three guest cabins, and a convertible saloon. Exact sleeping capacity depends on vessel configuration; confirm with the operator at booking.

How long is the sail from Marina Zadar to Kornati?

The passage to Kornati National Park (roughly 20–25 nautical miles) takes 4–6 hours under mixed sail and motor, depending on wind conditions. Catamarans typically sail faster than monohulls in light-wind Maestral conditions, often reaching Kornati in the lower end of that range.

What local specialities should I taste while sailing from Marina Zadar?

Pag cheese (hard, aged in salt caves) pairs with Pošip white wine from local Zadar vineyards. Maraschino liqueur (made from local maraschino cherries) is iconic. Try pešticada (slow-braised beef with plums) and peka at waterfront konobas — both are quintessential Dalmatian dishes worth seeking out.

Is Marina Zadar suitable for catamarans with families?

Absolutely. Marina Zadar's protected harbour and 250+ berths accommodate catamarans comfortably. The location is walkable to Old Town shops and restaurants, the nearby Telašćica and Ugljan islands offer protected family-friendly anchorages, and provisioning is straightforward. It is a popular base for family catamaran charters.
