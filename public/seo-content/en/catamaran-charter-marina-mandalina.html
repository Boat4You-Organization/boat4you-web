Island Routes: Kornati National Park & Beyond

The Kornati Islands lie 8 nautical miles southeast. This dramatic archipelago—89 uninhabited islets sculpted from limestone—presents textbook catamaran cruising: predictable anchorages in 4-6 meters, remarkable underwater visibility, and zero infrastructure (which encourages anchoring discipline). A typical passage takes 60-90 minutes under sail or light power. Many crews spend 3-4 days exploring Kornati's outer ring (Morter, Levrnaka) before returning to D-Marin for resupply and shower facilities.

The Krka River Estuary sits 6 nautical miles west. This Adriatic rarity—a navigable river with 7 meters depth at its mouth—offers cool anchorages among pine forests and limestone cliffs. The river's upper reaches (Skradin, 12 nm upstream) host historical towns and freshwater swimming. Summer crowds concentrate at Šibenik's harbor mouth; quieter moorings await crews who venture upriver at dawn.

Murter Island (5 nm northeast) provides a sheltered alternative to Kornati for families seeking day-trip bases with market access. The village of Betina offers excellent fish restaurants and a small chandlery. Larger catamarans often moor in Murter's protected harbor, using day anchorages in nearby Pasman and Ugljan straits for variety.

Trogir (30 nm southwest) rewards extended passages with its medieval Venetian square and anchorages on the outer island of Čiovo. The town's narrow harbor can accommodate catamarans up to 45 feet comfortably. Rogoznica, halfway between Šibenik and Trogir (20 nm), offers a modern alternative base with excellent restaurants and a working fishing fleet.

Seasons & Weather Patterns

Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) define ideal catamaran cruising here. Maestral winds blow reliably from the northwest, typically Force 3-4, perfect for stable sailing without excessive heeling. Water temperatures climb from 16°C in April to 21°C in October. Summer (June–August) brings intense heat, occasional Bura squalls (northeast 20-30 knots), and crowded anchorages.

Winter (November–March) sees Bura dominance, with sudden 30-40-knot northeast gusts and rough seas. Professional crews tackle winter passages, but most leisure charters operate May through September. The shoulder months reward early risers with calm mornings and dramatic sunset anchorages.
