ACI Marina Cres: Facilities and Infrastructure

ACI Marina Cres operates as a full-service facility meeting international standards. The 450+ berth layout accommodates vessels from 8 to 60 meters, with modern finger berths offering individual power connections, fresh water pedestals, and waste disposal. The marina operates year-round (peak season May–October), with professional reception staff fluent in English, German, and Italian.

Fuel docking is convenient and efficient. A dedicated fuel pier dispenses both gasoline and diesel during operational hours (typically 8 AM–5 PM daily). Potable water lines at every berth ensure no need for jerry-can runs. Waste water, oil, and sewage disposal facilities meet environmental standards. These practical considerations matter immensely on a catamaran charter, where convenience directly impacts your itinerary freedom.

Shore power supply runs 230/400V single and three-phase connections, allowing full recharge of batteries and operation of galley appliances while moored. A travel-lift (crane) capable of hauling vessels up to 60 tons accommodates maintenance, repairs, and emergency haul-outs. Boat4You maintains partnerships with ACI's mechanical team, ensuring rapid response if technical issues arise during your charter.

Location and Access

Cres Town's harbor sits directly adjacent to the Old Town's medieval quarter. Unlike distant marina complexes, ACI Cres lets you moor steps from daily markets, waterfront restaurants, and the Venetian loggia—the architectural heart of the town. Walking distance from your catamaran berth places you in genuine island life, not a isolated resort facility.

Vehicle access is straightforward. The island's main road connects to the mainland via the Brestova ferry (northern route) and Mali Lošinj ferry services. Rental cars picked up at Split or Rijeka airports (100–120 km distant) reach ACI Marina in 2–3 hours. The marina provides limited but dedicated parking for crew vehicles during charter periods.

International arrivals typically fly into Split, Rijeka, or Zagreb. Island Routes and Sailing Patterns

From ACI Marina Cres, Mali Lošinj lies 15 nautical miles south—a comfortable morning's catamaran passage. The voyage crosses open water with moderate depths, ideal for catamaran handling. Mali Lošinj's spacious harbor offers excellent provisioning and waterfront dining. Many crews overnight here, making it the natural first stop on multi-day charters.

Susak and Unije, the western islands of the Kvarner cluster, beckon for extended passages (18–22 nautical miles). These less-visited islands offer pristine sandy anchorages, clear water, and genuine solitude. Catamarans' shallow draft and spacious decks make overnight passages to Susak especially pleasant—the journey itself becomes part of the holiday.

Eastward routes cross the Kvarner passage toward Istra's mainland coast. Rovinj, Motovun, and Vrsar offer rich provisioning, medieval charm, and protected inner harbors. These routes suit crews preferring to avoid open water and those interested in mainland European sailing cultures beyond the island world.

North of Cres, Beli village (accessible by tender from nearby anchorages) hosts a griffon vulture reserve—Europe's only wild breeding colony of these magnificent raptors. The village sits 230 meters above the sea, offering dramatic clifftop character. Lubenice, similarly perched, provides elevated views and authentic local atmosphere.

Provisioning and Local Food Culture

Cres Town's daily market (Pazar) opens early and stocks fresh vegetables, local cheese, and island-made olive oil. This is where locals shop; prices reflect local income levels, making provisioning economical compared to major charter hubs. Fresh fish arrives daily; speak directly with fishmongers to secure the catch of the day.

Cres lamb is legendary. Every proper konoba features slow-braised pašticada or simple grilled lamb chops. The meat's distinctive flavor comes from island vegetation—sparse, windswept grasses and wild herbs. Pair with Cres red wine (Plavac Mali, house wines from family vineyards) or a crisp white from the Istrian peninsula.

Olive oil production dates to Venetian times. Many island producers sell directly from small farms; exploring villages like Valun and Lubenice often leads to producer meetings and tasting opportunities. These experiences—personal connections to island food culture—remain impossible from larger, impersonal charter bases.
