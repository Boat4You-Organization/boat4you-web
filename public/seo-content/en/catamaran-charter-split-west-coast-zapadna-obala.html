Catamaran Charter Split West Coast

Split West Coast — the quayside berthing strip along Split's Riva, south of Diocletian's Palace — occupies one of the Mediterranean's most theatrical charter positions. Your catamaran sits metres from Renaissance and Roman architecture, within arm's reach of restaurants and shops, yet positioned perfectly to sail within hours to Hvar island's lavender-scented hills, the isolated cliffs of Vis, and the limestone chaos of the Dalmatian archipelago. It is urban accessibility matched with world-class sailing terrain, something few Mediterranean bases achieve.

West Coast catamarans suit groups prioritising comfort and flexible pace. The multihull's shallow draft opens anchorages around Šolta, Brač, and Hvar that traditional yachts cannot safely approach. The wide platform creates space for mixed-experience crews and families. The location itself — directly adjacent to Old Town's restaurants, markets, and culture — reduces pre-departure stress and allows late arrivals to settle comfortably rather than spending charter hours driving to remote marinas.

Split West Coast: Location and Logistics

Split West Coast occupies a working harbour position on the Riva — Split's pedestrian waterfront promenade. The area hosts a mix of charter fleet berths, working ferries, and occasional megayacht traffic, creating an atmosphere of purposeful maritime activity rather than manicured resort tranquility. This working character is actually an advantage: professional crew rotations, reliable water and fuel services, and a constant flow of local knowledge make logistics straightforward. The proximity to Diocletian's Palace and Old Town shops means provisioning and last-minute supplies are a 5-minute walk away.

Split Airport (SPU) lies approximately 25 kilometres from the waterfront, reachable by taxi or shuttle in 30–40 minutes. Public transit (local buses) also connects the airport to the Riva in roughly 45 minutes for budget-conscious travellers. Most charter companies offer airport transfer services; expect to pay 30–50 euros for shared transfer or 60–90 euros for private transfer.

Why Catamarans Excel from Split West Coast

Catamarans from Split command particular advantages in the southern Adriatic routes that dominate this base's itineraries. The Hvar-Vis circuit — a classic week's cruise covering Šolta (5nm), Brač Bol (15nm), Hvar (25nm), and Vis (40nm) — favours multihulls in two decisive ways. First, the journey involves open-water passages (Hvar to Vis especially) where a catamaran's superior light-wind performance matters: the Maestral may arrive weak, but a catamaran accelerates enough to keep your schedule intact. Second, shallow-draft access becomes critical once you arrive. Hvar's protected western anchorages (Hvar Town waterfront, Pakleni Islands' numerous coves) rarely exceed 3 metres depth; Vis's most sheltered spots sit in even shallower water. A catamaran drawing 1.0–1.2 metres reaches these anchorages safely, while many monohulls require careful navigation or miss them entirely.

Stability at anchor matters profoundly during this route. Hvar's western coast, though protected, can roll in residual swell from summer thermal winds. A catamaran sits flat; monohulls rock and pitch. Over a week, this stability compounds into genuine comfort, especially for non-sailors and families with young children.

Week-Long Itineraries from Split West Coast

The Hvar-Vis Classic: Day-by-Day Breakdown

Most catamaran charters from Split West Coast follow a consistent structure. Day 1 (Departure): Cast off in late afternoon, motor to Šolta's Maslinica (5 nautical miles, roughly 1 hour), anchor in the protective cove south of town. Maslinica is a working fishing village with a konoba serving fresh catch and local wine; expect roughly 20 other yachts anchored nearby. Day 2: Motor to Brač island's Bol (15 nautical miles, 3–4 hours depending on wind). Bol is famous for Zlatni Rat, a shifting sand bar creating a distinctive triangular beach. Anchor in Bol's central bay, visit the beach, explore the town. Day 3: Sail northeast to Hvar island (25 nautical miles, 5–6 hours mixed sail and motor). Hvar Town's waterfront is congested; most charters moor at Pakleni Islands (small islands west of Hvar Town) instead, accessing the islands' numerous protected coves where depths stay shallow and holding is excellent.

Day 4: Rest day at Pakleni Islands. Swim, snorkel, explore the islands on foot, enjoy peka and local wine at island restaurants. Day 5: Sail south to Vis (40 nautical miles from Pakleni, 7–8 hours). Vis is isolated, dramatic, and less crowded than Hvar. The island's western anchorages (Komiža, Paljol bay) offer shelter and are accessible at shallow draft. Vis produces distinctive wine (Prošek, a dessert wine) and exceptional fish. Many crews anchor at Komiža and spend an evening at waterfront konobas serving lobster and locally caught dalmatian ink fish (a local specialty). Day 6: Return north toward Hvar or explore Vis's eastern coast (Vis Town, protected anchorages) depending on crew energy and wind. Day 7: Motor back to Split, arriving by evening. The return passage (roughly 6–8 hours depending on Vis position) can be timed to approach Split's harbour at sunset.

Brač Loop: The Shorter Option

Crews seeking less aggressive passage times or with limited sailing experience sometimes focus the week on Šolta, Brač, and Hvar (omitting Vis). Šolta (5nm from Split), Brač (15nm), and Hvar (25nm from Split) create a tight triangle. Day one: Šolta for lunch, return to Brač for overnight. Day two: Full day at Brač, exploring Bol beach and nearby coves. Day three: Motor to Hvar, spend two nights at Pakleni Islands. Days five–six: Leisurely return loop via Brač or Šolta, allowing swimming stops and a relaxed final passage. This itinerary suits families with younger children and non-sailor crew members, as no single passage exceeds 4 hours and anchoring opportunities are plentiful.

Practical Details: Pricing, Provisioning, and Seasons

Catamaran charter from Split West Coast ranges from approximately 1,600 to 3,600 euros per week, depending on vessel size (typically 40–47 feet), season, and services included. Peak season (July–August) commands premium pricing; shoulder months (May, June, September) offer 15–25% discounts. Early season (April) and late autumn (October) can approach 40% off peak rates, though weather becomes less predictable.

Provisioning at Split is straightforward. The adjacent Narodni Trg market (100 metres from the waterfront) offers fresh fish, vegetables, local cheese, and prepared foods. Supermarkets (Konzum, Carrefour) sit nearby. Restaurants within walking distance range from casual konobas to fine dining; waterfront tables with Diocletian's Palace views are common but come at premium prices (expect 15–25 euros per main course). Local pašticada (slow-braised beef with plums), fresh grilled octopus, and Plavac Mali red wine are constants on every menu.

Sailing season peaks May through October. June and September offer ideal conditions: reliable Maestral, moderate sea states, and manageable anchorage density. July and August guarantee wind and calm waters but bring peak pricing, crowded anchorages (especially Hvar), and peak tourism crowds ashore. Spring and autumn charters suit crews flexible on exact dates and budget-conscious.

Catamaran Configurations and Cabin Space

Charter catamarans from Split (Lagoon 40–47, Catana 41–47 class vessels) typically sleep 8–10 people across a master suite, two to three guest cabins, and convertible saloon berths. The master usually features a double bed, ensuite head, and small shower. Guest cabins split between double berths (primary guest cabins) and twin singles (smaller cabins). Saloon convertible sleeping accommodates 2–4 additional bodies when full capacity is needed.

Galley layouts sit high on modern catamarans, reducing cooking motion and allowing sightline visibility while meal preparation occurs. Head (toilet) systems feature electric flush and holding tanks on most modern charter boats. Water capacity typically runs 300–400 litres, adequate for 4–5 days with modest rationing. Fuel capacity (diesel) spans 300–500 litres, sufficient for 4–5 days of typical motoring if provisioned before departure.

FAQ

Is a sailing license required to charter a bareboat catamaran from Split?

Bareboat catamarans require an ICC (International Certificate of Competence) or national sailing credential demonstrating recent experience. Some operators accept logbook entries or references from previous charters. Crewed charters include a professional skipper; no license is required.

What is the best month for sailing from Split to Hvar and Vis?

June and September offer ideal conditions: reliable Maestral wind, moderate sea states, and fewer vessels than July–August. May and October work well for experienced crews. July and August guarantee wind and calm seas but bring peak pricing and crowded anchorages at popular spots like Hvar Town and Pakleni Islands.

How far is Split Airport from Split West Coast?

Split Airport (SPU) lies approximately 25 kilometres from the Riva waterfront. Airport transfer takes 30–40 minutes by taxi or shuttle and costs roughly 30–50 euros shared or 60–90 euros private. Local buses also connect airport and city centre for budget options.

Can I anchor near Hvar Town with a catamaran?

Hvar Town's central waterfront is congested and crowded. Most catamaran charters moor instead at nearby Pakleni Islands (small islands west of Hvar Town), which offer numerous protected anchorages in shallow water — ideal for catamaran shallow draft. Pakleni Islands are within 10 minutes sailing of Hvar Town, so accessing restaurants and shops remains easy.

What local specialities should I try while sailing from Split?

Pašticada (slow-braised beef with plums) is iconic to Split. Fresh grilled octopus, Pag cheese, and lobster feature at most waterfront konobas. Local wines include Plavac Mali (full-bodied red) and Pošip (crisp white). Maraschino liqueur (from local maraschino cherries) and Vis Prošek (dessert wine if you visit Vis) are worth seeking out.

Does Split West Coast charter company include park fees in the price?

Park fees (Hvar, Pakleni Islands, or Vis if visited) are typically separate from charter pricing. Confirm with your operator; some include park passes, others require you to pay daily entrance fees (roughly 10–20 euros per anchorage per day) at the park office or through the boat's WiFi.
