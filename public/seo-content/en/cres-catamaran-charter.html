Cres Town exudes Venetian character. Its stone loggia, waterfront restaurants serving local lamb and olive oil, and narrow cobbled streets transport you back centuries. Day Sails and Island-Hopping Routes

From Cres Town, the island of Lošinj lies just 8 nautical miles south. These two islands were nearly one before tectonic movement separated them, and they share similar character: pine-backed beaches, konoba restaurants, and a sailing culture that stretches back generations. Mali Lošinj's harbor is spacious and welcoming; Veli Lošinj offers quieter moorings.

Unije and Susak are the outer jewels of this route. Unije, 18 nautical miles southwest, hosts a tiny population and pristine anchorages ringed by Aleppo pines. Susak, further west, famously has sandy beaches (uncommon in the Adriatic) and excellent holding ground for overnight anchoring. Both islands reward the slightly longer passage with solitude and clear water.

Explore the Tramuntana forest that dominates Cres's interior. The northernmost stretches, accessed from anchorages near Beli, feature a griffon vulture reserve—Europe's only wild breeding colony of these magnificent birds. Beli village itself clings to cliffs 230 meters above the sea, offering dramatic views and excellent restaurants serving traditional Cres lamb.

Lubenice, another clifftop settlement, sits 300 meters up. The walk from your catamaran's dinghy is steep, but the reward is exceptional: an elevated village with medieval character, local wines, and sunsets over the Kvarner passage. These anchorages offer shallow, sandy bottoms perfect for catamaran mooring.

Cres Town: Base and Gateway

Cres Town's harbor wraps naturally around the Old Town. Morning walks lead you through the stone loggia—a Venetian administrative building—past waterfront konobas, and into markets fragrant with fresh fish and local produce. Boat4You ensures your catamaran is berthed at a convenient waterfront location, often within steps of these cultural anchors.

Provisioning is straightforward. The daily market (Pazar) stocks vegetables, cheese, and olive oil from island producers. Fresh fish arrives daily at the harbor's eastern end. Supermarkets stock international staples. Local bakeries open early; stop for fresh bread before dawn departures.

Winds and Sailing Conditions

The Kvarner passage, between Cres and the mainland, funnels the Tramuntana (northwesterly wind) into a powerful sailing corridor. In summer, moderate Tramuntana winds of 12–16 knots create excellent sailing conditions. Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) often bring lighter, more variable winds but fewer boats.

Catamarans handle the Kvarner's occasional steep waves better than monohulls, and their stability lets you sail confidently in moderate conditions. Winter passages are possible but rare; most catamaran charters operate May through October.

Local Food and Wine Culture

Cres lamb is legendary across the Adriatic. Raised on sparse island vegetation, the meat develops a distinctive, delicate flavor. Every konoba worth visiting features pašticada (slow-braised lamb) or simply grilled lamb chops. Pair with local red wine—Cres produces small quantities of Plavac Mali and house wines at family vineyards.

Olive oil from Cres dates back to Venetian times. Many anchorages near small settlements like Valun offer the chance to buy directly from producers. Fresh mussels and local fish, grilled simply aboard your catamaran, taste incomparably better here than elsewhere in the Mediterranean.

Practical Logistics

Cres Town airport is small; most arrivals come via Split or Rijeka airports (100–120 km distant by road). A car rental for drive-down time makes sense. The island's narrow mountain road connects Cres Town to the north and south; driving takes patience but rewards you with forest views and clifftop villages.

Ferry services link Cres and Lošinj to the mainland at Brestova (northern route) and Mali Lošinj to Zadar (summer service). These ferries sometimes accommodate vehicles, making road-and-boat combinations possible for multi-destination itineraries.

FAQ: Catamaran Charters from Cres

What size catamaran suits a mixed-ability group?

A 35–40-foot catamaran with three or four cabins accommodates six to eight people comfortably. Catamarans' wider cockpits and lower freeboard make boarding and dinghy transfers easier than monohulls, ideal for families or those less confident at sea.

Is Cres suitable for a week-long charter?

Absolutely. A week allows leisurely progression through Lošinj, Unije, and Susak, with unhurried afternoons ashore in villages like Beli, Lubenice, and Valun. Many sailors find one week too short and return to Boat4You to extend into a fortnight.

What certifications do I need to charter a catamaran from Cres?

An International Certificate of Competence (ICC) or national sailing license is typically required for bareboat charters. Boat4You's process reviews credentials and can arrange a professional skipper for less experienced crews—an excellent choice for first-time Adriatic sailing.

Are the Tramuntana winds difficult for catamaran sailors?

No. Catamarans' inherent stability and balanced design make them ideal for Tramuntana sailing. The wind, while present, rarely exceeds 18 knots in summer. Reef-down if gusts strengthen; the catamarans' shallower draft lets you retreat to shallow anchorages when needed.

Where can I refuel and resupply between Cres and Lošinj?

Both islands have fuel docks and supermarkets. Mali Lošinj is larger and better-stocked; Cres Town is smaller but often more atmospheric. Stopping at island villages like Beli and Lubenice means no fuel docks, but that's half the charm of a slower pace.
