Kornati Sailing Routes and Itineraries

From ACI Marina Jezera, Kornati routes are straightforward. Southern routes (Days 1–2) explore Levrnaka (Kornati's largest island), Mala Proversa, and surrounding rocky passages—ideal for establishing confidence and practicing anchoring skills. Central routes (Days 3–4) venture into the Žižanj island group and deeper passages, where isolation increases and village contacts diminish. Northern routes (Days 5–7) reach Telašćica cove, Kornati's northern crown jewel, famous for its dramatic 90-metre cliff faces, sheer anchorages, and pristine waters.

A typical seven-day itinerary combines all three: southern exploration, central island passages, and northern Telašćica finale, returning to ACI Marina Jezera for final evening and morning departure. Shorter charters (3–4 days) focus on southern or central Kornati. Extended charters (10–14 days) complete Kornati circumnavigations and venture toward Vis, Brač, or northward toward the Šibenik archipelago.

Provisioning and Onboard Sustainability

ACI Marina Jezera's adjacent village provides excellent provisioning opportunities. The harbor area hosts a small supermarket (basics: bread, cheese, cured meats, produce), a bakery (daily bread, pastries), and fish markets where daily catch is sold directly by fishermen. For extended stays, most sailors supplement village shopping with once- or twice-weekly runs to Murter town supermarket (8 kilometres distant, easily reached by taxi or rental car).

Onboard, most Boat4You yachts carry fresh water tanks (typically 300–500 litres), allowing 3–4 days between marina refueling without strict rationing. Diesel engines are economical—burning 8–12 litres daily in moderate use—making fuel costs predictable. Solar panels and wind generators (common on modern charters) reduce generator dependency, quieting night anchorages.

Sailing Conditions and Best Seasons

ACI Marina Jezera's position yields consistent sailing advantages. The Maestral wind pattern—reliable afternoon northwest breeze—dominates June through August, typically 12–18 knots. This wind provides excellent sailing conditions for intermediate crews: strong enough for substantial daily progress, mild enough to be manageable. Spring (May) and autumn (September–October) feature more variable conditions but fewer crowds and pleasant water temperatures (May 19–20°C, September 22–23°C).

Summer sailing is busiest; late June and September offer ideal compromise between weather reliability and crowd levels. Winter (November–March) is possible for experienced crews but brings shorter daylight, occasional severe Bura storms, and cooler water temperatures.
