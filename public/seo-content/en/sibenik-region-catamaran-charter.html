Why Catamarans Excel in the Šibenik Region

Shallow draft is the defining advantage. A typical 40-foot catamaran draws 1.0-1.3 meters, compared to 1.8-2.2 meters for a monohull of equivalent length. This difference transforms Šibenik sailing. Kornati's most prized anchorages—Levrnaka, Sakarun, Katina, and dozens of unnamed coves—feature shallow holding (4-6 meters deep, sand and mud bottom). Monohulls either cannot anchor in these spots or require careful positioning; catamarans drop anchor confidently, secure in their shallow footprint.

Catamaran stability is the second factor. Unlike monohulls, which heel 20-30 degrees in brisk wind, catamarans maintain near-level platforms even in 18-20 knot breezes—a characteristic that transforms the sailing experience for non-sailors, families with children, and anyone prone to motion sickness. The wide beam (roughly 50-60% of length, compared to 30-40% for monohulls) creates sprawling cabin spaces, often with walk-around queen berths and galley-forward layouts that eliminate the cramped quarters of traditional yacht cabins. A 40-foot catamaran sleeps 8-10 in reasonable comfort; a monohull of the same length sleeps 6-8 with more compact arrangements.

Twin engines provide engine redundancy and superior maneuverability. In tight marina berths or crowded Kornati anchorages, differential throttle (one engine idle, one full ahead) allows pivot turns impossible for single-engine monohulls. Fuel efficiency is competitive with modern monohulls; twin 75-100 hp Volvo or Yammer engines cruise at 7-8 knots using 8-12 gallons per hour combined—adequate for calm-weather progress when wind fails.

Speed is modest: most cruising catamarans achieve 8-10 knots under engine, 7-9 knots under sail in moderate wind. This is not a disadvantage; Šibenik sailing rewards pace and exploration over mileage accumulation. Day-sail distances of 12-18 nautical miles are typical and comfortable.

Catamaran Routes: Kornati Day-Hopping and Island Clusters

The Northern Kornati Loop is the entry-level catamaran route: depart Šibenik or Murter, sail 12-15 nautical miles south to Kornati's northern cluster (Veli Drvenik, Dugi Otok fringe, Mana), explore for 1-2 days, then return. The passage is straightforward; day-light sailing suffices. Northern anchorages are well-protected and scenic.

Central Kornati Hopping extends the scope: Šibenik to Levrnaka (20 nm), overnight at Katina or Sakarun (each offering excellent holding and island views), then explore adjacent islands. The route demands only modest navigation skills; channels are wide, and hazards are sparse. A 3-4 day catamaran itinerary covers Kornati's highlights comfortably, with each day offering 10-15 nautical miles of sailing and ample time for anchoring, snorkeling, and relaxation.

The Rogoznica Extension combines Kornati with southern sailing: base at Marina Frapa (Rogoznica), sail north into central Kornati (30 nm), spend 2-3 days exploring, then return via Primošten or push toward Split-region islands (Brač, Hvar). This route suits week-long charters and experienced crews.

The Skradin Inland Adventure is catamaran-specific. Shallow-draft multihulls navigate the Krka River's winding channels, passing beneath limestone cliffs and viewing the famous Krka National Park waterfalls. The 15-nautical-mile passage to Skradin takes 2-3 hours at idle speed, combining boating with cultural immersion unique to the region.

Catamaran Logistics: Marinas, Fuel, and Services

Marina Mandalina (Šibenik) is the region's largest catamaran base. Central location, modern docking infrastructure, fuel dock, water, shore power, and provisioning shops make it the practical departure point for city-focused crews. The marina sits adjacent to Šibenik's cathedral and historic waterfront.

Marina Frapa (Rogoznica), 20 nautical miles south, specializes in sailing yachts but maintains a catamaran contingent. Quieter than Šibenik, Rogoznica offers village charm with professional services. The marina is ideal for crews preferring working-harbor atmosphere and seeking easier access to southern (Primošten, Brač) routes.

Marina Betina (Murter) caters to smaller operators and provides catamaran berths with intimate, locally-focused character. Betina is the gateway marina for Kornati-focused crews; distances to prime anchorages are minimal (8-12 nm).

ACI Marina Vodice and ACI Marina Žut offer intermediate options, catering to crews seeking quieter bases between Šibenik and Rogoznica. Both have catamaran fleets and provisioning.

ACI Marina Skradin specializes in shallow-draft multihulls exploring the Krka River. Fuel, water, and service facilities are tailored to vessels accessing inland channels.

Fleet Specifications and Comfort Features

Modern Šibenik catamarans range from 34-foot compact cruisers to 50+ foot luxury multihulls. A typical charter catamaran (40-45 feet) features:

Layout: 3-4 double cabins (including forward master with walk-around berth), 1-2 heads with showers, spacious saloon, fully-equipped galley with refrigerator, electric stove, and microwave.

Deck: Roller-furling jib and mainsail, twin steering wheels (helm on each hull for weight distribution), spacious foredeck with sun lounges, aft cockpit with large dining table, sun awning.

Navigation & Safety: GPS chartplotter, VHF radio, life jackets, flares, liferaft, first-aid kit, comprehensive tool kit. Many carry spinnaker poles and asymmetrical sails for light-air sailing.

Power: Twin 75-100 hp diesel engines, 400-600 liter fuel capacity (typical 120-150 nm range at economy cruise), water-maker units (producing fresh water underway), solar panels and wind generators for electrical self-sufficiency.

Comfort: Air-conditioning in forward cabins, electric cabin fans, hot water, holding tank sewage systems. Newer fleets include Bose sound systems, flat-screen entertainment, and espresso machines.

Season and Pricing

The Šibenik region operates a standard Mediterranean charter season (May-October). May and June: Water temperature 18-21°C, Maestral winds 10-16 knots, fewer crowds. July-August: Warmest season, water 24-26°C, busiest period, Maestral 15-20 knots, peak pricing. September-October: Still warm (22-24°C), winds moderating, fewer tourists, second-best season for comfort and value.

Pricing for a 40-foot catamaran ranges €2,800-€5,000 weekly in low season (May, June, September) and €5,000-€8,000 in high season (July-August). Fuel, mooring fees, and national park permits are typically additional. Crewed charters (with skipper and/or hostess) add €400-€700 daily. Frequently Asked Questions

Do catamarans sail as well as monohulls in light wind?

Catamarans are slightly slower in light air (under 8 knots) due to greater weight and windage. In moderate-to-strong winds (10+ knots), performance is comparable to monohulls. Šibenik's summer Maestral provides reliable 10-18 knot winds; light-air passages are rare. If sailing performance in marginal wind is critical, bareboat monohulls are an alternative; most crews prefer catamaran stability and comfort over peak sailing speed.

Can I take a catamaran up the Krka River?

Yes, if your catamaran has shallow draft (under 1.5 meters). Many Šibenik catamarans (particularly 34-40 foot models) are sized for river access. Confirm draft with your charter operator before booking if Krka River exploration is essential. The river passage is slow (idle speed only, typically 2-3 hours for 15 nm to Skradin) but rewarding for cultural immersion.

Are catamarans more expensive to charter than monohulls?

Catamarans typically cost 10-20% more than comparable-length monohulls. The premium reflects superior comfort, spacious cabins, shallow draft, and operating costs (dual engines, water-maker power consumption). For families and larger groups, per-person cost is often lower in a catamaran (sleeping 8-10) than a monohull (sleeping 6-8).

What happens in strong wind (over 20 knots)?

Catamarans remain stable but slower. Most crews reduce sail or motor in winds exceeding 25 knots. Šibenik's summer Maestral rarely exceeds 20 knots; sustained gales are uncommon May-October. Spring and autumn Bura winds are stronger; experienced crews handle them confidently; casual crews should avoid May and October if strong-wind discomfort is a concern.

Do I need a sailing license for a catamaran charter?

Bareboat charters require proof of sailing competency (IYC, RYA Day Skipper, or equivalent). Crewed charters (with professional skipper) eliminate this requirement. Boat4You partners assist with certification verification and arrange crewed options if needed.

Can we take a catamaran into Greek waters?

Yes, if your charter contract permits. Clear out of Croatia at Zadar or Šibenik port authority; check in at the nearest Greek port (typically Corfu in the Ionian). Ensure insurance covers Greek waters. Multi-country sailing requires additional paperwork; Boat4You partners advise on documentation requirements.
