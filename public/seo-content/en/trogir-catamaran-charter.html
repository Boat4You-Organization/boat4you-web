Catamaran Charter from Trogir

Trogir sits on a small islet between the Croatian mainland and the island of Čiovo, its medieval town center a UNESCO World Heritage site. For catamaran charterers, this location offers an ideal balance: you're minutes from ACI Marina Trogir and Marina Baotić in Seget (with 750 berths), yet close enough to Split airport (15 minutes by car) that international guests arrive smoothly. The Adriatic spreads in all directions from here — 5 nautical miles to Šolta's protected coves, 15 to Brač's limestone cliffs and Pučišća stone quarries, 25 to Hvar Town's narrow harbor and evening promenade, and 40 to the Blue Cave at Vis Komiža. Catamarans suit Trogir's waters because they rest level in tight anchorages, offer cabin space for family groups, and carry enough fuel and water for multi-day cruises across the central Adriatic.

This region receives steady Maestral winds (10–18 knots from the northwest by afternoon), creating textbook sailing conditions most days from May through October. Winters bring occasional Bora gusts from the northeast, but spring and fall deliver weeks of forgiving, predictable weather. Catamaran charters from Trogir suit first-time bareboat crews and experienced sailors alike — the wide hulls give teenagers real cabin space and the galley actually functions for a week of full meals. a catamaran from Trogir delivers reach, comfort, and proven routes to islands that hold their own against anywhere in the Mediterranean.

Why a Catamaran Suits Trogir's Waters and Routes

The central Adriatic around Trogir is a catamaran's natural home. These islands — Šolta, Brač, Hvar, Vis — are separated by short open-water jumps: typically 5 to 40 nautical miles. A catamaran's shallow draft (1.3 to 1.5 meters for most charter cats) lets you approach Drvenik Veli's Blue Lagoon and anchor in Zavratnica Bay without grounding in the turquoise shallows where monohulls sit frustrated offshore. Their beam (width) provides stability in the thermal chop that builds up by midday when the Maestral hits, and their twin engines mean you can hold position while guests swim or dinghy ashore, rather than constantly resetting your anchor swing.

The cabin layout matters here. Three-cabin, four-cabin, or even five-cabin catamarans sleep 8 to 12 people comfortably — far better for multi-generational family weeks than a monohull's narrow berths. Galleys sit higher on a catamaran, reducing stooping and seasickness risk in the cabin. Air conditioning runs off the generator without draining batteries as fast. Many charter catamarans include a flybridge (upper deck), which becomes your nighttime gathering spot as the sun drops behind the Dalmatian hills, the Maestral fades, and the sea turns mirror-flat.

Top Routes from Trogir for Catamaran Sailing

Drvenik Veli, Blue Lagoon, and the Šolta Triangle (3–5 days)

The closest exotic destination is Drvenik Veli, lying 8 nautical miles southwest. Its southern shore reveals the Blue Lagoon — a shallow turquoise lagoon framed by white sand and limestone cliffs, sheltered enough for children to splash without hazard and deep enough for a proper swim from the beach. Anchor outside and dinghy in, or navigate carefully (read the tide and the color gradient) to approach closer. Most charterers make this their first night stop, arriving by late afternoon with a whole evening to explore. The water temperature typically reaches 22–24°C by August. In the mornings before the Maestral builds, visibility extends 30 meters down into the sand, and small fish school around your dinghy.

Just across the blue gap sits Šolta, the quieter sibling to nearby Brač. Its western settlements — Maslinica, with its small medieval fortress and waterfront restaurants, and the quieter village of Grohote inland — see fewer cruise-boat crowds. Anchor in Stomorska Bay on Šolta's northeast coast and walk uphill to find family konobas where peka (meat baked under a bell of hot coals) is still prepared daily. Šolta's olive groves slope to the water's edge; the island produces oil that tastes peppery and vivid. A three-day loop — Trogir to Drvenik Veli, Šolta, and back — covers no more than 25 nautical miles total and leaves you with four days to explore longer.

Hvar Town and the Pakleni Islands (4–6 days)

Hvar Town sits 25 nautical miles northwest from Trogir, typically a six-hour passage in normal Maestral conditions. The approach is marked — you'll see the Spaniola fortress crowning the ridge above the harbor well before you reach the breakwater. This is one of the Adriatic's most crowded anchorages in July and August, but it's crowded for good reason: the harborfront promenade hums with restaurants, the streets behind climb steeply past herb-scented gardens, and the locals have refined the evening passeggiata (waterfront stroll) into an art form. Arrive early (by 4 pm) to secure an anchor spot near the town center, or navigate deeper into the bay to find quieter swinging room.

The Pakleni Islands, just opposite Hvar Town, are smaller and far less crowded. Their sandy beaches and rocky coves — Stipica, Zaglav, Vlaka — anchor well in the afternoon Maestral. A catamaran's dinghy makes hopping between them effortless: Zaglav is known for a small beach konoba where fresh fish is grilled over coals. The water around the Pakleni stays remarkably clear even in high summer.

Vis Town and the Blue Cave (5–7 days, longer passage)

Vis Town sits 40 nautical miles from Trogir, a passage that takes 8 to 10 hours in typical conditions. The island was off-limits to tourists until 1989 (Yugoslav military), and the isolation shows: Vis Town retains a quieter, less-touristed character than Hvar, its harborfront still populated as much by fishing boats as by charter yachts. The town climbs steeply from the water, narrow streets connecting terraced houses painted in fading pastels. Local wines — Vugava (crisp, mineral) and Viski Plavac (red, full-bodied) — are produced in tiny quantities and rarely sold far from the island.

The Blue Cave (Biševo) lies 15 nautical miles south, near the small island of Biševo. The cave's entrance is only 1.5 meters high at high tide; entrance fees (roughly 100 kuna per person) are collected by local boatmen who know the swell conditions. A catamaran draws too much water to enter; you'll dinghy or hire a local skiff. The morning light, entering from beneath the waterline, turns the entire cave interior a luminous blue-green. The phenomenon is real, not oversold — worth the early start and the passage time. Anchor off Biševo in a small protected cove and explore the cave first thing; by midday, dozens of tour boats arrive from the mainland.

Practical Details: Weather, Season, and Licensing

Weather patterns and best season

The Maestral (northwesterly) dominates May through October, typically building to 12–18 knots by early afternoon and fading after sunset. June and September offer the most stable conditions: sunny, wind predictable, water warm (21–23°C), and fewer tourists than July and August. July and August are warmest (water reaches 25°C+), but the Maestral can occasionally kick up to 22–25 knots, causing thermal chop and making anchorages in shallow bays less comfortable. Bora winds (from the northeast) are rare in summer but possible in April and October; they arrive suddenly, blow hard (20–40 knots), and are gone within 24 hours. Winter (November–March) is doable but requires patience: cold snaps, less predictable wind, and many restaurants in smaller towns close until Easter.

Book early (by March) for July and August. May, June, September, and early October offer better wind consistency and fewer crowds — fewer anchor spaces taken by charter fleets, quieter konobas, and more chance of solitude at evening anchorages.

Provisioning and supplies

Trogir's town center has a Konzum supermarket (chain grocery) a five-minute walk from the marina breakwater. It stocks fresh vegetables, bread, cheese, wine, and basic provisions. However, before departing for the outer islands (Vis, Komiža), provision heavily: Vis Town has a smaller selection, and Biševo has nothing. Hvar Town and the Pakleni Islands have shops, but they're pricier than the mainland. Bread in Croatia is exceptional — local bakeries deliver fresh daily to the marinas or you can hike to a village panaria (bakery). Buy wine and olive oil from small producers if you find them; the supermarket selection is adequate but forgettable.

Licensing and bareboat requirements

Bareboat catamaran charter in Croatian waters requires an International Sailing Certificate (ASA 104 or equivalent, recognised by the International Sailing Federation) or proof of equivalent sailing experience. Most charterers from the US and UK will have ASA cards; European skippers often hold STCW certificates. If you don't hold a recognized credential, Boat4You's booking process explains bareboat eligibility and can arrange a skipper or mate to join for the week at a daily rate (typically €150–300 per day, depending on the skipper's experience).

Motor sailers and catamarans above 12 meters also require a General Operator's Certificate (technically, an RYA Yachtmaster or equivalent). Most large charter boats come with a local skipper on call via radio, available for nighttime harbor entry if you're uncertain of the approach.

Pricing and What to Expect

A 40-foot catamaran (four cabins) departing from Trogir costs roughly 4,500–9,000 euros per week, depending on the season and boat condition. Peak season (July–August) commands the top end. Shoulder months (May, June, early September) run 5,500–7,000 euros. April and October drop to 4,500–6,000 euros. These figures assume a bareboat rental; adding a skipper adds €150–300 per day (€1,050–2,100 per week).

Extras include: fuel (200–400 euros, depending on your engine use and passages), provisioning (budget 200–400 euros per week for groceries and wine if you self-cater), transit logs/fees (most marinas charge 15–35 euros per night), and the final cleaning fee (typically waived if you return the boat reasonably tidy, but charged 300–500 euros if deep cleaning is required). Marina fees in Hvar and Vis add up; expect 35–50 euros per night in peak season.

Sample Itinerary: Seven Days from Trogir

Day 1 (Monday): Trogir to Drvenik Veli. Depart the marina by 10 am, sail southwest for 1.5 hours, anchor in the Blue Lagoon by early afternoon. Swim, explore the limestone cliffs, and dinner at one of the island's two restaurants (simple grilled fish and pašticada, the Dalmatian slow-cooked beef stew).

Day 2 (Tuesday): Drvenik Veli to Šolta (Maslinica). Morning sail northwest, 8 nautical miles, arriving by noon. Lunch in Maslinica's small harbor, explore the village fortress. Afternoon swim off the beach. Evening walk inland to find peka — the dish is typically ready by 6 pm, baked under a bell of hot coals for three hours.

Day 3 (Wednesday): Šolta day sailing. Motor or sail to Stomorska Bay on the northeast coast, a quieter anchorage. Hike the olive groves above the village, sample local oil at a roadside stand. Return to the boat for sunset drinks on deck.

Day 4 (Thursday): Šolta to Brač (Bol or Pučišća). Sail eastward, 6 nautical miles. Bol is famous for Zlatni Rat beach (a long shingle spit that shifts with the wind). Pučišća, on the northwest coast, is quieter — known for its stone quarries and small fishing harbor. Anchor overnight in either.

Day 5 (Friday): Brač to Hvar Town. Morning sail northwest, 15 nautical miles, typically 3–4 hours. Arrive by early afternoon, secure an anchor spot or mooring buoy. Explore the harborfront, climb to the Spaniola fortress (steep but rewarding views), and dinner at a harborside restaurant. Pašticada and local Plavac Mali wine are staples.

Day 6 (Saturday): Hvar day sailing and the Pakleni Islands. Explore Pakleni anchorages (Zaglav, Stipica), swim, and beach lunch at Zaglav's konoba. Return to Hvar Town by late afternoon.

Day 7 (Sunday): Hvar Town to Trogir. An easy 25-nautical-mile return passage, typically 5–6 hours in morning Maestral. Arrive at the marina by early evening, return the boat.

Local Flavor: Food and Wine

Dalmatian cuisine centers on what the sea and the limestone hills provide. Gregada (fisherman's stew made from whatever catch didn't sell at market, simmered with tomato, garlic, and white wine) appears on nearly every konoba menu. Brodet (another stew, often made with scorpion fish and monkfish) is more refined, with red wine replacing white. Peka — meat (lamb, veal, or octopus) baked under a bell of hot coals — is the ceremonial dish; it takes three hours and emerges impossibly tender.

Plavac Mali is the regional red wine, full-bodied and warm, grown on terraced slopes. Pošip, from nearby Korčula, is the crisp white to pair with grilled fish and seafood. Babić (from the Šibenik hinterland) runs slightly earthier than Plavac Mali but with similar structure. A glass of local wine at sunset, anchored off a quiet island, is the meal itself.

FAQ

Is a sailing license required to charter a catamaran from Trogir?

Yes, most charter companies require an International Sailing Certificate (ASA 104) or equivalent proof of sailing competence for bareboat charter of catamarans above 12 meters. If you lack a certificate, Boat4You and other providers can arrange a skipper to travel with you, or you can take a one-week ASA course beforehand (many sailing schools operate in Dalmatia).

What is the best time to charter a catamaran from Trogir?

May, June, and September offer the best balance of wind stability, warm water, and manageable crowds. July and August are busiest and most expensive. April and October are cooler but still viable; winter (November–March) is for experienced sailors who embrace unpredictability.

How far can I sail in a week from Trogir?

A typical week covers the islands of Šolta, Brač, Hvar, and the Pakleni Islands — roughly 80–100 nautical miles of total passage and exploration. Reaching Vis and the Blue Cave is possible but requires a longer passage (40 nm) and means fewer stops elsewhere. A week is better spent thoroughly exploring the central islands than rushing to the far extremes.

What should I provision for a week-long catamaran charter?

Buy bread, fresh vegetables, cheese, and eggs from Trogir's main supermarket before departure. Stock wine and olive oil generously — they're cheaper on the mainland. Expect to resupply in Hvar Town if your trip runs long. Most marinas have a small provisions stand; fish, seafood, and daily deliveries from local suppliers make stopping at restaurants the easier option for seafood meals.

How much fuel should I budget for a catamaran week?

Budget 200–400 euros for fuel, depending on your speed and engine hours. Catamarans with twin 40–50 hp engines burn roughly 15–20 liters per hour under power. A week of mixed sailing and motoring (say, 60 engine hours total) consumes 900–1200 liters at Croatian fuel prices (roughly €1.40–1.60 per liter in 2024).
