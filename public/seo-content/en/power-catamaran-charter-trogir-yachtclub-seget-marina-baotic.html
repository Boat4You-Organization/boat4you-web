Power Catamarans: The Hybrid Advantage

Power catamarans occupy a middle ground between traditional sailing multihulls and rigid motor yachts. They retain the catamaran's shallow draft (typically 1.0–1.3 metres), allowing access to protected anchorages that deeper-hulled vessels cannot safely reach. They maintain the natural stability — minimal heeling, broad platforms, comfortable at-anchor motion — that families and non-sailor crews value. However, they replace sails with powerful diesel engines (typically twin 300–500 horsepower), delivering cruising speeds of 12–16 knots and the ability to follow precise itineraries regardless of wind.

This combination is increasingly popular. Sailing without sails removes the skill requirement; guests participate in navigation and anchoring decisions without technical demands. The speed allows multi-anchorage daily itineraries (Split to Hvar by noon, Hvar to Vis by evening) that pure sailing renders impossible. The stability eliminates seasickness and rolling that compromise comfort during week-long stays. The shallow draft opens anchorages — protected coves off Hvar, isolated spots near Vis — that traditional yachts must bypass.

Typical Power Catamaran Itineraries from Trogir

The Seven-Day Hvar-Vis Circuit

Most power catamaran charters from Trogir follow a familiar routing but accelerated by motor power. Day 1 (Afternoon departure): Cast off by 14:00, motor south toward Šolta (roughly 20 nautical miles from Trogir, 3–4 hours), arriving at Maslinica fishing village by evening. Anchor in the sheltered cove south of town, dine ashore at a waterfront konoba. Day 2: Motor to Brač Bol (15 nautical miles from Šolta, 2–3 hours under power). Arrive by mid-morning, allowing full afternoon at Zlatni Rat beach. Many charters remain overnight at Brač or transit immediately south toward Hvar, depending on crew energy and interest.

Day 3: Motor to Hvar's Pakleni Islands (25–30 nautical miles, 4–5 hours under power). Arrive by mid-afternoon, anchor at a chosen protected cove. The crew may coordinate shore dinners at waterfront konobas or prepare meals aboard. Days 4–5: Rest days and exploration from Pakleni Islands base. Dinghy access to Hvar Town (10 minutes away), optional car rental for island interior exploration, swimming and snorkelling at multiple coves. Evening meals feature local Dalmatian pešticada and Plavac Mali wine.

Day 6: Motor to Vis (40 nautical miles, 5–6 hours under steady power). Arrive by late afternoon at Komiža (working fishing harbour, atmospheric, moderately crowded) or Vis Town (eastern coast, quieter, more tourist-friendly). Dinner aboard or ashore features fresh seafood, local Vis Prošek dessert wine, and the island's relaxed character. Vis feels genuinely remote; many guests describe it as the high point of their week.

Day 7 (Return): Motor north back toward Trogir (roughly 50–60 nautical miles, 7–8 hours). Depart early to arrive by evening, or depart late morning and arrive early evening, allowing final meal ashore at Trogir Old Town before crew change.

Power Catamaran Specifications and Comfort Features

Power catamarans from Trogir typically range 40–50 feet, with sleeping capacity for 8–12 people across 4–5 cabins. Most feature twin diesel engines (600–1,000 combined horsepower), modern electronics (chartplotter, autopilot, VHF, radar), and air-conditioning in main areas. Fuel capacity typically spans 400–600 litres, adequate for 4–5 days of mixed cruising without refuelling; Trogir's fuel dock provides convenient mid-charter top-ups if required.

Cabins tend toward generous sizing compared to traditional sailing yachts — double berths in master and guest cabins, ensuite heads with electric flush and small showers, adequate headroom for sitting upright. Saloons feature entertainment systems (television, sound) and comfortable seating. Galleys are large enough for multi-course meal preparation. Deck spaces — flybridge or upper deck — provide sunbathing and entertainment areas distinct from interior cabins.

Stabilizer systems on modern power catamarans reduce rolling significantly; even in moderate seas, the ride remains substantially more comfortable than traditional mono-hull yachts. This feature particularly matters during the 40-nautical-mile open-water passage to Vis; guests remain comfortable while transiting without the motion sickness or constant bracing that sailing vessels demand.

Do I need a powerboat license to charter a power catamaran from Trogir?

Most charter companies require a powerboat license or equivalent national credential demonstrating boating experience. However, many offer skippered options (professional captain included) with no license requirement. Check with your operator; bareboat versus skippered options vary by company.

How fast are power catamarans, and what are typical transit times?

Power catamarans cruise at 12–16 knots depending on engine efficiency and sea state. Typical transits from Trogir: to Brač (35nm) takes 4–5 hours; to Hvar (60nm) takes 7–8 hours; to Vis (100nm) takes 11–13 hours. Shorter routes (Trogir to Šolta, to nearby Ciovo) take 2–4 hours depending on anchorage selected.

Is a power catamaran suitable for families with young children?

Yes. The natural stability (minimal heeling), shallow draft allowing protected-water cruising, and spacious cabins make power catamarans family-friendly. No sailing license required, no technical crew involvement, and consistent schedule foster family comfort. Confirm sleeping arrangement options (doubles, twins, cabins suitable for young children) with the operator.

Can I visit Hvar and Vis in a single week from Trogir?

Yes. The motor power makes the circuit achievable: Trogir → Šolta (1 day) → Brač (1 day) → Hvar (2 days) → Vis (2 days) → return to Trogir (1 day). The schedule is achievable but relatively packed; crews comfortable with daily motoring enjoy this itinerary. Alternatively, focus on Trogir's quieter northern islands (Ciovo, Blue Lagoon) for a more relaxed pace.

What local foods should I try while chartering from Trogir?

Pešticada (slow-braised beef with plums) is quintessentially Dalmatian. Fresh grilled octopus appears on every menu. Local wines: Plavac Mali (bold red), Pošip (crisp white), and if you reach Vis, Prošek (dessert wine). Pag cheese, seafood risotto (particularly with scallops), and Maraschino liqueur (from local maraschino cherries) are regional specialities worth seeking out.
