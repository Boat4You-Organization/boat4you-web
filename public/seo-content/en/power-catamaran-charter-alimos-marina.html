Licensing and Crew Requirements

Power catamarans, like all motor vessels, require a licensed skipper or captain. Unlike sailing yachts (which demand specific sailing qualifications), power catamaran charters almost typically include a professional captain as part of the package. If your group includes an experienced powerboat skipper with valid credentials (RYA Powerboat Level 2 or equivalent), some operators may allow you to helm under supervision; most, however, prefer professional captaincy for insurance and safety reasons.

Crewing is standard: a professional captain handles navigation, mooring, and engine management. Guest participation in helming and decision-making is encouraged; the captain's role is control and expertise, not command isolation. Seasons and Weather Patterns

Summer (June–September) offers calm seas and reliable conditions; power catamarans cruise easily in wind states that would challenge sailing yachts. Spring and autumn are equally pleasant; fewer crowds and lower prices are bonuses. Winter cruising is possible for groups comfortable with heavier seas; the Etesian wind pattern abates, and weather is more variable, but many Mediterranean power cruisers operate year-round.

Can a power catamaran navigate tight harbours and narrow channels?

Yes, with expert helming. Power catamarans are more manoeuvrable than large motor yachts; dual engines provide independent thrust control (no complex stern-drive steering). Your captain routinely navigates the narrow Limen Channel at Poros and tight mooring fields. However, very shallow anchorages (under 1.2 metres) may be inaccessible due to shallow draft limitations.

Are power catamarans fuel-efficient compared to mono-hull motor yachts?

Yes. Twin narrow hulls reduce drag; at cruising speed (10–12 knots), fuel consumption is typically 1.5–2 litres/nm for a 40-footer, competitive with smaller mono-hull cruisers. Fast-boat scenarios (15+ knots) increase consumption; most charters optimise for leisurely pace and fuel economy, maximising range and minimising refuelling stops.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.
