ARC Heritage and Bluewater Sailing Culture

The Atlantic Rally for Cruisers has transformed Las Palmas into a pilgrimage destination for sailors contemplating Atlantic and Caribbean passages. Starting in 1986 with fewer than 100 participants, the ARC now deploys 200-250 yachts each November, ranging from modest 30-footers to substantial 70-foot cruising vessels.

This concentration of oceangoing talent creates a unique cultural environment. Las Palmas' marina bars buzz with discussion of passage planning, weather routeing, provisioning strategies, and Caribbean logistics. Whether you're preparing for a transatlantic crossing or simply absorbing sailing wisdom, Las Palmas' sailing community is unmatched in European Atlantic sailing circles.

Chartering from Las Palmas during ARC season (August-November) exposes you to preparation activities, final provisioning rushes, and the vibrant pre-departure atmosphere. Off-season charters offer quieter harbor ambiance while retaining full access to world-class facilities and experienced local knowledge.

Sailing Routes and Passage Options from Las Palmas

The central location of Las Palmas creates optimal routing to diverse Canary Islands destinations. Southward passages reach Maspalomas' renowned anchorage (45 nautical miles, 6-10 hours) with its distinctive sand dunes, Playa del Inglés beach resort infrastructure, and sheltered overnight holding. Longer southern passages venture toward the Costa Calma region of Fuerteventura, featuring wild, undeveloped coastlines and pristine white-sand anchorages.

Westward passages from Las Palmas cross open Atlantic waters toward La Palma Island (80 nautical miles) and El Hierro (120 nautical miles), the westernmost Canaries. These remote islands experience less tourist traffic, maintaining traditional Canarian character and offering exceptional anchorages. La Palma's San Juan port provides resupply; El Hierro remains largely undeveloped, rewarding adventurous sailors with isolation and natural beauty.

Northward passages reach Tenerife's north coast (90 nautical miles toward Garachico's historic harbor) or Puerto Deportivo Radazul (100 nautical miles), where Teide volcano dominates the landscape. Combined itineraries connecting all major islands typically consume 10-14 days, providing comprehensive Canary Islands exploration.

For bluewater sailors, Las Palmas represents the departure point for Atlantic passages toward the Caribbean, Azores, or African coast. Weather and Seasonal Sailing Conditions

Las Palmas experiences year-round northeast trade winds, typically 12-20 knots. The Azores High-pressure system creates remarkably consistent wind patterns, particularly October through April. Winter months (December-February) bring stronger winds (15-25 knots) and larger Atlantic swells (2-4 meters), suited to experienced sailors comfortable with vigorous passages.

Spring (March-May) and autumn (September-October) offer moderate conditions with lighter swells and comfortable sailing. Summer (June-August) experiences weaker winds (8-15 knots) and occasionally glassy conditions requiring longer passages, though water temperatures reach pleasant 23-25°C levels ideal for swimming and water sports.

Visibility is consistently excellent. Hurricane season affects the eastern Atlantic but spares the Canaries, positioned 400+ miles east of typical storm tracks. Gale-force conditions (40+ knots) are rare; typical trade wind sailing maintains manageable conditions suitable for well-prepared oceangoing yachts.
