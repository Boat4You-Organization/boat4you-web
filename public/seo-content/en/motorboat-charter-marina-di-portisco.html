Why a Motorboat from Marina di Portisco

Motorboats (day boats and speedboats) suit day trips, island hops, and watersports rather than week-long cruising. They are typically open or semi-covered and hired by the day or half-day. Flexibility, day-trip cost control, and reach per litre of fuel is the main reason charterers pick this platform for Italy's cruising grounds.

Typical motorboat charters from Marina di Portisco run 5 to 12 metres, open or semi-covered, priced at roughly 200 to 800 euro per day depending on size and engine. Bareboat usually permitted with the country's basic licence; skippered day charters are the norm for first-timers. The berthing configuration at Marina di Portisco handles motorboats without issue — allow enough turning room in the basin on busy Saturday turnarounds.

Fit for the local cruising area

Tyrrhenian and Sardinian waters see Libeccio from the southwest and Maestrale from the northwest. Afternoon thermals dominate calm days. Motorboats work particularly well when daily passages stay under 25 nautical miles, which matches most Sardinia / Corsica-area itineraries.

Routes from Marina di Portisco

Marina di Portisco opens access to the surrounding coast and islands within a 20–40 nautical mile radius. Short early-morning hops and long afternoon anchorages are the rule rather than the exception — motorboat cruising in this region generally favours staying in sheltered water during the windiest hours.

Coastal exploration

Within a day's passage of Marina di Portisco, expect a mix of small fishing harbours, open bays for anchoring, and longer passages to larger destination towns.

Technical considerations

Motorboats at Marina di Portisco handle the region's conditions well when properly prepared. Check reefing points, engine hours, and safety gear during the check-in briefing — marinas in Italy run careful handover procedures and the briefing is the right moment to raise questions.

Anchoring and berthing

Anchor tackle on charter motorboats is generally sized adequately for Italy's typical bay depths (6–15 metres). Med-mooring stern-to is standard in marinas; the briefing at Marina di Portisco covers the local conventions.

Provisioning and logistics

Marina di Portisco sits within reasonable range of supermarkets and fresh markets. Plan a provisioning run for the first evening rather than day zero — the fleet turnaround day is not the moment to load the galley.

Pricing and what to expect

Weekly pricing for motorboats from Marina di Portisco runs roughly 200 to 800 euro per day depending on size and engine. Add roughly 10–20% for extras: transit log or tourist tax (country-dependent), fuel, provisioning, end-cleaning, final kitty for skipper/hostess if aboard. Tenders and outboards are usually included; premium extras (SUPs, diving gear, coffee machine) may carry supplements.

Last-minute bookings within four weeks of departure sometimes drop 15–25% below brochure — availability on the Boat4You search updates live as holds release.

Best time to charter

June and September are widely preferred over peak August when ports are full and prices spike. Winter and early spring (pre-April) most bases are closed. Tyrrhenian and Sardinian waters see Libeccio from the southwest and Maestrale from the northwest. Afternoon thermals dominate calm days.

Peak season

July and August see the highest demand, highest pricing, and the fullest marinas. Book 4–6 months ahead for the best fleet selection.

Shoulder months

May, June and September deliver near-summer weather at lower pricing — often 25–35% off peak rates — and lighter pressure on popular anchorages.

Sample 7-day itinerary from Marina di Portisco

Day 1 — Departure and first bay

Embark after noon once the cabin preparation is complete, stock provisions, and motor to a nearby sheltered bay for the first night. Swim, rig checks, and a quiet dinner aboard set the tone.

Day 2 to Day 3 — Short hops along the coast

Cover 15–25 nautical miles per day between protected harbours and anchorages. Lunch stops at lunch-only anchorages, evenings in towns with konoba dining and a morning bakery run.

Day 4 — Longer crossing

Pick a day with favourable forecast for the longest passage of the week. Target 25–40 nautical miles of sailing or motoring depending on wind.

Day 5 to 6 — Return legs

Work back toward base with cultural stops — old town walk, a local winery, and an anchorage-only overnight if weather allows.

Day 7 — Return to base

Early morning passage back, fuel top-up, a quick galley clean, and formal disembarkation by late morning.

Is a licence required for bareboat charter in Italy?

For bareboat motorboat charter, a recognised skipper licence and VHF certificate are typically required. If you don't hold them, a professional skipper from the Marina di Portisco network runs roughly 150–220 euro per day plus meals.

Base pricing covers the motorboat, insurance, and berth fees at Marina di Portisco. Fuel, provisioning, transit fees (country-specific), end-cleaning, and skipper (if hired) are typically added on top. The final pre-departure summary from the broker lists every line item.
