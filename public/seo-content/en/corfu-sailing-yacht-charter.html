The focused saloon, narrower beam, and performance design create a more immersive sailing experience than larger charter platforms.

Corfu as Your Sailing Home Base

Corfu Town (Kerkyra), the island's capital, is a living museum of Mediterranean history. The UNESCO-listed Old Town preserves a unique blend of Venetian fortifications, French arcaded streets (the Liston), and British-colonial architecture—a legacy unmatched in Greece. Narrow alleys lead to hidden piazzas, family-run tavernas, and bakeries selling fiadone (a ricotta-custard pastry unique to Corfu).

The island receives direct flights from major European cities, making Corfu International Airport (CFU) an easy entry point. Car rentals are inexpensive if you wish to explore inland olive groves and villages before or after your sailing charter. Boat4You coordinates seamless airport transfers and provisioning, so you arrive at your yacht ready to sail.

Sailing Routes & Anchorages from Corfu

The Classic Paxos Run: A 30–35 nautical mile passage south delivers you to Paxos, a tiny island of just 5,000 inhabitants divided into three idyllic villages. Gaios, the main settlement, wraps around a double harbor with Venetian fortifications on two small islands. Lakka to the north and Longos to the south offer equally charming waterfront dining. Summer anchorages include Ortholithi Bay (north Antipaxos) and the turquoise shallows off Sipri Beach—unforgettable swimming in gin-clear water.

Northern Corfu Sailing: Kassiopi and Sidari, on Corfu's northeast coast, provide shorter passages (15–20nm) and scenic anchorages. Sidari's striped limestone formations and the Canal d'Amour (a narrow inlet bordered by dramatic cliffs) make for memorable dinghy explorations. These spots suit sailors seeking day passages and relaxed village taverna suppers.

Albanian Coast Adventure: For confident crews, Saranda and Ksamil offer extraordinary value and unspoiled hospitality. Passage requires basic clearance (clearing in at Saranda Customs) but opens access to beaches and anchorages rarely visited by Western yachts. The anchorage at Ksamil, offshore from three small islands, is sublime for swimming and snorkeling.

Extended Passages: Multi-week charters may include Preveza (50nm), Parga, or Lefkada (60+nm), connecting the northern and central Ionian and offering more varied sailing angles and larger-scale anchorages.

Wind, Weather & Seasonal Sailing

The Ionian's summer Maistro thermal wind typically blows 10–18 knots from the northwest, creating predictable conditions perfect for sailing yachts. Mornings are often light; the breeze builds through midday, then eases in the evening—a rhythm that encourages conservative passage timing and leisurely anchorage days.

Occasionally, stronger northwesterlies (20–25 knots) occur in July or early August, but these typically last hours, not days. Sheltered harbors like Corfu Town, Gaios, and Lakka provide secure refuge. The Ionian rarely experiences the violent Meltemia (strong summer north winds) that plague the Aegean.

Spring (April–May) and autumn (September–October) offer equally favorable conditions with fewer charter yachts competing for anchorages. Water temperatures range from 18°C in April to 26°C in August, making a 3–5mm wetsuit essential for spring and autumn swimming.

Provisioning & Onboard Life

Corfu Town boasts modern supermarkets (Jumbo, Carrefour, Lidl) stocked with Greek olive oil, feta cheese, wine, and fresh seafood. Boat4You offers pre-provisioning services: coordinate a shopping list, and your charter base will stock the yacht before arrival. Many sailors prefer to shop daily in village markets (Gaios, Kassiopi) for fresh vegetables, local bread, and pescatarian delights.

Galley provisioning is straightforward: gas stoves and electric ovens are standard on modern sailing yachts. Water makers are not universal, so conservative water use (daily showers at anchor, minimal washing) is standard practice. Most crews consume 50–100 liters daily, achievable on a full 500-liter tank for 5–7 day cruises between villages with water docks.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a sailing license to charter a monohull in Corfu?

Bareboat charters require an International Certificate of Competence (ICC) or equivalent. accepts RYA, USSAILING, or recognized national certificates. If you lack credentials, our crewed option pairs you with a professional skipper and optional flotilla support for a worry-free experience.

What's included in a sailing yacht charter?

Bareboat charters include: the yacht, engine fuel, electronics (GPS, plotter, VHF), navigation charts, safety equipment, and insurance. Optional services: professional skipper ($150–250/day), provisioning, transit log copies, and 24/7 support hotline. Costs for dinghy fuel and mooring/anchorage fees are generally $10–30/night depending on destination.

Is the Ionian suitable for inexperienced sailors?

Absolutely. The Ionian's moderate winds, protected harbors, and short inter-island passages make it a world-class training ground. Chartering with a professional skipper for the first few days builds confidence; many sailors then continue bareboat independently. pairs newcomers with mentorship and conservative routing advice.

Can we swim and snorkel from the yacht?

Yes, the Ionian's clear, warm waters are ideal for daily swimming and snorkeling. Most anchorages (Antipaxos, Ksamil, Sidari, Kassiopi) offer excellent visibility and minimal pollution. Bring dive masks and snorkels; underwater life includes colorful fish, sea urchins, and octopi—typically docile and uninterested in swimmers.

What if unexpected repairs are needed during the charter?

Every sailing yacht is mechanically certified before dispatch. However, sailing is maritime practice: occasional minor issues (stuck winch, electrical glitch) do happen. Your charter includes 24/7 support and a network of repair contacts in Corfu, Paxos, and Saranda. Major issues are rare and covered by comprehensive insurance.

Can we extend the charter or switch yachts mid-charter?

Extensions are subject to availability and are billed at daily rates. Yacht swaps for repair or preference are accommodated when possible. Contact before the charter to discuss flexibility options—advance notice ensures smoother logistics.
