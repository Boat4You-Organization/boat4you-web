Catamaran Charter in Taranto — Explore Two Seas

Taranto is where sailing meets history. This ancient port city straddles two distinct bodies of water — the Mar Grande and Mar Piccolo — creating a unique sailing destination in Italy's Ionian heartland. When you charter a catamaran in Taranto, you gain access to one of the Mediterranean's most underexplored sailing regions, where Byzantine ruins meet pristine beaches and the Adriatic winds carry you toward Greece.

Why Charter a Catamaran from Taranto

Boat4You catamarans are ideal for Taranto's waters. The twin-hull design gives you shallow-draft capability to explore the Mar Piccolo's lagoon-like channels — a narrow, fjord-like waterway where Roman fleets once sheltered — while offering the comfort and speed to reach the open Ionian in hours. Catamarans excel in the inconsistent winds that characterise Taranto in summer, providing stable platforms for families and less experienced sailors alike.

If you're planning a week's sailing in Apulia, a catamaran is your best bet for comfort and flexibility.

Top Sailing Routes from Taranto

Base yourself in Taranto and sail south toward the Salento peninsula — the heel of Italy's boot. A two-day passage reaches Otranto, where limestone cliffs plunge into crystalline waters. Continue to Santa Maria di Leuca, where the Ionian Sea and Adriatic converge, marked by a 19th-century lighthouse. From there, ambitious crews can push east to the Greek Ionian islands, roughly 120 nautical miles across open water, or island-hop up the Croatian coast.

For shorter cruises, explore the Strait of Taranto and nearby Martina Franca islets, anchor off Castellaneta beach, and day-sail to Ginosa Marina. The coastline transitions from Baroque towns to wild limestone cliffs — each anchorage has its own character.

Taranto's Marinas and Facilities

Taranto's main charter berths are in the Mar Grande, near the modern harbour facility. Boat4You partners with local marinas offering water, fuel, and provisioning. The city itself has multiple supermarkets, chandleries, and fishing co-operatives where you can buy fresh mussels (Taranto is famous for these) and local taralli — crunchy savoury biscuits. The Marina Militare occupies much of the inner harbour, but commercial berths are well-organised and staffed.

Internet connectivity is reliable, laundry facilities are available, and the city centre is within walking distance for supplies and dining. If you need repairs or technical support, Taranto's shipyards have decades of experience with charter vessels.

Local Cuisine and Provisioning

Taranto's food culture revolves around the sea. Mussels (cozze) are cultivated in the Mar Piccolo using traditional rope-farming methods — visit a working farm or buy at the fish market (Mercato del Pesce) for supper. Orecchiette con le cime di rapa (pasta with turnip greens) and fresh anchovies are staples. Local wine from nearby Salento vineyards — Negroamaro and Primitivo — pairs perfectly with seafood.

The weekly market in Taranto's old town is a sensory overload: fresh produce, local cheese, and preserved vegetables. Stock your catamaran's galley in the morning and you'll dine as locals do.

Best Sailing Seasons in Taranto

May through October offers the most reliable wind and warmest water. Summer (July–August) brings predictable Maestral winds from the northwest, ideal for reaching south toward Leuca or east toward Greece. June and September have lighter winds but fewer crowds. Winter and early spring are quiet; winds can be strong and unpredictable, but if you're comfortable with rougher conditions, you'll find solitude and dramatic skies.

Getting There and Logistics

Taranto is served by Bari airport, roughly 100 km north, with connections to major Italian cities and European hubs. A rental car or bus gets you to Taranto in two hours. The city has hotels, restaurants, and car-hire desks within walking distance of the marina. Many Boat4You clients arrive a day early to explore the old town and its museums before boarding.

Frequently Asked Questions

What experience level do I need to charter a catamaran in Taranto?

If you hold a valid sailing certificate or have skippered monohulls for 100+ hours, you can charter bareboat. Beginners are welcome with a professional skipper or flotilla; Boat4You offers fully crewed options and skippered charters tailored to your group's experience.

Can I cross to Greece from Taranto?

Yes, experienced crews regularly sail from Taranto or Leuca to the Greek Ionian islands (Corfu, Paxos, Epirus coast). The crossing is roughly 120–150 nautical miles and takes 24–30 hours. You'll need the proper documentation (passports, boat papers, insurance) and should plan for at least 7–10 days to explore once you arrive.

What is the best month for a week's catamaran charter?

June and September offer the ideal balance: warm sea, steady winds, and fewer charter fleets. July and August are busier but reliable sunshine; May and October require flexibility for occasional rain but reward you with smaller crowds and lower rates.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.
