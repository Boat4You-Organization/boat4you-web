Corsica: A Sailing Paradise

Corsica sits south of mainland France, a UNESCO-listed sailing destination famous for the Scandola Nature Reserve, the soaring Calanques de Piana, and the clifftop fortress of Bonifacio. The island's interior climbs to Monte Cintu at 2,706 metres, creating dramatic vertical landscapes from the sea. Whether you're chasing turquoise lagoons, exploring UNESCO sites, or planning a crossing to Sardinia 8 nautical miles south, Boat4You can match you with the right vessel and itinerary.

Key Ports and Marina Gateways

Ajaccio, on Corsica's west coast, is the island's capital and Boat4You's primary charter gateway. Port de Plaisance Charles Ornano holds 830 berths and sits 5 km from the airport, making it the natural starting point. Nearby lies the Îles Sanguinaires, perfect for first-night anchorage.

Bonifacio, on the dramatic southern tip, anchors a classical cruise: a fjord-like harbour sheltered by 70-metre limestone cliffs and a citadel perched 80 metres above the waterfront. From here, the Lavezzi islands (a protected marine reserve) lie 5–10 nautical miles south, and Sardinia's Porto Cervo or Palau beckon across the 8-nautical-mile Strait of Bonifacio.

Calvi, on the north-west coast, offers a charming harbour beneath the Citadel and access to the Scandola Nature Reserve to the south-east. Propriano and Porto-Vecchio on the south-east coast provide sheltered bases for exploring the Côte Rocheuse and island-hopping. Pianottoli-Caldarello, a quieter south-coast haven, suits crews seeking boutique charm over bustling infrastructure.

Winds and Sailing Seasons

Corsica's winds shape the sailing calendar. The libeccio blows from the south-west, the mistral spills over from the north in spring, and the maestrale arrives from the north-west in summer, typically light to moderate. May through October defines the prime season: warm water, reliable winds, and crystal visibility. June and September offer the most stable conditions; July and August bring crowded anchorages and strong thermal effects. Winter sailing is possible but exposed to Atlantic fronts funnelling through the Gulf of Lion.

Circumnavigation: 7–14 Day Itinerary

A classic Corsica cruise begins at Ajaccio, threading the west coast (Calvi, Scandola Nature Reserve) before rounding the northern cape and descending the sheltered east shore through Macinaggio and Solenzara. The circumnavigation typically takes 7–10 days depending on weather and stops, covering roughly 180–220 nautical miles and including 6–8 major anchorages: Îles Sanguinaires, Scandola, Calvi, Iles d'Hyères (near Porquerolles, off the French mainland), the Côte Rocheuse, Lavezzi, and Bonifacio.

For longer charters (12–14 days), extend the itinerary south across the Strait of Bonifacio to Sardinia's Costa Smeralda, visiting Porto Cervo, Palau, and the Maddalena archipelago before returning to Corsica or proceeding to Elba and Tuscany's Toscana coast.

Crossing to Sardinia and Beyond

The Strait of Bonifacio—8 nautical miles of strong current, narrow passages, and dramatic seascapes—connects Corsica to Sardinia's exclusive resorts. The crossing typically takes 2–3 hours under sail and 1.5 hours by motor, depending on conditions. Swift catamarans handle the current and sea state more comfortably than smaller vessels, while sailing yachts relish the passage sailing. Once in Sardinia, crews explore the Maddalena National Park (seven islands), anchor off Porto Cervo's mega-yacht hub, or cruise the granite coves of Palau.

Local Highlights and Exploration

Scandola Nature Reserve (UNESCO World Heritage Site) protects dramatic red porphyry cliffs, inaccessible by land but perfect for boat exploration and snorkelling. The Calanques de Piana—narrow, cathedral-like inlets on the west coast—demand careful navigation but reward visitors with photogenic anchorages. Bonifacio's Grotte du Sdragonato and surrounding limestone caverns are accessible by tender or guided cave tours. The Lavezzi islands offer pristine white-sand beaches, crystalline water, and marine conservation zones home to dusky groupers and Mediterranean seagrass meadows.

Onshore, Ajaccio honours Napoleon's birthplace, Calvi blends Genoese fortifications with Côte d'Azur charm, and Bonifacio's upper town maze rewards hikers with vistas across the Strait. Provisioning is excellent: local Corsican wine (Patrimonio reds), charcuterie (lonzu, coppa), and daily markets in every port town ensure fresh Mediterranean larders.

Flora, Fauna, and Natural Heritage

Corsica's mountain backbone—crowned by Monte Cintu and the high plateaux of the interior—creates distinct microclimates where Mediterranean maquis (shrubland of rosemary, thyme, cistus, and juniper) meets European forests. Seabirds including osprey, Audouin's gull, and endemic subspecies inhabit coastal cliffs. The Lavezzi marine zone nurtures dusky groupers (endangered species bred in marine reserves), moray eels, octopuses, and Mediterranean herb gardens of Posidonia grass—the "lungs" of the Mediterranean. Snorkellers often spot these ecosystems firsthand; charter briefings emphasize low-impact diving and dinghy etiquette to protect fragile seagrass beds.

Water Activities Beyond Sailing

Corsica's crystalline waters invite kayaking, paddleboarding, snorkelling, and spearfishing (subject to local regulations). Many Boat4You charters stock inflatable kayaks or paddleboards; crews explore cove edges, limestone caves, and marine grottoes inaccessible by yacht. Scuba diving is regulated within marine reserves but abundant in specific zones; dive schools in Bonifacio and Porto-Vecchio offer PADI courses and guided exploration of wreck sites and rock formations.

Catamaran vs. Sailing Yacht: Choosing Your Vessel

Corsica suits both multihulls and monohulls, but for different reasons. Catamarans (29 boats in our fleet) dominate because they access shallow coves, offer stability for families, and excel at confident Strait of Bonifacio crossings with minimal motion. They're ideal for week-long charters mixing leisurely sailing, shore exploration, and social galley life. Sailing yachts (3 boats) appeal to experienced skippers who relish passage planning, value heavier-weather capability, and seek authentic Mediterranean sailing where performance and seamanship matter. Both fleet types deliver extraordinary Corsican adventures; the choice depends on your crew's experience, priorities, and tolerance for motion in open water.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best time to sail in Corsica?

May through October is the peak season, with June, September, and early October offering the most stable winds and comfortable temperatures. July and August are warm but crowded; spring (May–June) brings reliable north-westerlies after the mistral season.

Can I sail Corsica as a beginner?

Yes. Catamarans from our fleet's fleet are forgiving platforms; most anchorages are sheltered; and a skipper's brief and optional bareboat certification cover the basics. The west coast and Ajaccio–Calvi–Scandola route suit mixed-ability crews. More challenging passages (Bonifacio Strait, exposed north coast) benefit from a professional skipper hire.

How far is it from Corsica to Sardinia?

The Strait of Bonifacio crossing is 8 nautical miles, typically a 2–3 hour sail depending on wind and current. Strong tidal streams require awareness but are navigable year-round in daylight.

What marine reserves should I visit?

Scandola Nature Reserve (north-west coast), the Lavezzi islands and Straits of Bonifacio marine zone (south), and the Côte Rocheuse (east coast) are protected zones accessible to yachts. Mooring fees and eco-labelling apply; anchoring is restricted in core zones but dinghy exploration is encouraged.

Where can I find fresh provisions?

Every major port—Ajaccio, Calvi, Propriano, Porto-Vecchio, Bonifacio, and Pianottoli—has markets, supermarkets, and seafood stalls. Wednesday and Saturday markets burst with local produce, Corsican wines, and charcuterie. Plan stops around market days for the best selection.
