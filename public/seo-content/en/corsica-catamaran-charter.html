Corsica Catamaran Charter

Boat4You operates 29 catamarans across Corsica—the island of turquoise coves, soaring red cliffs, and hidden anchorages that demand shallow-draft vessels. Multihulls are Corsica's default choice: they access lagoons and rock-sheltered bays where monohulls can't venture, provide a stable platform for families and mixed crews, and confidently cross the 8-nautical-mile Strait of Bonifacio to Sardinia with ample motion control and interior comfort.

Why Catamarans Dominate Corsica

Corsica's sailing character—dramatic cliffs, boulder-strewn coves, and protected marine reserves—rewards shallow-draft hulls. Catamarans drawing 1.0–1.3 metres access the Lavezzi islands' white-sand lagoons, anchor in rock-lined cirques off the Calanques de Piana, and thread narrow channels around Bonifacio's fjord-like harbour that deeper-hulled vessels must skirt or avoid entirely. The result: your crew reaches anchorages others cannot and moors closer to the island's most photogenic features.

Stability is paramount. Corsica's open crossings—rounding the northern cape, transiting between island clusters, and above all the Strait of Bonifacio—bring swell and current. A catamaran's wide beam and twin hulls absorb motion far better than a displacement monohull, keeping cabins comfortable and seasickness rare. Families with children, chartered crews of mixed sailing ability, and guests with mobility concerns find catamarans infinitely more forgiving.

Interior volume translates to spacious cabins, en-suite heads in every berth, and social galley space. Corsican charters often blend daysailing with nightlife ashore—towns like Calvi and Bonifacio buzz after dark—and you'll appreciate the generous saloon where crews gather in poor weather or for sunset aperitifs.

Lavezzi Islands and Protected Bays

The Lavezzi archipelago, a protected marine reserve east of Bonifacio, offers some of the Mediterranean's most pristine anchorages. Shallow, crystal water in hues of jade and turquoise surrounds granite and limestone islets ringed by gently shelving sand. A catamaran's draft allows you to anchor in 2–3 metres where monohulls dare not tread, swim ashore directly, and set a second anchor for shelter in variable evening winds.

Explore Lavezzu and Cavallo islands by dinghy, snorkel over dusky grouper breeding grounds in the marine conservation zone, and beach-comb for Corsican granite and sea-smoothed marble. Sunset anchorages here feel utterly removed from modernity, yet Bonifacio's restaurants and provisioning lie just 5–7 nautical miles north—perfect for evening shore runs.

Rock-Lined Coves of the Côte Rocheuse

Corsica's east-facing coast (Côte Rocheuse) stretches from Solenzara to Porto-Vecchio, punctuated by narrow, boulder-scattered inlets carved into red granite headlands. Named anchorages—Cala di Trana, Cala Gonone, Île de la Pietra—demand careful navigation and nerves of steel. Catamarans, with their flat bottoms, instant shallow-water warning, and twin-engine manoeuvrability, handle these approaches far better than keeled monohulls. A shallow catamaran can anchor safely in 3 metres surrounded by turquoise water and soaring cliff faces; a 6-tonne displacement yacht might ground or swing dangerously on limited swing room.

The reward: some of Corsica's most secluded swims, snorkels over ancient Roman anchor stocks, and photo opportunities that rival the Calanques de Piana. Crews often spend full days at single anchorages, exploring by dinghy, paddleboard, and snorkel gear.

Gateway Marinas and Base Ports

Ajaccio's Port de Plaisance Charles Ornano—830 berths, fuel, chandlery, and a 5-km drive from the airport—is Boat4You's primary catamaran base. The city offers provisioning, restaurants, and the charm of Napoleon's birthplace, but the real magic begins once you slip moorings and motor south towards Calvi or west towards the Îles Sanguinaires.

Calvi's northern harbour, guarded by a Genoese citadel, is a traditional Corsican port-de-plaisance with good mooring availability and easy access northward to the Scandola Nature Reserve. A 20-nautical-mile passage south reaches the Calanques de Piana—steep granite slopes and red cliffs reflected in mirror water.

Bonifacio, anchored 8 nautical miles north of Sardinia, suits catamarans destined for longer Mediterranean voyages. The dramatic cliff-top fortress, cave-lined foreshore, and gateway to Lavezzi and Sardinia make it essential. Programmati on this base if crossing to Porto Cervo or exploring the Maddalena archipelago.

The Bonifacio Strait Crossing

Eight nautical miles separate Corsica from Sardinia—dramatic water where currents converge, swell funnels, and ancient wrecks remind sailors of the passage's raw power. Yet catamarans handle the Strait of Bonifacio with ease. Twin hulls and wide beam provide motion damping that single-hulled vessels struggle to match. Swift vessels (motor-catamarans or hybrid systems) cross in 1.5 hours; pure sailors often relay-race the leg, gybing on northerly swells and enjoying an exhilarating beam reach.

Plan the crossing for daylight and slack current (check tide tables at Bonifacio). A catamaran skipper can depart Lavezzi after breakfast, clear the Strait by lunchtime, and be anchored off Maddalena's granite isles by 4 p.m.—impossible in a slower monohull. This advantage opens multi-destination charters: Corsica–Sardinia–Elba in one week, or Corsica–Sardinia–Tuscany in twelve days.

Catamarans for Mixed-Ability Crews

Families with young children, non-sailors invited along, and friends with mobility considerations thrive aboard catamarans. Flat cabin soles, dual helm stations, and forgiving motion make sailing instruction intuitive. Dinghy launching and retrieval are straightforward via robust boarding platforms. Spacious decks allow crew to spread out and relax while two or three active sailors handle boat duties. Galley and saloon warmth in rare bad weather keeps morale high.

Boat4You recommends certified skippered or semi-bareboat charters for mixed-ability groups, pairing your catamaran with a local captain who knows Corsica's anchorages, provisioning, and weather nuances. He or she will maximize your comfort and confidence while teaching keen crew members the finer points of Corsican sailing.

Provisioning and Galley Life

Corsican catamarans return to port every 2–3 days, ensuring abundant fresh provisions. Wednesday and Saturday markets in Ajaccio, Calvi, Propriano, Porto-Vecchio, and Bonifacio overflow with local produce, Corsican wine (Patrimonio and Vin de Corse appellations), and charcuterie (lonzu, coppa, prisuttu). Supermarkets stock international goods; fishmongers sell daily catch. Fuel docks exist at every primary port and most secondary ones, so range anxiety is absent.

Catamaran galleys, larger and better appointed than monohull equivalents, support ambitious meals. Many crews prepare dinner aboard, invite skippers and fellow charterers from anchored yachts, and share sun-drenched saloon dinners with local Corsican wine and Mediterranean herbs. Pantropical provisioning is easy; authentic Corsican dining—fresh seafood, fennel-wrapped sausage, chestnut polenta—is both accessible and affordable.

Weather and Seasonal Patterns

May through October is the catamaran-friendly season. The libeccio (south-westerly) dominates late summer, the mistral occasionally spills from the north in spring, and the maestrale settles in from the north-west mid-summer, typically light to moderate. June and September are ideal: stable winds, warm water, minimal thermal turbulence, and fewer crowded anchorages than July–August.

Winter sailing is possible but exposes catamarans to Atlantic fronts funnelling through the Gulf of Lion. Stable weather windows shrink; prepared crews can succeed, but conditions are less predictable. Spring (May) and autumn (October) bookings reward early planners with perfect conditions, lighter fleets, and more availability across the 29-boat roster.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are catamarans slower than sailing yachts?

Not in Corsica's typical wind. Catamarans excel in light to moderate winds (5–15 knots) common May–October. They motor efficiently when the wind drops, so schedule flexibility matters more than raw boat speed. If epic offshore passages excite you, a monohull shines; if protected anchorages and turquoise water define your dream, a catamaran maximizes joy per sailing hour.

Can my family with children sail a catamaran bareboat?

Yes, if you or a crew member hold a sailing certificate (RYA Day Skipper or equivalent). Many families hire a local skipper for 2–3 days to orient everyone, then progress to semi-bareboat with phone support. Alternatively, full skippered charters relieve all worry, letting you enjoy swimming and exploring while the skipper handles navigation and sail trim.

What is the draft of a typical Corsica catamaran?

the charter fleet draws 1.0–1.3 metres (3.3–4.2 feet), accessing lagoons and coves unreachable by deeper-hulled yachts. This shallow draft is essential for Lavezzi, the rock-lined Côte Rocheuse, and protected bays north of Calvi.

Do I need a skipper for the Strait of Bonifacio crossing?

If you're bareboat and hold a coastal-passage certificate, you can cross independently; many experienced charterers do. First-timers or those unfamiliar with tidal streams benefit hugely from a local skipper hire (typically 200–350 euros per day). Skippered crossings include weather routing, current knowledge, and confidence built over years of Corsican sailing.

What happens if I encounter strong wind during my charter?

Catamarans handle 15–20 knot winds beautifully; stronger conditions favour sheltered ports (Ajaccio, Bonifacio, Calvi) and reduced-sail daysails. Our 24/7 support line connects you with local skippers for guidance if conditions escalate unexpectedly.

Many first-time charterers book 1–2 days of skipper-led orientation before taking over as captain. This investment pays dividends in confidence and seamanship.
