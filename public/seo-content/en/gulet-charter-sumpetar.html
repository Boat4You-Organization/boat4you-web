Sumpetar Port Facility & Mooring

Sumpetar Port operates as a functional village harbor without resort pretension. Four to six protected berths accommodate gulets; mooring arrangements are coordinated directly with the local harbor authority. The approach is straightforward: a clear channel leads to a small breakwater-protected basin. Boat4You coordinates all mooring procedures, ensuring seamless vessel berthing and pre-departure logistics.

Village Port Character & Atmosphere

Sumpetar retains its identity as a working fishing village. Local boats dock alongside charters; the port hums with genuine maritime activity—nets drying, fish sales, and daily catch auctions. This authentic atmosphere contrasts sharply with commercial resort marinas. Crews departing from Sumpetar experience real Adriatic culture: village restaurants, family-run tavernas, and personal hospitality from locals who have known the port for generations.

Access Roads & Ground Transportation

Sumpetar connects to the coastal road via a small paved road (approximately 500 meters). Vehicle parking exists near the harbor; most charter groups arrive by rental car or taxi from Split (50 km, approximately 60-minute drive) or from Split Airport (Kaštela, 75 km). Boat4You coordinates ground transport and provides detailed arrival instructions.

Marina Services & Facilities

Sumpetar Port provides basic services: fuel dock (diesel, unleaded), potable water, waste disposal, and functional reception. Shore-power connections are limited; most crews rely on vessel generators. The modest facility operates during daylight hours (8 AM–6 PM); after-hours contact is available through Boat4You's emergency hotline.

Gulet departures from Sumpetar typically target the Brač-Hvar-Vis triangle (15–40 nm from port). Weather & Seasonal Mooring Conditions

Sumpetar's harbor orientation (southwest-facing) provides good protection in summer maestral winds. Autumn and winter gales occasionally create challenging mooring conditions; coordinates seasonal berthing adjustments or re-routing to protected harbors during adverse forecasts. Most charters operate May through September; winter departures require experienced crews and professional support.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.

What deposit is required at booking?

Most operators request 30–50% at contract signing, with the balance due 4–6 weeks before departure. Exact terms appear on your quotation before you commit.

Can I move my charter to a different week if I need to?

Usually yes, subject to fleet availability and any operator handling fee. Request the change as early as possible; mid-season swaps are harder than off-season.

Is there a standard security deposit for damages?

Yes. Most operators block a refundable deposit on your credit card at check-in, typically €2,000–€5,000 depending on vessel. A damage-waiver upgrade reduces or eliminates this hold — ask your consultant for the specific options on your chosen boat.
