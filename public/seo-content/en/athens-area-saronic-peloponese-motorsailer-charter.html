The Best of Both Worlds: Motorsailers in the Saronic

A motorsailer is a hybrid vessel combining spacious sailing yacht interiors with powerful diesel engines, offering the aesthetic and spirit of sailing without total wind dependence. Typically 35–55 meters in length, motorsailers operate as bareboat or light-crew vessels, accommodating 6–16 guests across 3–6 spacious cabins. Boat4You partners with Saronic-based charter operators offering motorsailers—often traditionally styled Turkish gulets or modern aluminum designs—ideal for charter groups valuing sailing aesthetics, spacious interiors, and operational flexibility unavailable in smaller monohulls or pure motorboats.

Motorsailers are cherished by charter groups seeking a "sailing vacation" atmosphere without the relentless heel and tight quarters of traditional sailing yachts. Full-time sailing is optional; motor-sailing dominates summer Meltemi passages, while light-wind days allow genuine sail-training and performance sailing. Crews often number 2–4 (captain, cook/hostess, deckhand), making them more affordable than superyachts yet more service-oriented than bareboat sailing or power catamarans.

Fleet Types and Specifications

Traditional Turkish Gulets: 35–50 meters, typically wooden-hull construction with classic Aegean styling. Spacious open-air decks, multiple masts, and capacious interiors suited to group travel. Crewed-only charters; captain and cook integral. Example: modern motorized Turkish gulet operating seasonally from Alimos.

Modern Motorsailers (Steel/Aluminum): 40–55 meters, contemporary lines blending sailing efficiency with motor-boat speed. Full electronics, air-conditioning, and spacious saloons. Available bareboat (licensed skipper required) or with crew support. Example: Abeking & Rasmussen motorsailers in 45–50m range.

Rigging and Propulsion: Most motorsailers carry 2–3 masts with roller-furling sails (mainsail + 1–2 jibs). Powerful 300–500 hp diesel engines enable 10–14 knot cruising under power. Fuel capacity (2,000–4,000 liters) provides 1,000+ nm range, covering Saronic + Cyclades combinations without refueling.

Standard equipment includes GPS/chartplotter, autopilot, VHF, satellite communications, stabilizers, air-conditioning, and multiple generators. Spacious galleys support gourmet meal preparation; professional kitchens on crewed vessels enable fine-dining experiences.

Crewed Motorsailer Model (Most Common in Saronic)

Crewed motorsailers are the Saronic norm. A captain manages navigation and sail deployment; a cook (often trained) prepares multi-course meals; a deckhand handles rigging, anchoring, and water-sports equipment. Guests relax, participate in sailing if interested, and enjoy unprecedented comfort—6–16 person capacity with private staterooms rivals shoreside villas. Typical cost: €8,000–15,000/day depending on vessel size and season (low season carries 30–40% discounts).

Boat4You vets all crew for language skills, hospitality training, and sailing credentials. Pre-charter consultations align itineraries with guest interests: active sailing, relaxation, cultural exploration, or water-sports focus.

Bareboat Motorsailer Options (Professional Skippers)

A few bareboat motorsailers exist for licensed skippers seeking spacious, autonomous cruising without crew wages. These are rarer than crewed options but appeal to experienced sailors wanting larger groups (8–12 people) without service obligations. Bareboat motorsailers cost €3,000–7,000/day depending on vessel and season—significantly less than crewed equivalents.

Sailing Performance and Motoring Capability

Under Sail: Motorsailers are not performance vessels; they're comfortable cruisers. 35–45 knot apparent winds deliver 5–7 knots boat speed. Roller-furling simplifies sail trim; crews of 2–3 manage easily. Most operations motor-sail (engine + one sail) in summer Meltemi, balancing fuel economy and comfort.

Under Motor: Powerful single or twin diesels deliver 10–14 knots cruising; top speed reaches 15–18 knots if desired (though fuel-hungry). Shallow draft (1.5–2.2 m) accesses Saronic anchorages. Twin engines (on larger vessels) provide redundancy critical for longer Saronic + Cycladic itineraries.

The Saronic suits motorsailers perfectly: calm-weather sailing (spring, autumn) showcases sail handling; summer Meltemi is managed by powerful motors. No true sailing expertise required from crew—captain handles all sailing duties while guests relax.

Ideal Charter Groups for Motorsailers

Large multi-family reunions (10–16 people): Spacious cabins (double + twin berths), multiple bathrooms, and open decks accommodate extended groups without crowding. Galley feeds group meals; crew manages logistics.

Corporate and group charters (8–12 people): Spacious saloon suits business meetings, team-building sailing lessons, and evening entertainment. Professional crew adds sophistication; flexibility beats rigid itineraries.

Sailing enthusiasts seeking comfort: Active sailors appreciate sail-handling opportunities without the cramped quarters of performance racing yachts. Evening sails under 2–3 masts are memorable; daylight motoring reduces fatigue.

Non-sailing groups valuing aesthetics and space: Sailing atmosphere without sailing duty. Spacious decks, outdoor dining, and crew service create resort-like comfort at sea.

Crewed Itineraries: The Saronic Experience

Classic 7-Day Saronic Sailing (120–180 nm): Alimos/Piraeus → Cape Sounion sunset sail → Aegina (fish taverna) → Poros (Russian Bay anchorage, sail afternoon) → Hydra (morning sea-breeze sail, afternoon island exploration) → Spetses (overnight anchorage, evening sail trial) → Porto Cheli (cultural excursion, Palamidhi fortress) → return Alimos. Daily itinerary flexible; crew adjusts based on guest energy and wind conditions.

10-Day Saronic and Peloponnese (200+ nm): Add Nafplio (cultural hub), Ermioni (hidden anchorages), and inland excursion to Mycenae. Longer daily sails build sailing skills; crew mentors interested guests.

Ambitious 14-Day Saronic and Cycladic Grand Tour (350+ nm): Combine Saronic islands with Cyclades (Tinos, Mykonos, Delos, smaller Cycladic islands). Overnight passages beyond Alimos limit require confident crew but showcase Mediterranean sailing grandeur. Return via Lavrion or direct to Alimos.

Water Sports and Activities

Motorsailers typically carry tenders, snorkeling gear, and water-sports equipment (wakeboards, water skis on larger vessels). Crewed vessels coordinate diving expeditions (Minor Epidavros sunken city, Dokos wreck), island explorations, and land excursions. Evening entertainment includes stargazing decks, card games in saloon, and acoustic guitar sessions (if crew inclined). Island restaurants are pre-booked for special dinners ashore.

Sailing Participation and Skill-Building Opportunities

Crewed motorsailers are uniquely suited to sailors building experience or introducing newcomers to sailing. A captain manages overall navigation and safety but welcomes hands-on participation: crew members assist with sail raising, practice trim techniques, and gain helm time on open-water reaches. Evening debriefs explain weather patterns, tide dynamics, and piloting skills—informal mentoring that builds confidence.

Non-sailing guests can participate passively, watching the crew operate, or actively join sail-handling lessons without responsibility. The low-pressure environment—no racing, no performance expectations—makes motorsailers ideal "learn-to-sail" platforms. Guests complete a 7-day charter with newfound skills, often booking bareboat sailing yachts subsequently.

Climate and Seasonal Timing

Motorsailers operate May through October. June and September deliver ideal sailing conditions (8–15 knots), comfortable temperatures, and manageable crew workload. July and August bring stronger Meltemi (15–25 knots), warmer seas (26°C), and crowded anchorages—exhilarating for active sailors but potentially exhausting for non-sailing groups. Spring (April–May) and autumn (October) offer fewer crowds, lower rates, and more flexibility; weather becomes less predictable in November–March.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I participate in sailing on a crewed motorsailer?

Yes. Crew welcomes interested guests to assist with sail-raising, trimming, and helmsmanship. Instructional sailing sessions are available; passive participation (watching from deck) is equally welcome. Non-sailors simply relax while crew handles all operations.

What is included in a crewed motorsailer charter?

All prices include captain, cook, crew salaries, fuel, mooring, provisions (breakfast/lunch/dinner, drinks, snacks), water sports, and insurance. Excluded: gratuities (10–15%), alcoholic beverages beyond house wine/beer, and paid land excursions (museum entries, car rental, guided tours).

How far in advance should I book a motorsailer?

Popular vessels in July–August are often booked 6–12 months ahead. Spring and autumn allow 2–4 month bookings. Last-minute discounts (30–40% off) occur occasionally for late April, November, or unexpected cancellations.

Are motorsailers suitable for elderly guests or those with mobility limitations?

Yes. Spacious cabins, wide decks, handrails, and crew assistance suit guests with limited mobility. Tenders facilitate island access; crew manages dinghy operations. Discuss specific needs during booking; crew accommodates medical equipment and mobility aids.

Can crew accommodate dietary restrictions and allergies?

Absolutely. Professional chefs manage vegan, gluten-free, kosher, halal, and severe allergies routinely. Notify during pre-booking; crew sources specialized ingredients locally, ensuring quality and safety.
