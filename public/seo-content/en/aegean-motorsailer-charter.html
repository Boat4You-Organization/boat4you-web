The Motorsailer Concept

Motorsailers bridge the divide between dedicated sailing yachts and pure motor vessels. These vessels are engineered to sail beautifully—with well-balanced sail plans, responsive steering, and genuine sailing performance—yet incorporate powerful auxiliary engines enabling transit regardless of wind conditions. This combination proves exceptionally practical in Mediterranean sailing: early morning light-wind periods permit motor-sailing (engine running, partial sail deployed) preserving fuel economy and maintaining momentum; afternoon Etesian winds enable pure sailing, employing engines sparingly; evening approaches to harbours benefit from motor power ensuring safe, controlled entries.

Motorsailers typically feature deeper keels than catamarans but shallower drafts than pure sailing yachts (typically 1.5–1.9 metres), balancing sailing performance with shallow-water accessibility. The engineered mass distribution provides stability comparable to sailing yachts, yet the motor eliminates frustration during extended light-wind periods. For many sailors, motorsailers represent the optimal balance: engaging sailing when conditions favour it, motor convenience when practical, and constant momentum enabling timely destination arrival.

Turkish Aegean: Ideal Motorsailer Territory

The Turkish Aegean's summer Etesian winds—consistent northwesterly breezes at Force 2–4—create ideal motorsailer conditions. Morning light-wind or calm conditions favour motor-sailing; afternoon thermals produce steady winds ideal for pure sailing; evening wind diminishment permits relaxed motoring into anchorages. This daily wind pattern suits motorsailers perfectly: rather than waiting for sufficient wind or relying entirely on motors, you alternate between wind-powered passages and motor convenience.

Additionally, the Aegean's geography—numerous islands, protected straits, shallow bays, and extensive coastline—demands the shallow-draft advantage motorsailers provide. Coves accessible only to vessels drawing less than 1.8 metres open to motorsailer exploration. Conversely, the region's harbours, channels, and straits require transit capability that pure sailing yachts lack during calm periods; motorsailers' motor-sailing capability eliminates waiting for wind before proceeding.

Sailing Performance and Motor-Sailing Balance

A well-designed motorsailer achieves 7–9 knots under sail in moderate Aegean winds (Force 3–4), comparable to dedicated sailing yachts yet with superior comfort accommodations. The larger auxiliary engines permit sustained 8–10 knot motor-sailing, enabling meaningful progress during light-wind periods. The balance is deliberate: neither design extreme (pure sailing nor pure motoring), but rather optimised for practical Mediterranean cruising where wind-powered elegance and motor-assisted flexibility coexist harmoniously.

Experienced sailors often find motorsailers liberating: you retain sailing engagement and skill expression whilst eliminating the frustration of extended calms or excessive wind-beating. The motors function as insurance against unexpected weather shifts or urgent scheduling needs, rather than as primary propulsion. Many motorsailer captains employ a philosophy of "sail when practical, motor when efficient"—maximising sailing time whilst ensuring steady progress towards chosen destinations.

Bareboat and Skippered Options

Motorsailers accommodate both bareboat and skippered charters. Bareboat options suit experienced sailors—those holding RYA Competent Crew or Day Skipper certifications—comfortable with sail handling, motor operation, and navigation. The Aegean's forgiving summer conditions make motorsailer bareboat charters accessible to less experienced sailors than in more challenging regions; nonetheless, sailing competence and engine familiarity remain prerequisites.

Skippered charters pair you with a professional captain managing sail trim optimisation, navigation, and engine operations. This arrangement liberates guests to focus on swimming, exploring, and relaxing whilst retaining the satisfaction of sailing engagement. Captains often involve interested guests in sail handling—hoisting/furling sails, trimming sheets, steering—enabling participatory learning without operational responsibility. FAQ

Is a motorsailer faster than a sailing yacht?

Not substantially. Motorsailers under sail achieve 7–9 knots in typical Aegean winds, comparable to sailing yachts. However, motorsailers' motor-sailing capability ensures consistent progress during light winds: a sailing yacht becalmed in morning calm stalls until afternoon wind develops; a motorsailer motor-sails steadily at 8 knots. Over a week, this translates to more actual distance covered and greater scheduling flexibility. Speed isn't the primary advantage; reliability and steady progress are.

What is the fuel consumption of a motorsailer?

Motorsailers under pure sail consume zero fuel—a key advantage. Under motor or motor-sailing, fuel burn depends on engine size and speed: typical consumption ranges €0.80–1.20 per nautical mile. A week-long charter assuming 40% motor use (160 nautical miles motoring) costs roughly €130–190 in fuel—considerably less than equivalent pure-motor yacht charters.

Can I sail a motorsailer without experience?

Bareboat charters require recognised sailing qualifications (RYA Competent Crew or equivalent). However, skippered motorsailer charters suit those without sailing experience; the professional captain handles all sailing responsibilities. Many first-time sailors find skippered motorsailer charters ideal introductions: you experience authentic sailing engagement, learn from the captain, and develop foundational sailing knowledge whilst maintaining comfort and safety. can arrange teaching-focused skippered charters for those seeking sailing education.

Is a motorsailer comfortable for families?

Absolutely. Motorsailers combine sailing experience with contemporary comfort: spacious cabins, modern facilities, responsive crew, and flexible itineraries suit families excellently. Daily swimming stops, tender adventures, and gentle cruising appeal to children. Skippered charters eliminate sailing responsibility, freeing adults to supervise children and enjoy exploration. Many families specifically choose motorsailers for the balance of sailing authenticity and practical comfort.

What is the difference between a motorsailer and a gulet?

Gulets are traditional wooden motorsailers emphasising Blue Cruise heritage, all-inclusive crew service, and cultural immersion. Motorsailers in the charter fleet are contemporary vessels prioritising guest autonomy (bareboat options), sailing performance, and modern comfort. Gulets are crewed-only; motorsailers offer bareboat and skippered alternatives. Gulets emphasise slow cruising and cultural engagement; motorsailers balance sailing with exploration flexibility. Both are exceptional; choice depends on whether you prioritise tradition or contemporary sailing engagement.

Can I participate in sailing a skippered motorsailer?

Absolutely. Many captains enthusiastically involve guests in sail handling—raising/lowering sails, trimming sheets, steering techniques.
