Catamaran Charter in North East Aegean, Greece

The North East Aegean offers some of Greece's most unspoiled and culturally rich sailing territory. Catamarans are the ideal platform for exploring this diverse region, which spans from Lesvos and Chios in the south to Samos and Limnos in the north. With their stability, spacious cabins, and shallow draft, catamarans let you anchor off Molyvos castle, navigate between the mastic villages of Chios, or reach Pythagoreion's UNESCO harbour without compromise.

Why Choose a Catamaran for North East Aegean?

Catamarans excel in the North East Aegean's challenging conditions. The Meltemi wind can blow 15–25 knots from July through August, and a catamaran's wide beam provides superior stability in these swells. You'll spend less energy fighting the helm and more time enjoying the wine, ouzo, and layback culture that define Lesvos and Chios.

The shallow draft—typically 1.2–1.5 metres—lets you access anchorages inaccessible to monohulls. Off Ikaria, you can tuck into quiet bays surrounded by pine forests. Around the medieval streets of Mesta in Chios, local fishermen know the best sandy spots. And unlike a bareboat motorboat, a catamaran gives you the option to sail and conserve fuel on longer passages.

Cabin comfort matters when you're spending 30–50 nautical miles between islands. Catamaran saloons are wide and well-lit. Master cabins often have en-suite facilities across their full beam. You won't feel cramped even with four or six crew.

Top Routes from Mytilene and Pythagoreion

Most North East Aegean catamaran charters depart from Mytilene (Lesvos) or Pythagoreion (Samos). A classic week loops Lesvos, Chios, and Psara, visiting Molyvos's Genovese castle, the petrified forest near Sigri, and the mastic towns. Longer charters can reach Limnos (with its flamingo lagoon at Alyki) or venture toward the Turkish coast near Samos.

The island spacing—30–50 nautical miles between major stops—demands a boat that handles open-water passages cleanly. Catamarans do this better than smaller vessels, and you arrive refreshed rather than battered.

Technical Advantages for This Region

The North East Aegean's light holding and occasional strong gusts favour catamarans' dual-keel design and power. When the Meltemi picks up in July, a catamaran's power plants (typically twin 40–50 hp diesels) provide the reserve thrust to motor against the wind without exhausting the crew. In June or September, when conditions soften, you sail efficiently and enjoy the fuel economy of a wider, flatter hull.

Catamarans also anchor more securely. Two anchors deploy from the bows, and the platform's weight keeps you planted even as wind swings through the night. Around Myrina on Limnos, where the holding is sandy and inconsistent, this redundancy is reassuring.

Catamaran Charter Pricing

A 40-foot catamaran in the North East Aegean typically costs 1,800–2,500 EUR per week during peak season (July–August). Mid-season (June, early September) rates drop to 1,400–1,900 EUR. These rates reflect the catamaran's spaciousness, modern navigation systems, and operational complexity.

Best Season and Base Ports

June through September is ideal. June offers gentler winds, wild flowers on Samos, and fewer crowds at Molyvos. July–August brings steady Meltemi but busier anchorages. September sees lighter winds and cheaper rates.

Mytilene (Lesvos) is the region's largest base, with charter fleets, provisioning, and repair facilities. Pythagoreion (Samos) offers a smaller, more intimate option with access to UNESCO sights and Muscat wine bars. Both have excellent local knowledge and support networks for visiting yachts.

Sample Itinerary: Lesvos to Samos (8 Days)

Day 1: Depart Mytilene. Sail northwest to Molyvos (12 nm), anchor below the castle.

Day 2: Motor to Sigri (20 nm), explore the petrified forest hinterland.

Day 3: Reach Chios Mesta (35 nm), an intact medieval mastic village. Anchor and walk the maze-like streets.

Day 4: Day sail to Pyrgi (8 nm), another mastic village with black-and-white sgraffito stonework. Visit local tavernas.

Day 5: Passage south to Psara (30 nm), a quiet island with sandy bays and olive groves.

Day 6: Sail to Samos, stop at Kokkari for lunch, then motor to Pythagoreion (25 nm).

Day 7: Explore Pythagoreion: UNESCO tunnel, Roman harbour, wine tasting.

Day 8: Short hop to Marathounta or return to Pythagoreion. Final evening ashore before departure.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I sail a catamaran solo or with a small crew?

Yes. Modern catamarans have hydraulic systems, electric winches, and autopilot. A couple or three friends can manage comfortably, especially mid-season when sea states are moderate.

What happens if the Meltemi is very strong in July or August?

Strong Meltemi (20+ knots) is manageable in a catamaran. You can either motor downwind to sheltered anchorages, reef and sail, or wait it out at a safe mooring—catamarans swing less and hold better. Local skippers know the lee spots (e.g., behind Psara or in Limnos's Myrina harbour).

Is provisioning good in the North East Aegean?

Mytilene and Pythagoreion have supermarkets, fish markets, and bakeries. Smaller islands (Molyvos, Psara, Ikaria) have village shops with basics. Stock up at main ports and enjoy authentic Greek produce, local wine, mastic, and ouzo along the way.

How much experience do I need?

Bareboat requires a sailing certificate (ICC or equivalent). First-time charterers often hire a skipper for the first week to learn the waters, winds, and anchorages. Boat4You can arrange this.

Are catamarans popular in the North East Aegean?

Increasingly so. The region's longer passages and challenging winds suit catamarans well. They offer comfort and safety that appeal to families, mixed-ability crews, and those seeking stability over adrenaline.
