The facility cradles vessels in a sheltered bay, protected by hills to the north and the Čiovo peninsula to the south. Departures happen in quiet mornings without ferry traffic or commercial jostling. Close Access to the Drvenik Islands and Šolta

From Marina Agana, the Drvenik islands—Drvenik Veli and Drvenik Mali—lie just 8 nautical miles to the south. This short hop means morning departures can have crews anchored and swimming by midday. The islands feature pristine pine-backed beaches, minimal development, and excellent holding in sandy bottoms. Village restaurants on Drvenik Veli serve grilled fish and local wine, exactly the kind of intimate dinner experience that define small-boat island hopping.

Šolta, famous for its olive groves and wine production, sits a comfortable 6 nautical miles west. Moreška village and the sandy cove at Grohote offer protected anchorages. From there, larger passages to Hvar (roughly 20 nm) or Brač (15 nm) become day sails for confident crews.

Čiovo island, practically neighbors Marina Agana across a narrow strait. Its northern shore offers shallow anchorages ideal for a lazy afternoon swim, and the village of Slatine provides taverna dinners with views back to your catamaran.

The Trogir Alternative: Just Minutes Away

Trogir's UNESCO-listed old town, 10 kilometers overland, is worth an afternoon visit before or after your charter. The compact medieval streets, Renaissance palaces, and cathedral are walkable from Trogir's waterfront. Most catamaran crews visit Trogir for provisioning or a final meal before departing Marina Agana—boutique shops and restaurants line the harbourside.

The town also hosts a modern supermarket and chandlery chain, useful for stocking your catamaran galley with Croatian wines, cured ham, and fresh produce. Regular bus service (30 minutes) or a €15 taxi ride connects Marina Agana to Trogir's center.

Seasons and Wind Patterns

Marina Agana benefits from the same Adriatic wind patterns as broader Split, but gains shelter from local geography. The mistral (northwesterly) arrives in spring and autumn, typically gusting 15–25 knots—ideal for sailing. Summer brings lighter, thermal breezes that test catamaran engines but reward patience with glassy evenings. The Jugo (southeasterly) arrives occasionally, bringing warm air and larger swells; most crews find it manageable for the island protection available nearby.

Spring (April–May) remains the quietest season: water at 17–19°C, fewer vessels, and calm mornings. Summer (June–August) brings warm days and social anchorages but peaks in mid-July to mid-August. Autumn (September–October) combines warm water (21–23°C) with reliable sailing breeze and moderate crowds. Winter sailing is possible but brings sporadic conditions.

Provisioning and Local Food Culture

The village shop near Marina Agana stocks basic groceries. For richer provisioning—fresh fish, local cheeses, and Croatian wines—a short trip to Trogir is worthwhile. The Open Market in Trogir operates daily and supplies the catch of the morning, perfect for dinner aboard your catamaran.

Dalmatian coastal cuisine centers on fish: grilled sea bream, squid, and John Dory appear nightly in harbourside restaurants. Pašticada (slow-braised beef in prune and wine sauce) defines meat meals ashore. Local wines—particularly Babić, a bold red from nearby vineyards—pair naturally with Mediterranean fare. Marina Agana village taverna serves simple, honest food: pasta, fish, and local wine at modest prices.

What's the advantage of departing Marina Agana versus Split Marina?

Marina Agana offers quieter mornings, no city traffic, personalized service, and a more authentic village atmosphere. Split Marina is busier and more central; Agana suits crews seeking a slower pace and intimate village experience. Both offer excellent sailing grounds once you depart.
