Best Time to Charter a Catamaran

May, June, and September offer optimal conditions: light to moderate winds, warm seas (20–24°C), and calm anchorages. July and August bring stronger Meltemi winds but shorter days and more intense sun. Spring and autumn are ideal for families preferring gentler sailing and fewer crowds.

Winter (November–March) sees occasional strong winds and cooler temperatures, but experienced sailors value this season for solitude and dramatic seascapes. Boat4You advises first-time charters to aim for May–June or September for the best balance of weather, visibility, and amenities.

Catamaran Stability & Family Appeal

Twin-hulled catamarans are the gold standard for families with young children and guests prone to seasickness. The wide beam distributes weight evenly across two hulls, minimizing rolling and pitching compared to monohull sailing yachts. Parents appreciate the reduced motion when cooking, dining, or resting below decks.

Deck layouts on catamarans prioritize safety. Wide walkways, substantial railings, and flat sterns create secure environments for children and elderly guests. Tender launches from calm transom platforms, and water access is straightforward and supervised. Boat4You verifies all safety equipment and briefing protocols on every chartered catamaran.

Catamaran Charter FAQs

What is the typical catamaran size at Alimos Marina?

Catamarans at Alimos range from 36 to 45 feet (11–14 metres), accommodating 6–10 guests across 3–4 cabins. Larger crewed options (50+ feet) are available for luxury charters with professional skippers and crew.

Can I bareboat charter a catamaran if I'm inexperienced?

Most catamarans at Alimos require proof of sailing competence (RYA Day Skipper or equivalent). First-time sailors can take onsite courses or hire a professional skipper. coordinates all training and certification needs.

What are typical catamaran charter costs?

Weekly rates range from €1,500–€4,000 depending on season, boat size, and inclusions. Fuel, mooring, and skipper (if hired) are usually additional.

Is a catamaran suitable for rough seas?

Yes. Twin hulls distribute weight and motion better than monohulls, reducing seasickness. The Saronic Gulf is generally calm and protected; rare rough conditions occur in midsummer Meltemi or winter storms.

What's included in a catamaran charter?

Standard inclusions: mooring at Alimos, kitchen (galley) equipment, life jackets, safety gear, navigation charts, and welcome briefing. Most do not include food, fuel, or skipper unless specified. Check your listing for exact inclusions.

How many crew members do catamarans carry?

Bareboat catamarans carry no permanent crew—you operate the vessel with your party. Skippered charters include a professional skipper (and sometimes a deckhand). Crewed luxury catamarans (50+ feet) add chefs, stewards, and diving masters for full-service hospitality.

Are catamarans comfortable for multi-week charters?

Yes. Spacious cabins, generous galley facilities, and excellent stabilization make catamarans ideal for extended cruises. Two-week or longer charters allow deeper exploration of the Cyclades and allow you to truly settle in and enjoy the Greek islands at leisure.

Arrival day includes comprehensive briefing: navigation systems tutorial, engine and electrical familiarization, mooring techniques, safety equipment locations and protocols, galley orientation, water and fuel management, communication procedures (VHF radio, marina contact), and emergency response. Vessel-Type Differences in Saronic Cruising

Catamarans excel in Saronic conditions: shallow-draft designs access protected anchorages inaccessible to deep-draft monohulls. Twin hulls minimize rolling in the Saronic's sometimes-choppy open-water transitions. Spacious decks and saloons suit families wanting relaxation over sailing athleticism. Power catamarans add speed—covering 150+ nm daily vs. sailing catamarans' wind-dependent pacing. Bareboat sailing yachts demand sailing competence but deliver traditional seacraft engagement.
