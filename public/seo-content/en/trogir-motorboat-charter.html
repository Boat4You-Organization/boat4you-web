Motorboat Charter from Trogir

Trogir is the ideal launching point for motorboat day cruises and short island hops. Unlike the longer passages required for sailing yachts, a motorboat from here can cover Šolta (5 nautical miles), Brač (15 nm), or the Blue Lagoon at Drvenik Veli (8 nm) in a single easy day. The motorboats available from ACI Marina Trogir and Marina Baotić in nearby Seget range from open bowriders (20–25 feet, ideal for sunbathers and swimmers) to cabin cruisers (28–35 feet, with shade, a galley, and a head). Many require no sailing license — just a basic boating competence certificate, which many countries don't even mandate. This opens the Adriatic to couples, families with younger children, and groups who want speed and flexibility without the technical demands of sail.

A motorboat trades fuel consumption for convenience. You're not at the mercy of the Maestral wind; you leave when you want, arrive on your schedule, and can explore multiple islands in a single afternoon. From Trogir, you can breakfast in the medieval town, motor out to Drvenik's turquoise waters by 10 am, anchor and swim for two hours, visit Brač's Bol beach by 2 pm, and be back in Trogir's harbor sipping wine by sunset. For families with children aged 5–14, or for groups prioritizing comfort over sailing tradition, a motorboat makes the Adriatic accessible in ways that sailing or larger mega-yachts cannot.

Why a Motorboat Suits Trogir and Its Surrounding Islands

The distances from Trogir are short, and motorboats excel at short hops. Šolta's Maslinica is 5 nautical miles away — 30 minutes of cruising. Brač's Bol is 15 nautical miles — 1.5 hours with a leisurely pace, 50 minutes if you're in a hurry. Drvenik Veli and its Blue Lagoon sit 8 nautical miles southwest — less than an hour. These passages are long enough to feel like a proper journey but short enough that even non-boating guests don't get fatigued. Motorboats also offer practical advantages for families: you can anchor in shallower bays where sailboats risk grounding, make quick detours to explore a beach that catches your eye, and return to the marina at a predictable time (important if you have a restaurant reservation or an evening flight to catch).

Most Trogir motorboat charters do not require a sailing license — only a local competence certificate or, in some cases, proof that one of your group has spent time at the helm of a power boat. Check with your rental company; most will accept evidence of prior powerboat experience, a one-day certification course (available locally for €150–250), or simply witness a 15-minute orientation by the marina staff. Skippers are also available for hire if you'd prefer not to handle the helm yourself, though many guests find driving a motorboat more approachable than sailing.

Best Motorboat Routes from Trogir (Half-Days and Full Days)

The Blue Lagoon and Drvenik Veli (4–6 hours)

The quickest exotic destination from Trogir is Drvenik Veli, lying just 8 nautical miles south. The Blue Lagoon — a shallow turquoise pool framed by white sand and limestone cliffs — is the iconic stop. Depending on your motorboat's cruising speed (typically 18–22 knots), you'll arrive in 30–40 minutes. The lagoon anchor holds well in sand; once you're set, the afternoon is yours. The water temperature peaks at 24–25°C by August. Swimming, snorkeling with a mask in the shallows (small fish dart around), and a long lunch ashore at one of the island's two konobas fills a half-day comfortably. Several motorboat operators offer four-hour morning cruises (Trogir, 8 am departure, return by 1 pm) or full-day versions that include a second stop at Šolta or Brač on the return leg.

Šolta and Maslinica (4–5 hours)

Maslinica, on Šolta's western coast, is a postcard-perfect Dalmatian village: a small harbor, a medieval fortress perched on the ridge above, and narrow streets climbing steeply uphill. The motorboat ride from Trogir takes 25–35 minutes at moderate speed. Anchor in the harbor (it can be crowded in July–August, but the holding is reliable), dinghy ashore, and explore the village on foot. The fortress offers views across the Adriatic; it's a 20-minute walk from the waterfront. Lunch at a harborside konoba — try gregada (the fisherman's stew), pašticada, or grilled dentica (sea bream). The return journey offers an alternative route along Šolta's quieter southern coast, with options to stop at Nečujam (another anchorage) or Grohote (island interior, reached by dinghy and uphill walk to the village).

Brač: Bol and Zlatni Rat Beach (5–7 hours)

Bol, on Brač's southern coast, sits 15 nautical miles from Trogir — 1.5 hours at a relaxed cruising speed. The approach is marked by Zlatni Rat, a long shingle spit that shifts with the prevailing wind, creating a distinctive triangular profile visible from the water. The beach is backed by a tree-lined strip of restaurants and bars; the water is shallow and ideal for families. Motorboat charters typically arrive by mid-morning, anchor in the bay, and spend 3–4 hours swimming, sunbathing, and lunching on the beach before cruising back. Bol's beach restaurants serve grilled fish, squid, and seafood platters; quality varies, but the setting is unbeatable. The return journey can include a detour to the quieter Pučišća harbor on Brač's northwestern coast, famous for its stone quarries and fishing traditions. Pučišća's anchor is less crowded than Bol's; the village is smaller and more authentic.

Combination Day: Drvenik Veli and Brač (Full day, 8 hours)

Many motorboat operators offer itineraries that link multiple stops. A typical full day: depart Trogir 8 am, Blue Lagoon at Drvenik Veli by 8:45 am (swim and breakfast until 11 am), motor to Bol, Brač by noon (lunch and 2–3 hours of beach time until 3:30 pm), and return to Trogir by 5:30 pm. This requires a larger motorboat (28+ feet) with a shaded cabin and galley, and a skipper who understands pacing. The day feels full but not rushed; you get the iconic photos at both stops without the exhaustion of a whistle-stop tour.

Fleet Types and What to Expect

Open bowriders (18–25 feet)

These are fast (25–30 knots), sporty, and ideal for small groups (4–6 people) who enjoy sun exposure and don't mind wind noise at cruising speed. They typically have minimal shade, a small head (toilet), and no galley. Fuel consumption is moderate (12–15 liters per hour at cruising speed). Good for short morning blasts to Drvenik or Šolta; less comfortable for full-day exposure in high summer.

Cabin cruisers (25–32 feet)

These are the workhorse of Trogir motorboat charters. They cruise at 12–16 knots, carry 6–8 people comfortably, and feature a shaded cabin, a galley with a stove and sink, a head with a shower, and often an aft sundeck. Fuel consumption is higher (18–25 liters per hour), but the comfort factor is substantial — especially valuable on full-day trips or when traveling with children.

Larger cabin cruisers (32–40 feet)

Trogir also has access to larger power cruisers, sometimes called motor yachts. These range from day-charter vessels that sleep four (and include a proper kitchen and two bathrooms) to multi-day capable boats that can reach Vis, the Pakleni Islands, or even the Kornati with ease. They're expensive per day (typically €800–1,500 for a 35–40 footer), but ideal for groups of 10–12 or for those prioritizing luxury and space.

Practical Matters: Licensing, Fuel, and Seasons

Licensing and boating experience

Motorboat rental in Croatia is less regulated than sailing yacht charter. Many rental companies accept customers with a basic powerboat endorsement (IWC or equivalent), a one-day local certification course (available at the marina for €150–250), or simply demonstrated boating experience. Some operators require the skipper to be present; others allow experienced operators to charter unsupervised. Boat4You's charter guidelines explain licensing requirements in detail. If you lack credentials, hiring a skipper (€80–150 per day) provides both legal compliance and peace of mind, especially if you're unfamiliar with the Adriatic's navigation marks and harbor entries.

Fuel costs and consumption

Motorboat fuel (diesel) costs roughly €1.40–1.60 per liter in Croatian marinas. A typical cabin cruiser burns 18–25 liters per hour at cruising speed. A half-day trip to Drvenik (3 engine hours) costs €80–120 in fuel. A full-day Brač run (5–6 engine hours) costs €130–200. Fuel is typically included in the charter cost or invoiced separately; confirm with the rental operator before departing.

Season and weather windows

Motorboats are less weather-dependent than sailboats, but not immune. The Maestral (15–18 knots NW wind by afternoon) creates manageable chop for a cabin cruiser but can make open bowriders uncomfortable. Bora (strong northeast winds, 20–40 knots) should trigger cancellation; it arrives suddenly and can create hazardous sea states in exposed waters. Spring (May) and early summer (June) offer stable conditions and longer daylight. July and August are busiest and often booked solid two weeks in advance. September and early October are excellent — warm water, less crowded anchorages, and cooler air temperatures. November through April requires patience; the Maestral is weaker, skies are grayer, and winter Bora events are more common.

Pricing for Motorboat Charter from Trogir

Half-day motorboat rentals (4–5 hours) for a cabin cruiser cost €400–700, depending on the boat size and season. Full-day charters (8–10 hours) run €700–1,200. Open bowriders are cheaper — €250–450 for half-day, €400–750 for a full day. These prices typically include fuel, insurance, and a basic safety briefing; skipper fees are separate (€80–150 per day if requested). Sample Motorboat Itinerary: A Full Day from Trogir

8:00 am: Depart Trogir marina after a brief safety orientation. Cruise south with the morning light painting the Čiovo coastline gold. Temperatures are cool; enjoy the open air on deck.

8:45 am: Arrive at Drvenik Veli Blue Lagoon. Anchor in shallow sand. Swim, snorkel, and relax. The water is cool (22°C in June, 25°C in August) but refreshing.

11:00 am: Weigh anchor, motor east toward Brač. The sea flattens as the morning Maestral hasn't built yet. Bring a light breakfast on board — local pastries and coffee from Trogir's bakeries.

12:30 pm: Arrive at Bol, Brač. Anchor in the bay near Zlatni Rat beach. The beach is quieter at noon; afternoon crowds haven't arrived. Lunch at a beachfront konoba — try brodet (fish stew) or grilled dentica with a glass of Pošip white wine.

3:00 pm: Swimming and final beach time. The Maestral is building; you'll feel its force when you motor back.

4:30 pm: Depart Bol for Trogir. The return leg takes 1.5–2 hours into a light chop. Relax in the cabin, read, or take the helm if you're comfortable doing so.

6:00–6:30 pm: Arrive back at Trogir marina. The sun is setting, casting the UNESCO town in orange light. Return the boat, collect your deposit, and head to dinner in the Old Town — you've earned it.

Getting to Trogir and Onward

Split airport (Kastela, 20 km south of Trogir) is the primary gateway. From the airport, a taxi or rental car reaches Trogir's marina in 20–30 minutes. Motorboat operators at ACI Marina Trogir and Marina Baotić can arrange your boat briefing, fuel top-up, and any last-minute provisioning. No sailing experience is necessary — just a willingness to turn a helm and follow the waterfront.

Local Flavor: Dalmatian Food and Wine

Gregada is the dish most associated with Trogir's waters — a fish stew made from mixed catch, white wine, garlic, and tomato. It's humble, authentic, and available at nearly every konoba. Brodet (similar to gregada but often made with red wine and specific fish like scorpion fish and monkfish) is slightly more refined. Pašticada is the meat dish — beef slow-cooked in a rich tomato sauce with prunes and served with gnocchi. It's comfort food, best eaten on a quiet evening anchored off an island, watching the sun drop toward the hills.

Plavac Mali (deep red, full-bodied) is the local wine that pairs with these heavier dishes. Pošip (crisp white, from nearby Korčula) is the seafood pairing. Babić (from the Šibenik area) is a darker, earthier red that works with grilled fish. Buy a bottle at Trogir's supermarket and drink it chilled (slightly cooler than room temperature) over dinner on the aft deck.

FAQ

Do I need a boating license to rent a motorboat from Trogir?

Most motorboat rental companies in Trogir accept customers with basic boating experience or a one-day local certification (€150–250). A skipper can be hired (€80–150 per day) if you prefer not to handle the helm. Boat4You's how-we-work section details licensing pathways.

What is the best motorboat route for a family with young children?

Drvenik Veli's Blue Lagoon is ideal for families. The water is shallow and clear, the bay is protected, and the journey takes only 45 minutes. A full day combining Drvenik and Bol, Brač works well for older children (8+) who can spend time swimming and on beaches. Avoid open bowriders for small children; a cabin cruiser offers shade and comfort.

How much fuel does a motorboat use?

A typical cabin cruiser burns 18–25 liters per hour at cruising speed. A half-day trip (3–4 hours) consumes 55–100 liters; a full day (8 hours) uses 140–200 liters. Fuel costs range from €80–200 per day depending on the boat and speed.

Can I charter a motorboat with a skipper from Trogir?

Yes. Skipper rates are €80–150 per day. The skipper will navigate, pilot the boat, and advise on anchoring and weather. This is ideal if you're uncomfortable with navigation or prefer to relax and focus on family time rather than boat handling.

What is the best time to charter a motorboat from Trogir?

June and September offer stable weather, warm water (22–24°C), and fewer crowds than July–August. July and August are hottest and busiest; book at least two weeks in advance. May and October are cooler but still viable. Avoid November–April unless you're experienced in winter Adriatic conditions.

Can a motorboat reach Vis or the Blue Cave from Trogir in one day?

Vis sits 40 nautical miles away — 3–4 hours of cruising one way. A motorboat can reach it, but the day is long and fuel consumption is high (€250–350 for fuel alone). The Blue Cave near Biševo is even farther. These destinations are better suited to multi-day charter; for a single day from Trogir, stick with Brač, Šolta, and Drvenik.
