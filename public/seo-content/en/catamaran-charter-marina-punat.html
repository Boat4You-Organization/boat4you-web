Why a Catamaran from Marina Punat

Twin-hulled stability eliminates the heeling that unsettles passengers on monohulls, which makes catamarans the default choice for families, mixed-experience groups, and anyone prone to seasickness. Shallow draft (typically 1.2–1.5 metres) opens up anchorages closed to keeled yachts. Deck space, shade, and stable anchor holding in afternoon blows is the main reason charterers pick this platform for Croatia's cruising grounds.

Typical catamaran charters from Marina Punat run 38 to 48 feet, with four to six cabins and equally numerous heads, priced at roughly 2,800 to 7,500 euro per week depending on size, age and season. Bareboat charter suits experienced skippers holding a valid licence and VHF certificate. For groups without credentials, a professional skipper runs around 180–220 euro per day plus meals. The berthing configuration at Marina Punat handles catamarans without issue — allow enough turning room in the basin on busy Saturday turnarounds.

Technical considerations

Catamarans at Marina Punat handle the region's conditions well when properly prepared. Check reefing points, engine hours, and safety gear during the check-in briefing — marinas in Croatia run careful handover procedures and the briefing is the right moment to raise questions.

Anchoring and berthing

Anchor tackle on charter catamarans is generally sized adequately for Croatia's typical bay depths (6–15 metres). Med-mooring stern-to is standard in marinas; the briefing at Marina Punat covers the local conventions.

Provisioning and logistics

Marina Punat sits within reasonable range of supermarkets and fresh markets. Plan a provisioning run for the first evening rather than day zero — the fleet turnaround day is not the moment to load the galley.

Pricing and what to expect

Sample 7-day itinerary from Marina Punat

Day 1 — Departure and first bay

Embark after noon once the cabin preparation is complete, stock provisions, and motor to a nearby sheltered bay for the first night. Swim, rig checks, and a quiet dinner aboard set the tone.

Day 2 to Day 3 — Short hops along the coast

Cover 15–25 nautical miles per day between protected harbours and anchorages. Lunch stops at lunch-only anchorages, evenings in towns with konoba dining and a morning bakery run.

Day 4 — Longer crossing

Pick a day with favourable forecast for the longest passage of the week. Target 25–40 nautical miles of sailing or motoring depending on wind.

Day 5 to 6 — Return legs

Work back toward base with cultural stops — old town walk, a local winery, and an anchorage-only overnight if weather allows.

Day 7 — Return to base

Early morning passage back, fuel top-up, a quick galley clean, and formal disembarkation by late morning.

Local food and culture

expect peka (slow-cooked lamb or octopus under an iron bell), gregada (Adriatic fish stew), local Pošip and Plavac Mali wines, and konobas (family taverns) on nearly every islanded harbour. Meals aboard tend to work well for breakfast and lunch; evenings ashore deliver the local character — ask marina staff for current recommendations rather than relying on online ratings alone.
