Why a Catamaran from ACI Marina Vodice

Twin-hulled stability eliminates the heeling that unsettles passengers on monohulls, which makes catamarans the default choice for families, mixed-experience groups, and anyone prone to seasickness. Shallow draft (typically 1.2–1.5 metres) opens up anchorages closed to keeled yachts. Deck space, shade, and stable anchor holding in afternoon blows is the main reason charterers pick this platform for Croatia's cruising grounds.

Typical catamaran charters from ACI Marina Vodice run 38 to 48 feet, with four to six cabins and equally numerous heads, priced at roughly 2,800 to 7,500 euro per week depending on size, age and season. Bareboat charter suits experienced skippers holding a valid licence and VHF certificate. For groups without credentials, a professional skipper runs around 180–220 euro per day plus meals. The berthing configuration at ACI Marina Vodice handles catamarans without issue — allow enough turning room in the basin on busy Saturday turnarounds.

Technical considerations

Catamarans at ACI Marina Vodice handle the region's conditions well when properly prepared. Check reefing points, engine hours, and safety gear during the check-in briefing — marinas in Croatia run careful handover procedures and the briefing is the right moment to raise questions.

Anchoring and berthing

Anchor tackle on charter catamarans is generally sized adequately for Croatia's typical bay depths (6–15 metres). Med-mooring stern-to is standard in marinas; the briefing at ACI Marina Vodice covers the local conventions.

Provisioning and logistics

ACI Marina Vodice sits within reasonable range of supermarkets and fresh markets. Plan a provisioning run for the first evening rather than day zero — the fleet turnaround day is not the moment to load the galley.

Pricing and what to expect

Sample 7-day itinerary from ACI Marina Vodice

Day 1 — Departure and the Kornati archipelago

Embark after midday, provision, and sail or motor the first short leg to the Kornati archipelago. Evening dinner aboard or ashore at a family-run restaurant.

Day 2 — Skradin

Morning departure, mid-day anchor stop for swimming, and evening mooring at Skradin. Explore the old town on foot.

Day 3 — Primošten

A longer passage today — roughly 20–30 nautical miles depending on wind and conditions. Arrive mid-afternoon, drop anchor in a protected bay, and enjoy an early swim.

Day 4 — Murter

Short hop followed by a long lunch at anchor. Dinner at a local taverna if the harbour offers good options.

Day 5 — Return leg

Begin the return voyage with a lunch stop at a favourite anchorage. Aim for a marina or well-protected bay within a short distance of base.

Day 6 — Final exploration

Use the final full day for a highlight the group missed — a wine tour ashore, a hike to a viewpoint, or a long swim stop.

Day 7 — Return to base

Short morning passage, fuel top-up, clean-up, and formal disembarkation before noon.

Local food and culture

expect peka (slow-cooked lamb or octopus under an iron bell), gregada (Adriatic fish stew), local Pošip and Plavac Mali wines, and konobas (family taverns) on nearly every islanded harbour. Meals aboard tend to work well for breakfast and lunch; evenings ashore deliver the local character — ask marina staff for current recommendations rather than relying on online ratings alone.
