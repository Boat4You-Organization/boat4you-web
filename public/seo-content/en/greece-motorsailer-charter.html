Greece Motorsailer Charter: Traditional Sailing with Modern Reliability

A Greece motorsailer charter combines the romance of authentic sailing with the practicality of engine backup, making it ideal for exploring the Aegean without the constraints of wind-dependent voyages. Motorsailers—whether traditional wooden caiques or contemporary designs—offer a unique way to experience Greek waters, particularly the Dodecanese and Saronic Gulf, where calm seas and challenging thermal winds are common. These versatile vessels typically range from 15 to 30 meters and are often crewed, allowing you to focus on the journey rather than navigation. Whether planning a family holiday, corporate gathering, or celebration at sea, a Greece motorsailer charter delivers the sailing heritage Greece is famous for alongside reliable modern engine power.

Why Motorsailers Suit Greece Waters

Greece's Mediterranean climate and geography make motorsailers particularly well-suited to Aegean sailing. The notorious Meltemi winds—strong northerlies that dominate summer months—can challenge traditional sailing boats, but a motorsailer handles these conditions with flexibility. When the Meltemi blows favourably, you can sail and enjoy the authentic sailing experience. When winds are unfavourable or absent during calm summer days, the engine ensures steady progress between islands without frustration.

The Aegean archipelago's character demands this dual approach. Island-to-island distances are manageable—typically 4 to 8 hours between major destinations—and the ability to motor through calms means you maximise time exploring rather than drifting. Greece's traditional wooden motorsailers, evolved from the historic caique and trehantiri designs, embody centuries of Greek maritime craftsmanship. Modern hulls combine this heritage aesthetic with reliable diesel engines, comfortable cabins, and safety systems that contemporary sailors expect.

Crewed motorsailers remove the navigation burden entirely. Your captain knows the best anchorages, understands local wind patterns, and can adapt the itinerary to conditions. The crew often includes a cook preparing Greek-style meals using fresh fish and local ingredients—a culinary experience integral to any Greece motorsailer charter. This arrangement suits groups and celebrations particularly well, as guests can relax, socialise, and enjoy the voyage without watchkeeping duties.

Top Routes for Greece Motorsailer Charter

The Dodecanese circuit remains the most popular Greece motorsailer charter destination. Starting from Rhodes or Symi, vessels explore islands including Tilos, Nisyros, Kos, Patmos, and Leros. The route offers dramatic volcanic landscapes, excellent anchorages, authentic villages, and manageable daily passages. Winds favour eastbound passage in early summer, allowing pure sailing days, while motorpower handles the westbound return or mid-summer conditions.

The Saronic Gulf, accessible from Athens, provides an alternative Greece motorsailer charter experience. Close to the capital yet genuinely remote, this route encompasses Aegina, Poros, Hydra, and Spetses. Shorter passages suit families and those preferring frequent stops over long ocean stretches. The Saronic combines ancient history—proximity to mainland sites—with island culture and excellent provisioning in larger ports.

The Northern Sporades, based from Volos, offer a quieter Greece motorsailer charter option. Islands like Skiathos, Skopelos, and Alonissos feature pine forests meeting turquoise waters and less crowded anchorages than southern routes. This area suits those seeking tranquility and stronger sailing conditions. Meltemi winds are more consistent, favouring extended sailing passages.

The Ionian islands west of mainland Greece represent a distinct alternative, with dramatic scenery, deeper green landscapes, and fewer competing vessels. A Greece motorsailer charter here feels less touristy and offers genuine exploration of less-travelled waters. Passages are slightly longer and conditions more variable, making reliable motorpower genuinely valuable.

Technical Advantages of Modern Motorsailers

Motorsailers represent a pragmatic evolution of traditional sailing design. Unlike pure motor yachts, their efficient hull forms and sail plans mean they sail genuinely when conditions permit, consuming fuel economically when motoring. This hybrid approach reduces running costs compared to equivalent motor vessels while maintaining sailing's intangible satisfaction.

The traditional wooden motorsailers Greece is famous for showcase authentic Greek shipbuilding heritage. These vessels, often hand-built using time-honoured techniques, feature varnished timber interiors, substantial wooden masts, and genuine sailing character. Modern systems—electronics, engines, generators—operate within designs perfected over centuries. The result is a boat that feels historic while performing safely and comfortably.

Practical advantages matter on a Greece motorsailer charter. Diesel engines provide range and reliability; most can operate 500+ nautical miles between refuelling. Modern navigation systems eliminate guesswork in piloting the Aegean's complex waters. Stabiliser systems—fins or flopper-stoppers—reduce rolling at anchor or during motoring, crucial when navigating smaller anchorages. Composite structures in newer builds offer lighter weight, better fuel economy, and lower maintenance than traditional timber.

Accommodation on motorsailers typically exceeds expectations. Cabins are well-ventilated and surprisingly spacious. Many feature ensuite facilities, air-conditioning, and proper galleys. Deck space encourages socialising—a critical factor for group charters and celebrations. The traditional designs particularly offer character; sleeping in a wooden-hulled caique-style boat carries historical weight that modern fibreglass cannot replicate.

Pricing and Rates for Greece Motorsailer Charter

Greece motorsailer charter costs vary by vessel size, age, season, and whether crewed. Uncrewed motorsailers, typically 10 to 15 metres, start around €1,200 to €1,800 per week in shoulder seasons, rising to €2,000+ in peak July-August. These suit experienced sailors comfortable with navigation and provisioning.

Crewed charters command higher rates but eliminate many variables. A traditional wooden motorsailer, 20 to 25 metres, with crew and catering, typically costs €4,000 to €8,000 per week depending on season and specifications. Premium vessels or high season (July-August) push towards €10,000+ weekly. These rates distribute across groups—a 10-person party pays €400 to €1,000 per person weekly, often cheaper than land-based holidays for equivalent comfort and meals.

A Greece motorsailer charter remains exceptional value when you factor inclusions. Crewed rates typically encompass skipper, crew salaries, fuel, provisioning, meals, and insurance. Uncrewed rates include insurance, mooring fees, and provisioning packages. Compare total Greece motorsailer charter costs against hotel, restaurant meals, and activities ashore; the all-inclusive nature of a boat charter often proves competitive, particularly for groups.

Booking flexibility affects pricing. Last-minute Greece motorsailer charter bookings may attract discounts on uncrewed vessels or repositioning charters. Shoulder season (May-June, September-October) offers the best value-to-experience ratio—excellent weather without peak-season premiums. Early or late summer bookings reduce rates 20-30% compared to July-August extremes.

Best Time to Visit and Charter Bases

May through October remains ideal for a Greece motorsailer charter. May and June offer warm days, calm seas for beginners, and cooperative winds for sailing. Meltemi winds intensify July-August, providing thrilling sailing but occasional frustration for motor passage. September-October deliver stable conditions, fewer tourists, and the most pleasant sailing across broader wind ranges.

April and November remain viable, particularly for experienced sailors. Spring brings unpredictable weather and occasional cool nights. Autumn offers excellent sailing but occasional storms. Winter (November-March) suits only the committed; cold water, strong winds, and unreliable sunshine deter most, though a Greece motorsailer charter then costs half summer rates and attracts knowledgeable sailors seeking solitude.

Rhodes hosts the largest Greece motorsailer charter fleet. The city offers superb facilities, international airport access, and excellent provisioning. Rhodes suits longer Dodecanese explorations and appeals to international groups. Bases there range from large professional operations to intimate family charters.

Athens and Volos provide mainland departure points. Athens bases—Piraeus and Lavrion—suit those combining sailing with city visits or taking the Saronic route. Volos, in Thessaly, specializes in Northern Sporades charters and attracts those seeking less touristic sailing. Smaller bases like Symi or Nydri (Ionian) offer intimate alternatives but fewer vessel options and require pre-booking.

Geographic choice matters for experience. The Dodecanese suit those prioritising sailing conditions and island character. The Saronic appeals to first-time sailors and families given proximity to Athens and frequent stops. The Ionian attracts those seeking dramatic scenery and cultural exploration. Your base determines route possibilities—moving a Greece motorsailer charter between regions requires significant sailing time.

Sample 7-Day Greece Motorsailer Charter Itinerary

Day One: Rhodes to Symi. Depart Rhodes harbour mid-morning; the passage takes three hours via motorpower. Symi's narrow harbour captivates visitors with pastel-painted houses cascading to the water. Evening in the village square with fresh grilled fish.

Day Two: Symi to Tilos. Morning sail under Meltemi conditions; afternoon arrival at Livadia harbour, Tilos. Explore the village, swim in turquoise waters from the boat, and enjoy dinner anchored offshore with views across the volcanic plateau.

Day Three: Tilos to Nisyros. Full sailing day if Meltemi favours—typically 6-7 hours. Nisyros's crater dominates; moon-like volcanic landscapes contrast with the green villages. Mandraki's tiny harbour anchors beneath steep hills. Walk the crater at sunset or take a swim between passages.

Day Four: Nisyros to Kos. Short motor passage or sail; Kos offers larger facilities, excellent restaurants, and historical sites. A Greece motorsailer charter day in Kos combines swimming, provisioning, and land exploration. Evening return to the boat for crew-prepared dinner.

Day Five: Kos to Patmos. Classic longer passage (8-9 hours) combining sail and motor. Patmos, spiritual heart of the Dodecanese, features the Monastery of Saint John and quiet anchorages. Explore monasteries or simply relax; your Greece motorsailer charter crew adjusts pace to preference.

Day Six: Patmos to Leros. Short morning sail; afternoon in Alinda bay, Leros. Quiet and green, Leros offers respite from more-touristed islands. Swimming, local taverna meals, and evening socialising on deck characterise the day.

Day Seven: Leros to Rhodes (return). All-day passage using both sail and motor; typically eight hours. Relax, reflect on the voyage, and prepare for Rhodes arrival. Evening in the harbour with final group dinner celebrating the week.

Actual itineraries vary with wind, crew preference, and passenger interest. Your Greece motorsailer charter crew builds flexibility into planning; a calm day might mean extended time ashore on one island rather than pushing toward the next. This adaptability distinguishes crewed charters from other holidays.

Frequently Asked Questions

How far in advance should I book?

For peak season (July and August), reservations 4–6 months ahead secure the best fleet selection and pricing. Shoulder months (May, June, September) typically allow booking 6–8 weeks out.

What does the charter price include?

Base pricing usually covers the vessel, insurance, and marina fees at the home base. Fuel, provisioning, skipper service, end-cleaning, and tourist tax are generally added separately. Check the specific listing on Boat4You for a full breakdown.

Do I need a sailing licence?

For bareboat charter, a valid skipper licence plus a VHF certificate are typically required, and rules vary by country. If you don't hold one, hiring a professional skipper is straightforward — pricing runs roughly 150–220 euro per day plus meals.
