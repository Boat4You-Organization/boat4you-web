The Art of Gulet Cruising

Gulets represent the ultimate expression of unhurried Mediterranean leisure. These traditional Turkish wooden yachts—called "gulets" in Bodrum and the Turkish Riviera, adopted across the Aegean—combine classical design, spacious open-air living, and a crewed service model that makes charter almost effortless. Boat4You offers 5 gulets in the Cyclades, a small but distinguished fleet catering to those who prize tranquility over speed, and cuisine over rope work.

A gulet cruise is not a sailing expedition; it's a floating sanctuary. Days revolve around anchoring in quiet bays, swimming, snorkeling, and the cook's menu—often featuring fresh catch, island vegetables, and wines from local producers. The crew handles all navigation and seamanship; you focus purely on enjoying the Aegean's beauty and your companions' company.

Design & Onboard Comfort

Gulets typically measure 15–25 meters, with distinctive high bows and open sterndecks that function as social gathering spaces. Unlike compact sailing yachts, gulets offer expansive deck area for sunbathing, shaded awnings for afternoon rests, and generous cabin spacing. Interiors balance traditional wood aesthetics with modern plumbing, air conditioning, and entertainment systems.

Sleeping arrangements vary: some gulets offer 4–5 large cabins with ensuite heads; others provide 6–8 cabins sleeping up to 16. Boat4You curates vessels matching your group size and privacy preferences. The open stern deck becomes your living room—the heart of gulet culture where crews prepare meze spreads, sunsets are toasted, and conversations drift late into Mediterranean nights.

Island Routes & Leisure Pace

Gulet itineraries rarely follow strict schedules. A typical week might progress: depart Mykonos, motor southeast toward Paros (enjoying swimming stops en route), anchor in Naousa for dinner ashore, move to Naxos for morning provisioning, drift to Amorgos for quiet seclusion, and return via Ios and Santorini. Daily distances are modest—10–20 nautical miles—allowing leisurely 10 am departures and 4 pm anchoring.

This pace suits families with children, couples seeking romance, and groups prioritizing relaxation over distance. Unlike sailing yachts racing the meltemi, gulets accept the wind as backdrop—not adversary—and adjust plans accordingly without complaint.

Why Gulets Are Rare in Greece

Gulets dominate the Turkish coast (Bodrum, Marmaris, Gocek) where traditional wooden-boat culture thrives. The Cyclades, being smaller and more Mediterranean-centric, favor monohull sailing yachts and motor catamarans. Boat4You's 5-gulet collection represents a carefully curated niche—owners who've invested in maintaining these vessels in the Greek islands specifically for those seeking something different from typical charters.

This scarcity is an advantage. Gulet charters avoid the congestion of larger bareboat fleets; crew expertise is deep; and the experience remains exclusive. Each gulet cruise feels bespoke, not templated.

Culinary Excellence & Wine Culture

Gulet cooks are often trained in Mediterranean cuisine, familiar with Greek island traditions, and proud of their menus. Expect traditional meze (saganaki cheese, calamari, octopus), pasta dishes featuring local herbs, and nightly catches prepared simply—grilled with lemon and olive oil. Breakfast includes fresh fruit from island markets, local cheeses, and yogurt.

Wine pairings highlight Greek regions: crisp whites from Paros and Santorini, rosés from Limnos, and modest reds from larger producers. Coffee rituals follow Greek tradition—strong espresso in the morning, lighter versions as the day cools. Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between a gulet and a sailing yacht?

Gulets prioritize comfort and service; sailing yachts prioritize sailing experience. Gulets come crewed; sailing yachts are bareboat (or skippered for extra cost). Gulets emphasize deck space and dining; sailing yachts emphasize performance and seamanship. Both are wonderful—the choice depends on your priorities.

Is a gulet suitable for non-sailors?

Absolutely—that's the point! Gulets are designed for people who want to float the Mediterranean without navigating, maintaining, or understanding rigging. You can spend a week aboard a gulet without touching a rope.

Can we customize the itinerary?

Yes, within reason. The crew knows the islands and weather patterns; they'll suggest routes and adjust daily based on conditions and your interests. Want to spend two days in Naxos instead of one? It's done. Interested in a specific underwater site? The crew will navigate there.

What about seasickness on a gulet?

Gulets are wide, stable platforms. Unlike narrow sailing monohulls that heel and pitch, gulets roll gently. Most people find them remarkably comfortable in moderate seas. The crew can also adjust speed and routing to minimize motion if weather is rough.

Are meals included in the charter price?

Typically, gulet prices include crew and basic provisions; confirm whether meals are all-inclusive or if you contribute a provisioning budget. High-end venues ashore (Michelin-starred restaurants in Mykonos) are your cost; simple tavernas and onboard dinners are usually included.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.

What deposit is required at booking?

Most operators request 30–50% at contract signing, with the balance due 4–6 weeks before departure. Exact terms appear on your quotation before you commit.

Can I move my charter to a different week if I need to?

Usually yes, subject to fleet availability and any operator handling fee. Request the change as early as possible; mid-season swaps are harder than off-season.

Is there a standard security deposit for damages?

Yes. Most operators block a refundable deposit on your credit card at check-in, typically €2,000–€5,000 depending on vessel. A damage-waiver upgrade reduces or eliminates this hold — ask your consultant for the specific options on your chosen boat.
