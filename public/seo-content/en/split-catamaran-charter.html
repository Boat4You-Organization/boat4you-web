Catamaran Charter in Split: Spacious Luxury Sailing Through Central Dalmatia

A catamaran transforms a sailing holiday in Split into an experience of unparalleled comfort and stability. With their twin hulls, wide deck space, and shallow draft, catamarans are ideally suited to the central Dalmatian coast, offering both the thrill of sailing and the ease of handling that modern charterers expect. Whether you're navigating the sapphire waters between Brač and Hvar or anchoring off the Pakleni islands, a catamaran gives you the platform to explore Split's sailing grounds with confidence and style.

Why Catamarans Excel in Split Waters

The shallow bays and rocky islands around Split demand a hull design that combines shallow draft with stability in variable winds. Catamarans deliver both. Their twin hulls provide minimal heel even in the afternoon Maestral winds typical of summer months, meaning passengers stay comfortable and the saloon remains navigable. The 1.0–1.5 metre draft of most charter catamarans allows you to approach Pakleni island anchorages and squeeze into Maslinica bay on Šolta, where deeper keeled yachts struggle. The wide deckspace—often spanning 8–12 metres beam—gives you genuine room to move, sunbathe, and enjoy the Mediterranean without feeling cramped.

Catamarans in this region typically carry 4 to 12 crew plus guests, meaning they're nimble in tight anchorages yet roomy enough for families or couples seeking space. The modern catamaran hull also cuts through Bura wind chop with less drama than a monohull, a reassurance when winter squalls or unexpected northeast blows sweep down the coast.

Top Routes for Catamarans from Split

The classic Split-based catamaran route runs south to Brač, then west across open water to Hvar. Bol on Brač—famous for Zlatni Rat beach—is a natural first stop, giving you a protected anchorage and a chance to explore tavernas and the town's Herculean wine. From there, a 12-nautical-mile sail brings you to Hvar town, where narrow stone streets, lavender fields inland, and the island's Plavac Mali wine make for a memorable afternoon and evening.

A second option pushes further: Split to Šolta (Maslinica bay) in one easy afternoon sail, then across to Drvenik Veli for remote anchorages, and onward to Vis island. This route suits catamarans particularly well because the bays between islands offer excellent holding and minimal swell, while the speed and shallow draft mean you can explore lesser-known anchorages like Komiža on Vis, home to traditional fishing heritage and locally caught fish prepared as gregada.

The third option, favoured by longer charters, loops north to Trogir (a UNESCO-listed medieval town just 30 kilometres from Split marina), exploring the Kaštela bays and the Trogir waterfront before returning to Split. This route is suitable for families and those wanting fewer open-water hours.

Technical Advantages of Catamarans in Central Dalmatia

Catamarans' beam—often 50% wider than a monohull of the same length—creates a large, level saloon and cockpit. In the warm, sunny summers of central Dalmatia, that extra space translates to genuine comfort. The dual-engine configuration typical of charter catamarans delivers redundancy; if one engine fails, you have a backup. Engine power also means that in the variable winds of July and August, you can make progress reliably without worrying about frustrating calms.

The shallow draft is a genuine advantage. While monohull charter yachts often draw 1.8–2.0 metres, catamarans draw 1.0–1.5 metres, opening access to Maslinica bay on Šolta, shallow anchorages around Brač, and the crystal-clear bays of the Pakleni islands where deeper-drafted vessels cannot venture.

Solar panels and larger water tanks are standard on modern catamarans, meaning longer periods between marina visits—valuable when exploring the remote anchorages around Vis and Drvenik, where provisioning options are limited.

Pricing and What to Expect in Split

A 40–45 foot catamaran typically costs between €3,500 and €6,500 per week in high season (July–August), depending on age, layout, and onboard systems. Low season (May, June, September, October) sees rates of €2,500–€4,500. Bareboat charters—where you captain yourself—are typically 20–30% cheaper than crewed options, though skippers with Mediterranean experience are strongly advised for first-time charterers in Croatian waters.

Fuel, mooring fees, and provisioning are additional costs. A typical week's budget for two people on a mid-range catamaran, excluding the charter fee, runs €600–€1,000. ACI Marina Split and Marina Trogir charge €50–€80 per night for a 40-foot catamaran. Refuelling usually costs €300–€500 for a week of casual sailing.

Best Time to Sail and Marina Departure Points

May through October offers sailing, but July and August bring thermal consistency and reliable afternoon Maestral winds—perfect for catamarans, which love steady breezes. June and September are less crowded and often have more varied wind, challenging but rewarding. April and November are possible but bring occasional Bura squalls and shorter daylight hours.

Most Split-based catamaran charters depart from ACI Marina Split, the largest facility, offering fuel, repair facilities, and provisioning. Marina Kaštela (12 kilometres west) is quieter and closer to the Kaštela bays if your itinerary favours that region. Marina Trogir, 30 kilometres northeast, is ideal if you plan to explore northward towards the Croatian coast's medieval towns.

Sample 7-Day Catamaran Itinerary from Split

Day 1: Depart ACI Marina Split at noon. Motor-sail to Brač, Bol, arriving by sunset. Dinner on the waterfront; overnight mooring or anchorage.

Day 2: Morning swim at Zlatni Rat beach. Afternoon sail to Hvar town, exploring the Pakleni islands' anchorages en route. Overnight in Hvar town harbour.

Day 3: Full day exploring Hvar: lavender farms, Plavac Mali wine tasting, the Franciscan monastery. Evening at a harborside taverna.

Day 4: Early sail to Vis. Stop at Stiniva cove (dramatic, narrow sea cave entrance) on route. Overnight in Komiža or Vis town. Sample gregada (traditional fish stew) at a family-run restaurant.

Day 5: Explore Vis's quieter anchorages or sail to nearby Biševo island (Blue Cave if conditions permit). Return to Vis town. Overnight.

Day 6: Sail northeast to Šolta, Maslinica bay, or detour to Drvenik islands for remote anchorages. Overnight at a protected bay.

Day 7: Return to ACI Marina Split via Brač, arriving by late afternoon. Provisioning and final evening exploring the Riva promenade.

Frequently Asked Questions About Split Catamarans

Do catamarans require more crew than monohulls?

Not necessarily. Charter catamarans are often easier to handle than monohulls of similar size, thanks to twin engines and responsive steering. Most 40-foot catamarans operate comfortably with two experienced sailors.

Are catamarans slower than sailing yachts?

Catamarans typically sail 1–2 knots slower than fine-hulled monohulls in light-to-moderate winds, but they maintain speed and comfort in stronger wind and chop. For a relaxing charter where speed is secondary to comfort, this trade-off is worthwhile.

Can I explore the Pakleni islands from a catamaran?

Absolutely. The Pakleni's shallow bays (often 2–5 metres) are perfect for catamarans. The shallow draft and wide beam let you anchor where monohulls cannot, offering greater flexibility and solitude.

What's the fuel cost for a week of sailing?

Typically €300–€500, depending on engine hours. Motor-sailing (using engine plus sail) is more economical than pure motoring and remains reliable in the variable winds of Split waters.

Is a catamaran suitable for beginners?

Yes. Catamarans are more stable and forgiving than monohulls, with minimal heel and large decks. A professional skipper for your first charter is recommended if you lack Mediterranean experience.
