Full-Service Marina Infrastructure

Les Marines de Cogolin delivers comprehensive maritime services: fuel dock (diesel and petrol, 8 AM–6 PM daily), fresh-water provision points, shore-based 220V and 380V electricity, waste disposal (hazardous materials included), and professional reception. Wi-Fi connectivity enables weather monitoring, passage planning, and email communication. A working chandlery stocks spares, safety equipment, and navigation aids.

Onshore facilities include laundry, shower blocks, and secure parking (€12–15 daily). A nearby medical clinic and pharmacy provide healthcare access. The marina's quiet setting—fewer mega-yachts, less nightlife spectacle than Port Grimaud—creates a more restful base camp for crews prioritizing sleep over social activity.

Provisioning & Authentic Provençal Access

Cogolin's village center—750 meters from Les Marines—is where authentic Provençal provisioning happens. Tuesday and Thursday markets overflow with seasonal produce: spring asparagus, summer tomatoes and courgettes, autumn grapes and figs. Local farmers, not wholesalers, stock these markets. A daily bakery delivers warm bread at 7 AM. The fishmonger stocks fresh Mediterranean catches—rouget, daurade, loup de mer, poulpe (squid).

Cheese shops offer regional varieties: creamy chèvre (goat cheese), aged Mimolette, and Comté from nearby regions. Cured-meat shops supply saucisson, pâté, and jambon (ham). Wine merchants stock Côtes de Provence rosé (light, dry, perfect for ship-board aperitifs) and regional reds (Gigondas, Vacqueyras). The entire provisioning experience—walking village streets, selecting fresh produce directly from vendors, conversing in French or broken English—is the authentic Provence experience that cruising seeks.

Sailing Routes from Les Marines de Cogolin

Gulf of Saint-Tropez Classic: Northwesterly passage (8 nm, 60 minutes) reaches Pampelonne Beach and Saint-Tropez harbor. Anchor for lunch, explore the village, enjoy waterfront dining, and return by sunset. This quintessential Riviera day-sail suits all wind conditions and crew experience.

Îles d'Hyères Multi-Day Circuit: Southeasterly course (12–15 nm, 90–120 minutes) accesses Porquerolles, Port-Cros, and Îles du Levant. This 3–5 day loop offers dramatic anchorages, snorkeling, and National Park protection. Porquerolles' Plage Notre-Dame and Port-Cros' Plage de la Palud represent the Mediterranean's most beautiful landfalls.

Sainte-Maxime & Extended Riviera Run: Easterly passage (8 nm) reaches Sainte-Maxime. Continue further to quieter coastal bays, the Lérins Islands (40 nm, full-day passage), or overnight anchorages tucked away from mainstream tourism.

Catamaran Specifications & Handling

Modern cruising catamarans (36–42 feet) staged at Les Marines typically feature: twin Yanmar engines (75–110 hp each), roller-furling jibs and mainsails, hydraulic systems, modern chartplotters and navigation suites. Twin-hull design provides shallow draft (1.0–1.3 meters), allowing sandy-bay anchoring unavailable to monohulls. The spacious saloon and wide deck accommodate groups of 6–10 comfortably without cramping.

Fuel consumption: 8–12 gallons combined per hour at cruise. A week-long charter mixing sailing and motoring typically uses 40–60 gallons. Water-maker units enable extended independence; refrigeration systems keep provisions fresh for multi-day passages between resupply points.

Best Sailing Seasons

May & June: Water warming to 18–20°C, northwesterly winds 10–14 knots, extended daylight. Moderate crowds; ideal for learning and exploring quiet anchorages away from peak-season congestion.

July & August: Peak season. Reliable afternoon thermals (12–16 knots), temperatures 28–30°C. Anchorages crowded; book 8–10 weeks ahead. Social atmosphere; evening gatherings at popular anchors.

September & October: Water warm (22–24°C), winds steady at 10–12 knots, golden light, dramatically fewer tourists. Les Marines' quieter setting amplifies the benefit—fewer transient crews, more peaceful anchorages, reduced marina congestion.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between Les Marines de Cogolin and Port Grimaud?

Port Grimaud is a purpose-built residential marina village with Venetian architecture, curated design, and integrated social scene. Les Marines is a traditional working marina adjacent to Cogolin village. Port Grimaud offers atmosphere; Les Marines offers authenticity and value. Both provide identical sailing access and professional services.

Is Les Marines de Cogolin quieter than Port Grimaud?

Yes. Les Marines caters primarily to active cruisers and charter crews, not residential waterfront living. You'll encounter fewer permanent residents, less evening spectacle, and more peaceful anchorages. The tradeoff: fewer waterfront restaurants and nightlife within the marina itself (though Cogolin village offers modest options).

What certifications do I need for a bareboat catamaran charter?

Bareboat charters require an International Certificate of Competence (ICC) or equivalent national sailing license. Day-skipper level or higher is standard. Boat4You reviews credentials; crewed options are available if certification is lacking.

Can I visit Saint-Tropez directly from Les Marines?

Yes. Saint-Tropez lies 8 nm northwesterly (60 minutes under sail or power). You can anchor off Pampelonne Beach or moor at town quays. Day-trip or overnight options suit your schedule. The contrast between quiet Les Marines base and vibrant Saint-Tropez makes this an ideal blend of tranquility and stimulation.

How is provisioning at Cogolin compared to Port Grimaud?

Cogolin's village markets and shops are more authentic but require walking 800 meters from the marina (10 minutes). Port Grimaud's integrated marina village places everything steps away. Cogolin offers better food quality and lower prices; Port Grimaud offers convenience. Many crews prefer Cogolin's market experience despite the short walk.

Is Les Marines cheaper than Port Grimaud?

Slightly. Charter rates are typically €100–200 per week lower for equivalent vessels. Marina mooring fees are €0.50 per meter less. Fuel and provisioning are the same. The main savings derive from lower operator costs and charter prices—modest but meaningful for multi-week charters.
