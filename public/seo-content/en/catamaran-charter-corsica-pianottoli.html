Pianottoli-Caldarello: A Quiet Harbour

Pianottoli-Caldarello sits 20 kilometres west of Bonifacio on Corsica's south-west coast, a working fishing village where local boats outnumber yachts and authenticity trumps resort polish. The small port-de-plaisance holds roughly 100 berths; mooring often means tying alongside local fishermen or anchoring in the sheltered bay outside the basin. Depths are adequate (2.5–3.5 metres); fuel and water are available, though at smaller scale than Bonifacio or Ajaccio.

The village itself is unspoiled: a harbourside restaurant or two, a small chandlery, and local bars where crews mingle with fishermen over pastis. Supermarkets are absent; provisioning happens at the morning fish market or a tiny épicerie stocking basics. This simplicity appeals to crews valuing authenticity over convenience, and seeking access to Corsica's natural heritage without modern marina trappings.

Access to Lavezzi Islands Without the Crowds

Pianottoli lies 8–12 nautical miles west of the Lavezzi islands—close enough for a 2–3-hour morning passage yet far enough to avoid Bonifacio's dense anchoring. Crews departing Pianottoli often reach Lavezzi by mid-morning, anchor in uncrowded lagoons, and enjoy hours of solitude while day-trip boats from Bonifacio depart by 5 p.m. The result: pristine turquoise water, white-sand beaches, and a sense of discovery that crowded seasons deny.

Catamarans from our 5-boat fleet are perfectly suited to Lavezzi's shallow bays. Their 1.0–1.3-metre draft accesses 2–3-metre lagoons where monohulls cannot venture. A second anchor prevents swing in evening winds. Snorkelling and swimming are extraordinary; dusky grouper breeding grounds and Mediterranean seagrass meadows are thriving. Dinghy exploration of islet edges reveals granite caves, ancient mooring stones, and undisturbed Mediterranean ecology.

Protected Bays and Quieter Cruising

South and west of Pianottoli lie protected bays and unnamed anchorages—smaller, shallower, and far less visited than Bonifacio–Lavezzi routes. Many are not marked on standard charts; local knowledge (available through Boat4You's skipper network) unlocks anchorages where crews feel utterly removed from tourism. Cove names—Cala Gonone, Cala di Trana—appear in specialist sailing guides; anchoring in turquoise water, framed by red granite cliffs, often means sharing the bay with just one or two other boats, not a dozen.

This quieter profile appeals to experienced crews seeking adventure without infrastructure, and families desiring peaceful anchorages where children can swim safely in calm, clear water. Pianottoli-based charters typically favour 5–8 nautical-mile daysails, building flexibility for exploration and rest days in favourite anchorages.

Corsica's South Coast from Pianottoli

North-east of Pianottoli, the coast climbs towards Porto-Vecchio (30 nautical miles) and Solenzara (45 nm), the Côte Rocheuse defined by boulder-strewn coves and granite headlands. West of Pianottoli, the coast turns wild—named anchorages fade, navigation relies on pilot books and GPS, and crews venture into genuine exploration. The stretch towards Propriano (25 nm north-west) is dramatic: soaring red cliffs, hidden lagoons, and the sensation of stepping back into Mediterranean sailing's golden age.

Many week-long charters from Pianottoli form a triangle: Pianottoli–Lavezzi–quiet protected bays–return, covering 50–80 nautical miles and spending 5–6 nights anchored ashore. Crews often provision once (before departure) and live off stored provisions, returning to Pianottoli for fresh supplies and laundry mid-week.

Strait of Bonifacio Passage

While Pianottoli is 15–20 nautical miles west of the Strait of Bonifacio crossing point, the passage to Sardinia is straightforward. Crews planning Corsica–Sardinia multi-week charters typically navigate to Bonifacio (4–5 hours from Pianottoli), anchor overnight, and cross the Strait the following morning. Alternatively, a direct south-south-east passage from Pianottoli to Maddalena (25 nautical miles) bypasses Bonifacio entirely, reaching Sardinia's national park in 5–6 hours under motor or sail.

Catamarans handle this extended passage comfortably; the open-water crossing is longer than from Bonifacio, but conditions are typically manageable. Boat4You provides detailed routing and optional skipper support for crews new to multi-country passages.

Provisioning and Galley Culture

Pianottoli's morning fish market (typically 7–9 a.m.) is the village's pulse. Local fishermen sell catch; small vendors offer fresh vegetables, bread, and Corsican specialities. The épicerie stocks staples. Unlike Bonifacio's sprawling supermarkets, Pianottoli's provisioning requires morning discipline—crews wake early, shop, and return to their catamaran by 10 a.m. to prepare lunch and plan the day's sail.

This rhythm encourages self-catering. Many crews provision heavily upon arrival, stock galleys with Corsican wine, charcuterie (lonzu, coppa, prisuttu), and fresh vegetables, then cook dinner aboard for 4–5 nights before returning to Pianottoli for resupply. The result is intimate crew bonding, cost savings, and the satisfaction of dining on Mediterranean fare in anchorages where land is mountains and sea is turquoise.

Seasonal Patterns and Weather

May through October is Pianottoli's season. June and September offer ideal conditions: stable maestrale from the north-west, warm water (22–26°C), and calm anchorages. Late May sees occasional mistral spillover; August brings heat and occasional libeccio gusts (15–20 knots), adding exhilaration to afternoon sails. The south coast is slightly more exposed than Ajaccio's west-coast anchorages, but Pianottoli's protected harbour ensures safe anchorage in most conditions.

Winter sailing is possible but less predictable. Spring (May) and autumn (October) reward early planners with exceptional conditions, fewer visiting yachts, and rates 20–30% below peak season.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Pianottoli harder to reach than Bonifacio?

Driving takes 10–15 minutes longer from Corsica's main airports (Ajaccio or Bastia). If you're flying into Ajaccio, Bonifacio is actually closer (90 km vs. 110 km). However, ferry services serve some nearby towns. advises on ground transport; many crews rent a car and enjoy a scenic drive exploring Corsica's interior before reaching Pianottoli.

What happens if weather changes during my charter?

Pianottoli's protected harbour is safe in most conditions. If strong wind develops, sheltered anchorages abound within 5–10 nautical miles (protected bays east and west of the village). Catamarans' stability handles the modest swell that can develop on exposed coasts.

Can I reach Lavezzi and back to Pianottoli in a daysail?

Yes. Lavezzi lies 8–12 nautical miles east. Departure at 8 a.m. reaches the islands by 10–11 a.m., allowing 5–6 hours of swimming and exploration, then a 2–3-hour return passage by 5–6 p.m. Many crews anchor overnight at Lavezzi, extending their exploration and returning to Pianottoli the next day.

Is Pianottoli suitable for families with young children?

Excellent. Protected bays, calm water, and quiet anchorages create ideal swimming and snorkelling conditions. The village is peaceful, uncrowded, and friendly. Provisions are simple but adequate. Crews can base themselves in Pianottoli for 5–7 nights, exploring nearby anchorages by daysail and returning daily to the secure harbour. Families often appreciate the slower pace and absence of mega-yacht crowds.

What charts and pilot books do I need?

Admiralty charts for the south-west Corsica coast (particularly the Lavezzi and Strait regions) and French Pilot or specialist sailing guides (e.g., Sailing Directions for the Mediterranean by Ionian Publishing) are essential. Local skippers carry hand-marked charts identifying lesser-known coves.
