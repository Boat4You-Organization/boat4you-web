Motorsailer Charter in Split: The Best of Sailing and Motor Power

A motorsailer combines the appeal of traditional sailing with the practical reliability of diesel propulsion, offering a genuine hybrid experience. These vessels—typically 40–60 feet—are designed and rigged to sail well, but carry powerful engines allowing sustained passages in calm conditions or adverse wind. A motorsailer charter in Split allows you to engage with sailing when winds favour it, motor confidently when they don't, and explore Central Dalmatia without the frustration of calms or the pressure to maintain sailing discipline. The result is remarkable freedom: sail when conditions are ideal, motor when they're not, and typically make progress towards your chosen destination.

Why Motorsailers Suit Central Dalmatia

Central Dalmatia's summer weather is partly beautiful and partly unpredictable. The Maestral winds often arrive in mid-afternoon and favour sailing from noon to sunset. Mornings can be calm, forcing early-morning decisions: do you wait for wind, or do you motor to your next destination? A motorsailer removes this dilemma. You motor comfortably during morning calms, arrive at your chosen anchorage, and then sail back if afternoon winds materialise. This flexibility is invaluable for charterers with fixed itineraries and time constraints.

Motorsailers are seaworthy and economical. A well-designed motorsailer carries significant fuel capacity, allowing extended cruising without frequent refuelling. The sailing rig provides fuel redundancy; if engine fuel depletes, you can sail. Modern motorsailers feature efficient diesel engines and optimized hull designs, achieving 8–12 knot motoring speeds on reasonable fuel consumption (20–30 litres per hour at cruise).

Motorsailer Design and Capabilities

Modern motorsailers are designed to sail efficiently while accommodating substantial engines. The rig is typically a cutter or ketch configuration (two masts), allowing sail reduction and flexibility. Hull design emphasizes stability under sail and economical motoring. Most feature moderate sail areas (compared to pure sailing yachts) and robust auxiliary engines (40–80+ horsepower), prioritizing motor reliability over extreme sailing performance.

The key distinction: a motorsailer is a sailing vessel with powerful engines, not a motor yacht with sails. This design philosophy means the sailing is genuine and rewarding, while the motors ensure you're rarely stuck. The result is a vessel that appeals to sailors who value flexibility and motorists who appreciate the option of sailing when conditions align.

Technical Advantages of Motorsailers in Central Dalmatia

Motorsailers typically draw 1.8–2.2 metres, providing good holding ground in sandy and muddy anchorages. The twin-hull designs (on some models) or fin keels (on traditional designs) offer stability and weatherliness. Most modern motorsailers carry GPS/chartplotting, autopilot, and electronic navigation systems ensuring safety in variable conditions. The engine redundancy—if you lose engine power, sails provide propulsion—is a genuine safety advantage in transition seasons.

Fuel capacity is typically generous (500–1,200 litres), allowing extended cruising. Solar panels are often standard, reducing genset runtime and noise. Modern electronics mean navigation is straightforward, and communication systems (SSB radio, satellite phone) provide connectivity without compromising the sailing experience.

May through October offers cruising, with June through September providing warmest conditions and most reliable Maestral winds. Motorsailers excel in shoulder seasons (May, early June, September, October) when variable winds make pure sailing challenging; the engine provides insurance without compromising sailing opportunity.

Frequently Asked Questions About Motorsailers

Is a motorsailer better than a pure sailing yacht for Split?

It depends on your priorities. For sailors who value flexibility and weather insurance, motorsailers excel. For pure sailing enthusiasts, dedicated sailing yachts deliver better performance. For non-sailors, motor yachts are simpler. Motorsailers suit those wanting both sailing and reliability.

How much sailing will I actually do?

Highly variable. In summer (June–August) when Maestral winds are reliable, you might sail 50–70% of passage time. In shoulder seasons, calms are more common, and motoring increases. You choose: sail when conditions suit you, motor when you prefer efficiency.

Can I sail if I'm inexperienced?

Yes. Many motorsailer charters are bareboat but hire professional skippers (€80–€150 daily). The skipper handles sailing while you learn or relax. Crewed motorsailers include a professional captain who sails as you prefer.

Is a motorsailer good for families?

Yes. The flexibility (motor for predictable timing) and sailing option (engaging when conditions suit) appeal to families. The hybrid approach reduces frustration while maintaining sailing appeal.

What's the fuel cost for a week?

Typically €300–€500, depending on the balance of sailing and motoring. Heavy motoring increases consumption; extensive sailing reduces it. Motorsailers' fuel efficiency is a genuine advantage compared to dedicated motor vessels.
