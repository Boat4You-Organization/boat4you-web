Motor Yacht Charter from Trogir

A motor yacht from Trogir transforms a week on the Adriatic into a seamless, captained experience. Unlike bareboat sailing, where you navigate and handle the boat yourself, a motor yacht arrives with a professional skipper, a chef (on the largest vessels), and crew who manage navigation, meal preparation, and logistics while you focus on enjoying the water, the islands, and the company of family or friends. Trogir, with its proximity to Split airport (15 minutes by car) and its central position between the Adriatic's best-sailed destinations, has become a hub for crewed motor yacht charters across the central and southern Adriatic.

Motor yachts range from 40-foot owner-operated vessels (with minimal crew) to 60+ foot luxury yachts with cabins for 8–12 guests, a full galley, crew quarters, and modern stabilization systems that keep you comfortable even if the Maestral picks up. The advantage over sailboats: you're not held hostage to the wind. Your skipper can depart on schedule, adjust your route based on weather or your mood, and anchor in places where sailboats would struggle. The advantage over motorboats: you have space, multiple cabins with private bathrooms, and the ability to drift at anchor for hours without engine noise, as the yacht is large enough to swing freely on a scope.

Why a Motor Yacht Suits Trogir and Extended Adriatic Routes

Motor yachts excel at longer passages and multi-day itineraries where comfort and reliability matter. From Trogir, you can reach Hvar Town (25 nm, 4–5 hours), Vis and Komiža (40 nm, 6–8 hours), the Pakleni Islands, and overnight anchorages where you wake to stillness and coffee on the flybridge. A motor yacht's independent power — not reliant on wind — means your skipper can navigate you out of Trogir harbor by 9 am on a calm morning, find afternoon anchorages even if the Maestral hasn't built, and optimize your route around swell, weather, or the restaurant reservation you've made for dinner at Hvar Town's harborfront.

The crew component is transformative. A professional skipper knows every anchorage's holding ground, the best approaches into crowded harbors, and how to position the yacht for evening light and protection from overnight wind shifts. If the yacht includes a chef (typical for boats above 50 feet), your meals evolve from self-catered dinners to restaurant-quality cuisine prepared onboard — often featuring local ingredients. Even modest crew (skipper plus one deckhand) handles anchoring, dinghy provisioning, and daily cleaning, leaving guests free to swim, read, explore villages, or simply exist on deck without logistics hanging over their heads.

Typical Routes for Motor Yachts from Trogir

The Central Adriatic Loop (5–7 days)

A classic week from Trogir covers Šolta, Brač, Hvar Town, the Pakleni Islands, and Vis Town, returning via Komiža and the quieter western islands. Day one and two explore Šolta and Brač — short passages that allow the group to settle in and the yacht to reach its rhythm. Days three through five focus on Hvar Town and the Pakleni Islands: Hvar's harbor requires early arrival (by 4 pm) to secure an anchor spot, but the harborfront's energy in the evening — locals and tourists mingling along the waterfront, tavernas spilling out onto the street — rewards the effort. The Pakleni Islands, just across the bay, offer quieter days: sandy coves at Zaglav, Vlaka, and Stipica, where your yacht's dinghy makes island-hopping effortless and your crew can prepare lunch ashore while you swim.

Days six and seven extend to Vis Town and Komiža. Vis Town's quiet harbor and terraced houses create an almost forgotten-time feeling. Nearby Komiža is a working fishing village; its harbor is smaller, more intimate. The Blue Cave at Biševo (nearby) is a famous photo opportunity, though the entrance requires a transfer by local skiff. Your crew will coordinate the logistics — you simply appear on the dinghy when ready. The return to Trogir (40 nm) takes eight hours at cruising speed and is typically done as a daytime passage, arriving by early evening.

The Extended Dalmatian Route (10–14 days)

Larger groups and longer charters often push south from Trogir into the Kornati Islands National Park and beyond. Kornati, reachable from Trogir in 15–20 nautical miles (via Vodice, the primary Kornati base), is an archipelago of 89 islands, most uninhabited. Anchoring in clear, shallow bays — Telašćica Bay on Dugi Otok, Obonjan, Mula — provides days of swimming, snorkeling, and absolute solitude. The park requires a permit (paid by the crew) and use of designated mooring buoys; the yacht never needs to move far, and your days reduce to swimming, reading, and dinner preparation. Extended charters continue south to Šibenik (home to a medieval fortress-ringed town), Primošten, and eventually Makarska and the southern Dalmatian coast.

The Hvar Town and Vis Focus (5–7 days)

Some charterers prioritize depth over breadth, spending multiple days in a single harbor. Hvar Town's nightlife, restaurants, and social scene reward lingering; spend two or three days anchored there, exploring the town on foot, dining at different harborside restaurants each evening, and day-sailing to the Pakleni Islands for afternoon swimming. Alternatively, anchor near Komiža on Vis, use it as a base for day-sails to Biševo's Blue Cave and quieter coves, and find the rhythm of a small island without the churn of constant anchorages changes. This approach suits couples and smaller groups who prefer tranquility over itinerary pressure.

Fleet Options and What to Expect

40–50 foot motor yachts

Entry-level crewed yachts, typically with two cabins (sleeping 4–6 guests) and a skipper (sometimes a skipper-chef combo). These boats are often owner-operated on charter as a secondary income source, lending them a personal, lived-in character. Fuel efficiency is reasonable; they cruise at 8–10 knots and burn 25–35 liters per hour. Pricing runs €3,000–5,000 per week.

50–60 foot motor yachts

The sweet spot for crewed charters. Typically three to four cabins, sleeping 6–8 guests comfortably. A skipper and one deckhand handle all operations; many include a chef for a premium. Stabilization systems (gyroscopes or fin stabilizers) reduce motion even in moderate chop. Cruise speed is 10–12 knots; fuel consumption is 35–50 liters per hour. Weekly pricing ranges €6,000–12,000 depending on season and whether a chef is included.

60+ foot luxury motor yachts

Full-service vessels with 4–6 cabins, sleeping 8–12, plus dedicated crew quarters. These yachts often feature a professional chef, a host/hostess, and advanced navigation and entertainment systems. The Adriatic has a limited number of these super-yachts on charter, but luxury operators in Trogir and Split can source them. Weekly pricing for a 65–75 foot boat with full crew: €15,000–30,000+. These vessels offer the highest comfort level but commit to a longer logistical planning process.

Crew and Catering

Skipper

Every crewed charter includes a professional skipper. Most have 10–20+ years of Adriatic experience and can speak English, German, and Italian fluently. They handle all navigation, harbor entries, anchoring, and safety. A good skipper is part yacht captain, part concierge — they suggest quiet anchorages, advise on weather, and know which harborside konoba serves the best pašticada.

Deckhand/Crew

Yachts above 50 feet typically include a deckhand or additional crew member who handles provisioning, dinghy management, and daily cleaning. This person can also assist the skipper with mooring, anchoring, and navigation.

Chef

Yachts above 55 feet often include a professional chef. Menus are coordinated in advance with the charterers; most chefs source local ingredients at each port, preparing breakfast, lunch, and a three-course dinner daily. The chef's presence transforms the yacht from accommodation into resort-level experience. Cost is typically €200–400 per day, added to the base charter rate.

Practical Details: Season, Licensing, and Pricing

Licensing

As a guest on a crewed motor yacht, you need no license. The skipper manages all legal and navigational requirements. If you're interested in taking the helm, your skipper can arrange this (with the skipper standing by for safety); this is a memorable bonus for experienced sailors, though not a requirement.

Season and weather

The best months for motor yacht charter from Trogir are May, June, September, and early October. The Maestral is predictable, water temperatures are warm (21–25°C), and anchorages are less crowded than in July–August. Peak season (July–August) is hottest and most expensive; book by March for those months. Spring (April–May) and fall (October) offer excellent conditions at lower prices; expect cooler air and water (19–22°C in May, 21–24°C in October). Winter (November–March) is doable but requires flexibility; Bora winds can arrive suddenly, and some smaller anchorages become uncomfortable.

Pricing and inclusions

Motor yacht charter pricing depends heavily on vessel size, crew configuration, and season. A 45-foot bare-skipper charter (skipper only, no chef) costs €3,500–6,000 per week in shoulder season, €5,000–8,000 in peak. A 55-foot yacht with skipper and deckhand runs €7,000–12,000 shoulder, €10,000–15,000 peak. Adding a chef typically adds €1,500–2,800 to the weekly cost (depending on the chef's experience and provisioning quality). Fuel is usually included up to a quota; overage fuel is invoiced at cost (€1.40–1.60 per liter). Mooring fees, park permits, and provisioning are typically invoiced separately unless a full-catering package is arranged.

Sample Itinerary: Seven Days on a Motor Yacht from Trogir

Day 1 (Monday): Depart Trogir at 10 am after crew briefing and provisioning. Cruise to Šolta (Maslinica or Stomorska Bay), arriving by early afternoon. Lunch at a harborside konoba, swim in the protected cove. Evening drinks on the aft deck as the Maestral fades.

Day 2 (Tuesday): Explore Brač — Bol's Zlatni Rat beach for swimming and lunch, or Pučišća (quieter, stone-quarrying village) for history. Return to Maslinica or Stomorska by evening. Dinner prepared by your crew or ashore at a local restaurant (crew will arrange reservations).

Day 3 (Wednesday): Motor to Hvar Town (25 nm, arriving by late morning). Secure an early anchor position near the town center. Lunch ashore, explore the Spaniola fortress, and navigate the narrow streets. Dinner at a harborfront restaurant; your crew can arrange bookings. Evening passeggiata along the waterfront (the local tradition of strolling and socializing).

Day 4 (Thursday): Day-sail to the Pakleni Islands (just across the bay). Anchor at Zaglav, Vlaka, or Stipica. Swim, snorkel in the clear water, and lunch prepared by your crew ashore or onboard. Return to Hvar Town by early evening, or stay overnight in the Pakleni for absolute solitude.

Day 5 (Friday): Motor south to Vis Town and Komiža (40 nm, an 8-hour passage or split into two days with overnight on Brač if you prefer a slower pace). Arrive Vis Town by late afternoon. Explore the harbor and narrow streets. Dinner ashore at a local fish taverna. Vis's local wines (Vugava white, Viski Plavac red) are worth trying.

Day 6 (Saturday): Explore Vis and day-sail to Biševo's Blue Cave (your crew coordinates the local skiff transfer and timing). Alternatively, stay anchored and take a long dinghy exploration of the surrounding coves and islands. Evening at anchor with dinner onboard — your chef will have sourced local fish and prepare it simply, with local Pošip wine.

Day 7 (Sunday): Motor back to Trogir (40 nm, 8 hours, typically departing early to arrive by late afternoon). Return the yacht to the marina, settle final invoices, and depart for the airport or dinner in Trogir's Old Town to debrief the week.

Getting to Trogir

Split airport (20 km south) is the primary gateway. Most motor yacht charters arrange airport transfers (typically €40–60 per person, booked in advance through Boat4You or your charter company). The transfer reaches Trogir's marina in 25–35 minutes. Your skipper will meet you at the marina, brief you on the yacht, finalize provisioning with the crew, and depart once everyone is settled (usually by 10 am).

Local Flavor: Dalmatian Food, Wine, and Island Culture

Dalmatian cuisine tells the story of the sea and the limestone hills. Gregada, the fisherman's stew, begins with whatever didn't sell at the market — mixed white fish, tomato, garlic, and white wine — simmered for hours. Brodet, more refined, uses specific fish (scorpion fish, monkfish) and red wine. Pašticada is the meat tradition — beef braised slowly with tomato, garlic, and prunes, served with gnocchi or polenta. These dishes appear at virtually every waterfront restaurant; the best versions are simple and unhurried.

Plavac Mali (deep red, full-bodied, grown on terraced Dalmatian slopes) is the signature wine. Pošip (bright white, crisp, from Korčula) is the seafood pairing. Babić (from the Šibenik hinterland) runs earthier than Plavac Mali but with similar structure — excellent with grilled fish. Your crew will source local wines at each port; some of the smaller producers' bottles rarely leave the islands. Sipping a glass of cold Pošip at sunset, anchored in a quiet cove with no engine sound, no crowds, and no schedule — that's the motor yacht experience.

FAQ

What is included in a motor yacht charter from Trogir?

A base crewed motor yacht charter includes the yacht, skipper, fuel (up to a quota), insurance, and harbor fees. Catering, chef, deckhand, and provisions are typically added as upgrades. Ask your charter company to itemize inclusions before booking.

Do I need sailing experience to charter a motor yacht?

No. Your skipper handles all navigation and boat operation. If you want to take the helm and have sailing experience, your skipper can supervise — but it's optional.

How far can a motor yacht travel from Trogir in a week?

A typical 45–55 foot motor yacht cruises at 8–12 knots and carries enough fuel for 60–80 nautical miles of daily passage. A week covers the central Adriatic (Šolta, Brač, Hvar, Vis) or a mix of central islands plus an extension south to the Kornati. Longer passages (Trogir to Makarska, 100+ nm) are possible but reduce time for anchorage-based relaxation.

Can a motor yacht accommodate a family of 10 for a week?

Yes, but you'll need a 55+ foot yacht with 4–5 cabins. Pricing will be substantial (€10,000–18,000+ per week depending on chef and season), but the experience — private vessel, all meals included, crew managing logistics — is incomparable for large group travel.

What if I prefer a quieter week with fewer anchorage changes?

Motor yachts excel at "hub-and-spoke" cruising. Anchor in one location (Hvar Town, Komiža, or a Pakleni island) and take day-sails to explore nearby destinations, returning to the same overnight anchorage. This eliminates the fatigue of nightly moves and allows deeper connection to a single area. Discuss this preference with Boat4You; they can coordinate a custom itinerary with your skipper.

What is the best time to charter a motor yacht from Trogir?

May, June, September, and early October offer the best balance of comfortable weather, warm water, and moderate pricing. July–August are warmest and busiest; book by March. April and October are cooler but still pleasant. Winter requires flexibility for weather.
