Technical Conditions: Tides, Wind, and Navigation

Tidal range: 12–15 meters (40–50 feet) near Mont Saint-Michel and Saint-Malo. Plan passages at slack water. Current streams run 2–3 knots; work them right and you fly, work them wrong and you stall. Chartplotters help, but paper charts and tide tables are not optional.

Wind: Atlantic swells funnel through the Channel. Prevailing westerlies; summer thermals weaker than inland. Gales arrive rapidly; three-day weather windows are common. June–September seas calm, but conditions remain variable.

Tidal gates govern entry to harbors. Cherbourg, Saint-Malo, and smaller ports have restricted windows. A 40-foot yacht drawing 2 meters cannot approach Granville on all tides. Plan accordingly or pay a pilot.

Navigation hazards: Roches de Saint-Malo, the Îles Chausey reef field, Les Plateins off Roscoff, and commercial traffic from Le Havre. Coasting pilotage and piloting books essential. Electronic navigation alone is dangerous here.

Best Bases and Seasons

Cherbourg: Northwest gateway. Largest marina, full facilities, ferry connections. Ideal for crossing to Jersey. Base for northbound exploration and D-Day history.

Saint-Malo: Medieval rampart port. East-facing anchorages, close to Dinard and Rance estuary. Busiest base, most atmosphere, limited swing-mooring space.

Granville: Central base between Cherbourg and Saint-Malo. Quieter, fishing village charm, gateway to Îles Chausey and Mont Saint-Michel approaches.

Season: June–September. July–August warmest and most crowded. June and September offer steadier winds and fewer tourists. October onward: gales increase, daylight shrinks, many marinas reduce services.

Sample Itinerary: Seven Days from Cherbourg

Day 1: Rig and provisions at Cherbourg. Short sail to Barfleur (granite village, strong tides). Dine on fish soup.

Day 2–3: Cross to Jersey (12 hours night sail or two-day passage via Saint-Malo). Anchor off Saint-Helier, explore Norman Isles granite architecture.

Day 4: Return passage; tidal planning critical. Arrive Saint-Malo or Dinard for rest day. Walk ramparts and the Rance estuary.

Day 5–6: South toward Granville, Îles Chausey. Anchor in protected pools, dinghy to fishing village, marvel at tidal range.

Day 7: Passage back to Cherbourg or end at Granville. Debrief over calvados and Normandy cheese.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need a skipper for Normandy/Bretagne sailing?

Strongly recommended for first charters. Tides, current streams, and narrow passages demand local knowledge. Experienced crews comfortable with UK waters and tidal pilotage can self-drive.

What are the biggest risks?

Strong tides creating standing waves and confused water. Narrow harbor entrances with commercial traffic. Unpredictable weather windows. Rock outcrops and coastal reefs. Overconfidence. Respect conditions, plan conservatively, and you'll be safe and have the best sailing of your life.

Is there good provisioning and dining?

Excellent. Cherbourg and Saint-Malo have full supermarkets and fish markets. Normandy cider, calvados, and Camembert available everywhere. Cancale for oysters, Granville for scallops, every port for moules-frites. Shore dining is outstanding and affordable.

What's the best time to go?

June and September: steadier winds, fewer tourists, manageable seas. July and August: warmest but crowded and occasionally flat-calm. Avoid October onward; gales increase and daylight shortens rapidly.

Can I sail single-handed or as a couple?

Not advisable. Tidal streams, current, and traffic require two crew minimum. Four is comfortable for seven-day passages. Six or eight for slower cruising and watchkeeping spreads fatigue.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear.
