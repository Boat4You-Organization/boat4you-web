Sailing in the Cyclades is not passive tourism; it's a craft. Classic Routes & Island Passages

The Santorini-to-Mykonos route is iconic: roughly 35 nautical miles of pure Aegean sailing, often completed in eight to ten hours with full mainsail. Many charters anchor in the calm waters of Paros or Naxos as intermediate stops, using villages like Naoussa and Agios Georgios as provisioning ports.

From Athens gateways—Alimos or Lavrion—you'll motor south 20–25 nautical miles to reach the first major islands (Kea, Andros), then settle into the classic Cyclades wind pattern. Smaller islands like Io and Milos offer quieter anchorages and exceptional seafood tavernas. Koufonisia and Amorgos, further east, reward those seeking solitude and pristine anchorages.

Wind Tactics & Meltemi Conditions

The meltemi is relentless in summer. Unlike the leisurely trades of the Caribbean, Cyclades sailing demands respect for pressure systems tracking eastward across the Mediterranean. Most days, the wind fills from the northwest by late morning, peaks in mid-afternoon, and drops towards evening—offering a reliable rhythm for daily passages.

Our fleet of monohulls are built for this: full-keeled designs with weighted stability, large canvas plans, and responsive helms. Whether you're aiming for a fast offshore passage or a lazy coastal crawl, Boat4You experienced-skipper options ensure you sail safely and efficiently through the meltemi's most intense weeks.

Marinas & Mooring Fields

Mykonos hosts modern facilities (New Port Marina, Ornos Bay) with high-season congestion but excellent provisioning. Santorini's Amoudi Bay is deep, sheltered, and iconic for sunset anchoring. Paros and Naxos offer quieter, village-friendly harbors where locals still repair nets and traditional fishing boats dock alongside modern charters.

Most anchorages are natural—sandy bays protected from the dominant northwest swell. Our Boat4You sailing fleet guides you to lesser-known moorings where few tourists venture: Antiparos, Donousa, and the Ionian-facing coast of Naxos deliver serenity and outstanding snorkeling.

Seasonality & Weather Windows

April–May brings lighter meltemi (10–15 knots) and wildflower-carpeted villages. June intensifies the wind but guarantees sunny skies. July–August is the meltemi peak: warm, dry, and challenging for inexperienced crews. September–October sees the wind ease and air temps remain pleasant; water is warmest in September–October (25°C).

Winter (November–March) sees sporadic charters, calmer winds, and fewer tourists—ideal for peaceful cruising if you tolerate cooler water and occasional squalls. Spring and autumn are ideal balance: moderate winds, mild temperatures, and manageable crowds.

Local Provisioning & Island Food

Each village has a small supermarket (peripetera), farmer markets appear twice weekly, and tavernas serve fresh catch daily. Mykonos is pricey; Naxos and Paros offer exceptional value and authentic meze. Try local cheeses (graviera, kalathaki from Milos), fresh squid from Ios, and the white wines of Paros and Santorini. Boat4You crews often buy directly from fishing boats or village suppliers, saving money and tasting authentic island life.

Frequently Asked Questions

What sailing experience do I need?

Our fleet spans first-time charter (calm-water, beginner-friendly monohulls with autopilot and easy-reefing mainsails) to advanced ocean-racing designs.

Is the meltemi dangerous?

The meltemi is strong but predictable. It builds gradually after dawn, peaks mid-afternoon, and drops by evening. Proper reefing, well-maintained rigging, and conservative passage planning make it manageable and exhilarating, not dangerous. Our sailing yachts are designed for these conditions.

Can we visit both Santorini and Mykonos in one week?

Yes—it's a classic itinerary. Santorini-Mykonos is 35 nm; add Paros and Naxos (20–25 nm each leg) for a perfect seven-day loop. Allow anchor days for swimming, provisioning, and rest.

What's included in a sailing charter?

Hull, sails, rigging, navigational gear, safety equipment, and berths are standard. Fuel, provisioning, mooring fees, and skipper costs (if hired) are typically additional. Check the detailed listing for your chosen yacht.

Are there beginner-friendly alternatives to the meltemi?

Absolutely. Charter in May or September for gentler 10–15 knot winds. Or choose a smaller island circuit (Paros, Naxos, Antiparos, Iraklia) where you can return to the same bay if weather shifts. staff will match your crew to ideal conditions and vessel type.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics. Good preparation is the quiet foundation of every relaxed charter.

Most operators request 30–50% at contract signing, with the balance due 4–6 weeks before departure. Exact terms appear on your quotation before you commit.

Can I move my charter to a different week if I need to?

Usually yes, subject to fleet availability and any operator handling fee. Request the change as early as possible; mid-season swaps are harder than off-season.

Is there a standard security deposit for damages?

Yes. Most operators block a refundable deposit on your credit card at check-in, typically €2,000–€5,000 depending on vessel. A damage-waiver upgrade reduces or eliminates this hold — ask your consultant for the specific options on your chosen boat.
