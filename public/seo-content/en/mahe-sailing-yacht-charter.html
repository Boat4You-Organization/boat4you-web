Wind Conditions and Sailing Seasons

The Seychelles enjoys two distinct wind regimes. The southeast trade winds (April–October) are a sailor's dream: consistent 12–18-knot breezes, moderate sea states, and stable high-pressure systems. These conditions allow confident passage-making with reasonable comfort. The northwest monsoon (November–March) brings lighter, more variable winds interspersed with occasional dramatic squalls—challenging for some, exhilarating for others. Many sailing enthusiasts actually prefer the shoulder months (March–April, September–October) when winds are moderate and the sailing is engaging without being brutish.

Bareboat vs. Crewed Sailing Yachts

Bareboat sailing means you captain your own vessel, with full responsibility for navigation, seamanship, and safety. Boat4You provides detailed briefings, charts, weather routing, and shore-based support. This option suits experienced sailors seeking independence and the thrill of commanding a real ocean-going yacht. Crewed charters, conversely, pair you with a professional skipper (and possibly additional crew) who handles sailing while you learn, participate at your comfort level, or simply relax and enjoy. Many first-timers and those rusty from years ashore opt for crewed experiences, which often transform into lifelong sailing passions.

Sailing Routes and Island Exploration

A week-long sailing charter from Mahé typically encompasses the inner island loop: northeast to Praslin's Baie Ste Anne, south to the twin islands of La Digue and Félicité, west to Silhouette's dramatic anchorages, and the return leg visiting Curieuse, Cousin, or other gems as winds and interest dictate. Each passage is a pure sailing event—reaching across open water, tacking through narrow channels, and arriving at anchorages earned through seamanship and wind-reading.

Mahé itself offers excellent daysailing: short hops around granite-studded bays, exploring the coast from granite outcrops near the capital to the wild northern shores near Beau Vallon. Longer charters can venture to outer islands or multi-week expeditions for truly adventurous crews.

Anchorages and Island Time

The Seychelles boasts dozens of anchorages ranging from famous tourist sites (Anse Source d'Argent on La Digue, Anse Lazio on Praslin) to hidden gems frequented only by yachts and occasional locals. Many anchorages offer perfect holding ground in sand or mud, protected by surrounding reefs or islands. Dinghies allow beach explorations, snorkeling excursions, and island hikes. Evening anchorages—surrounded by stars, dolphins visible in phosphorescent wakes, bioluminescent plankton lighting the water—are the stuff of sailing dreams.

Frequently Asked Questions

What sailing experience do I need for a bareboat charter?

We typically require evidence of competency: sailing certifications (ASA, RYA), a logbook showing blue-water experience, or references from previous bareboat charter companies. Alternatively, hire a professional skipper for your first Seychelles venture—many clients graduate to bareboat after one crewed experience.

Are sailing yachts expensive to charter compared to catamarans?

Pricing is competitive. Sailing yachts often cost less to charter than equivalent-sized catamarans, and their fuel efficiency can offset apparent price differences.

What happens in light wind or calm conditions?

The Seychelles rarely experiences true calms. During lighter wind periods, we motor-sail (engine + partial sails) to maintain speed and comfort. Modern yachts have efficient engines and carry sufficient fuel for such scenarios. Crewed charters adapt itineraries based on conditions; bareboat clients receive weather routing and support.

Can I participate in sailing without formal experience?

Yes! Crewed charters are perfect for learning-oriented clients. Our professional skippers welcome keen students, teach seamanship during passages, and can involve you as much or as little as you prefer.

What safety equipment is onboard a yacht?

All our yachts carry life jackets, life rafts, EPIRB (emergency position-indicating radio beacons), first-aid kits, fire extinguishers, and comprehensive communication systems. We maintain insurance coverage and meet international maritime safety standards.

What to Pack for a Week Aboard

Soft bags only (hard suitcases will not fit in most cabins). Layered clothing — mornings and evenings cool down, midday sun is intense. Non-marking deck shoes or quality flip-flops. Reef-safe sunscreen, polarised sunglasses, a wide-brim hat, and a waterproof phone pouch. A personal snorkel and mask if you dislike shared gear. Any prescription medication, clearly labelled, in its original packaging.

Your skipper will provide a full safety briefing before departure — life-jacket locations, EPIRB, flares, fire extinguishers, engine kill-switch, man-overboard procedure, and VHF usage. Listen carefully, ask questions, and make sure every adult aboard knows the basics.
