Catamaran Charter in Sukošan: Spacious Comfort at Croatia's Premier Marina

Sukošan, a small village nestled on the Dalmatian coast just 9 kilometres south-east of Zadar, has become one of Croatia's most strategic bases for catamaran charters. Home to D-Marin Dalmacija—a 1,200-berth superyacht and charter marina—Sukošan offers unrivalled access to the Kornati archipelago, Dugi Otok, and the crystal-clear waters of Northern Dalmatia. A catamaran from Sukošan combines the twin-hull stability and spacious deck space that families and larger groups crave with fast access to some of the Mediterranean's most unspoiled sailing grounds.

Why Catamarans Excel from Sukošan

The shallow bays and prolific islands surrounding Sukošan demand a hull design that balances draft with living space—catamarans deliver both. The 1.0–1.5 metre draft of modern charter catamarans allows you to penetrate the shallow anchorages of the Kornati National Park, approach Dugi Otok's hidden coves, and explore the narrow channels around Ugljan and Pašman islands where deeper monohulls cannot venture. The generous beam—typically 14–16 metres—provides a vast saloon and cockpit, ideal for the warm, sun-baked summers of Northern Dalmatia.

Catamarans based at D-Marin Dalmacija benefit from protected anchorage within the marina complex. The sheltered setting reduces stress during departure and arrival, while the marina's full-service provisioning, fuel dock, and maintenance facilities ensure you leave nothing to chance. Twin engines are standard, offering redundancy and sufficient power to navigate the afternoon Maestral wind (typically 12–18 knots) with confidence. Stability is paramount: minimal heel even in stronger breezes means passengers remain comfortable and galley work becomes straightforward.

Strategic Location: Gateway to the Kornati Archipelago

Sukošan's position at the entrance to Zadar Bay provides unmatched proximity to the Kornati National Park. A 15–20 nautical mile sail south-west places you amid 140 uninhabited islands, each offering pristine anchorages, exceptional snorkelling, and the sense of true remoteness. Catamarans' shallow draft opens access to Levrnaka Cove, Rava, and Pirovac's tight bays—impossibly beautiful spots where monohulls cannot anchor. The park's crystal-clear waters (visibility often exceeds 40 metres) reward underwater exploration, and catamarans' ample deck space provides ideal sunbathing and dinghy-launch platforms.

Dugi Otok, stretching 43 kilometres south of Zadar, is a natural second destination from Sukošan. The island's west coast features dramatic cliffs and remote coves; Telašćica Bay on the southern tip is a designated marine park with shallow, protected anchorage perfect for catamarans. Many charterers allocate 3–4 days to the Dugi Otok circuit alone, combining swimming, hiking inland to the Telašćica salt lake, and dining at family-run konobas in villages like Sali.

Classic 7-Day Itinerary: Sukošan to the Kornati and Back

Day 1: Collect your catamaran at D-Marin Dalmacija by noon. Provisioning stop in Sukošan village (small markets, fresh produce). Afternoon sail to Ugljan island's western shore, anchoring in a protected bay by Turanj. Evening exploring the village waterfront. Overnight Ugljan.

Day 2: Motor-sail south-west toward the Kornati entrance. Stop at Pirovac's narrow cove (excellent snorkelling, dramatic rock formations). Late afternoon arrival at Rava anchorage in the Kornati. Sunset dinner on deck overlooking uninhabited island silhouettes. Overnight Rava.

Day 3: Full-day island-hopping within Kornati National Park. Anchor at Levrnaka Cove (shallow, crystal-clear). Lunch ashore at a rustic restaurant on a neighbouring island. Afternoon snorkelling and swimming. Evening anchorage at a remote spot with no other vessels in sight. Overnight in the Kornati.

Day 4: Early sail to Dugi Otok, south along its scenic west coast. Stop at Veli Rat lighthouse (picturesque, quiet anchorage). Late afternoon passage to Telašćica Bay on Dugi Otok's southern tip—a UNESCO marine park with excellent holding and protected waters. Overnight Telašćica.

Day 5: Explore Telašćica: morning hike to the famous salt lake (Mir) overlooking the bay, afternoon swim at the beach, evening at Sali village's waterfront restaurants. Catamarans' comfort allows you to base a full day here without fatigue. Overnight Telašćica.

Day 6: Sail north along Dugi Otok's eastern shore, stopping at smaller villages and coves. Afternoon passage to Pašman island's western anchorages. Overnight in a secluded bay, dinner on deck under stars.

Day 7: Return to Sukošan and D-Marin Dalmacija via Ugljan and Zadar Bay. Arrive by mid-afternoon, allowing time for final provisioning and evening at a waterfront taverna in Sukošan village. Overnight, preparing for departure or extending your charter.

Technical Advantages of Catamarans in Northern Dalmatia

Modern charter catamarans (40–50 feet) operating from Sukošan average 1.0–1.3 metre draft, allowing access to anchorages with just 1.5–2.0 metres depth. This is transformative in the Kornati, where many pristine bays offer only shallow holding. The wide beam—often 14–16 metres—creates superior living areas: separate cabins (typically 3–4 guest cabins), large galley, and spacious saloon. Twin engines provide single-engine maneuvering capability in tight marina approaches and shallow-water navigation.

D-Marin Dalmacija's infrastructure supports catamaran operations perfectly. The marina offers fuel dock, freshwater fill, waste disposal, and repair facilities. Solar panels and large water tanks on modern fleet catamarans reduce dependency on marina resupply, ideal for extended Kornati explorations where services are minimal. Battery capacity on newer boats supports electric dinghy motors, reducing noise and fuel consumption.

Pricing and Budget Expectations

A 40–45 foot catamaran from D-Marin Dalmacija typically costs €2,800–€5,200 per week in high season (July–August), depending on age, cabin layout, and onboard systems. Shoulder season rates (May–June, September–October) drop to €2,000–€3,800. Bareboat (self-skippered) charters are 25–35% less expensive than crewed options. For those without sailing certification or Mediterranean experience, a skipper (€250–€350/day) adds professionalism and peace of mind.

Additional costs include fuel (typically €300–€500 for a relaxed week of sailing and motoring), D-Marin Dalmacija mooring fees (€50–€80/night), and provisioning (€400–€700 for two people over seven days). Advance payment of the security deposit (usually €1,500–€2,500) is standard. Boat4You provides transparent pricing upfront, eliminating surprises.

Best Seasons and Wind Conditions

May through October offers sailing, but conditions vary significantly. July and August bring warm, predictable weather and reliable afternoon Maestral winds (12–18 knots from the north-west), perfect for catamarans. These months are busiest at Kornati anchorages and most expensive. June and September offer excellent sailing—fewer crowds, stronger and more variable winds, and prices 20–30% lower. April and November are possible but bring occasional Bura squalls (north-easterly, cold) and shorter daylight, limiting exploration range.

D-Marin Dalmacija remains open year-round, supporting winter and spring sailing for those experienced in Mediterranean seasonal patterns. Water temperature ranges from 13°C in February to 27°C in August, influencing comfort levels and the appeal of daily swimming.

Local Flavor: Provisioning and Dining

Sukošan village itself is small but charming—a few waterfront konobas (traditional tavernas) serve fresh seafood and Dalmatian specialties. The village pebble beach is perfect for evening swims. For comprehensive provisioning, Zadar city (9 kilometres away) offers supermarkets, fish markets, and butchers. Many Boat4You charterers base their shopping in Zadar or Sukošan before departure, then rely on village markets and restaurants along the Kornati circuit.

The Kornati islands themselves have minimal infrastructure, but a few rustic restaurants operate seasonally on main anchorages. Dugi Otok villages like Sali offer local wine (Zinfandel-like red varieties), fresh fish prepared as gregada (traditional fish stew), and olive oil. Dining from the catamaran—provisioning fresh produce in Zadar and cooking aboard—is increasingly popular for those seeking solitude and cost control.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to reach the Kornati from Sukošan?

Typically 2–3 hours under sail or motor-sail, covering 15–20 nautical miles south-west. Fast ocean passages allow morning departures and afternoon anchorages at Kornati's best sites.

Is a catamaran suitable for families with young children?

Excellent choice. The stability, spacious cabins, generous deck space, and shallow-draft access to protected anchorages make catamarans ideal for families. Minimal heel means children move safely below deck. The slow cruising speeds typical of Kornati circuits suit young passengers perfectly.

What qualifications are required for a bareboat catamaran?

Most bareboat charters require an IYT Day Skipper or equivalent (sailing competency certification). Crewed charters require no sailing background—your professional skipper manages all navigation and manoeuvring. Boat4You offers both options and can advise which suits your group.

Can I anchor overnight at Kornati islands?

Yes, anchoring is permitted in designated park anchorages. A modest per-vessel fee (approximately €10/night) applies. Your catamaran's shallow draft and large deck provide unrivalled comfort during multi-day Kornati explorations.

What happens if winds exceed safe sailing limits?

Twin engines and stability mean catamarans motor reliably even when winds are strong (15+ knots). The Maestral wind is predictable, and D-Marin Dalmacija's shelter provides safe harbor if conditions worsen unexpectedly. Pre-departure briefings by your charter company cover weather contingencies.
